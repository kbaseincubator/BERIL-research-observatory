# Chris Mungall

Chris Mungall (ORCID 0000-0002-6601-2165) is a contributor in this compendium, credited on two reanalysis projects that mine the BERDL data lakehouse for biological signal: a study of how decades of experimental soil warming reshapes a forest microbial community, and a pangenome-scale characterization of the SNIPE antiphage defense system. This page summarizes that footprint — which projects the contributor touched and which scientific topics those projects span — so a reader can navigate from a person to the concrete claims and the underlying datasets. The two projects share a methodological style: they treat existing omics and fitness resources as a queryable substrate, layer published findings against in-lakehouse data, and report their confidence honestly, including where the data cannot support a strong conclusion.

## Overview

The contributor's work splits cleanly into two scientific domains. The first, [Harvard Forest warming](#projects), is an environmental-genomics reanalysis of a long-running soil-warming experiment: it asks how 25 years of +5 C heating has changed the functional capacity and the metabolite pools of a temperate forest soil community, separating organic-horizon from mineral-horizon soils. The second, [SNIPE defense system](#projects), is a comparative-genomics study of an antiphage defense protein, tracing it from a single domain of unknown function up to a pangenome-wide distribution and down to gene-level fitness phenotypes.

Across both, the topic footprint is broad. The warming work anchors in [Environment Biogeography](../topics/environment-biogeography.md), [Metabolic Pathways](../topics/metabolic-pathways.md), and [Microbial Ecotypes](../topics/microbial-ecotypes.md). The SNIPE work spans [Functional Dark Matter](../topics/functional-dark-matter.md) (characterizing previously uncharacterized protein domains), [Mobile Genetic Elements](../topics/mobile-genetic-elements.md), [Pangenome Architecture](../topics/pangenome-architecture.md), and [Microbiome Engineering](../topics/microbiome-engineering.md). [Gene Fitness](../topics/gene-fitness.md) connects the two, because both projects lean on transposon-mutant fitness data to ground their functional claims. The datasets underpinning this work — [Nmdc Arkin](../data/nmdc-arkin.md), [Kbase Ke Pangenome](../data/kbase-ke-pangenome.md), [Kescience Fitnessbrowser](../data/kescience-fitnessbrowser.md), and [Phagefoundry](../data/phagefoundry.md) — are linked from the project discussions below.

## Projects

### Harvard Forest warming

This project reanalyzes metagenome and metatranscriptome samples from the Barre Woods soil-warming plots at Harvard Forest, where reference soils have been held at +5 C above ambient for roughly 25 years. The headline community result reproduces the published signal for the site: after a quarter-century of warming the soil community shows a real but modest compositional shift, with Actinobacteria up and Acidobacteria down. Most of the functional response, however, is not community-wide but stratified by depth — roughly 39% of DNA-level KEGG Orthology (KO) responses are organic-only, mineral-only, or actually flip sign between the two horizons, so warming responses are best read horizon by horizon rather than as a single soil-wide effect.

On carbon cycling, the project finds partial support for the expectation that warming accelerates organic-matter degradation. Curated carbon-cycling KOs are significantly enriched among genes upregulated by heat in the DNA-organic samples (Fisher odds ratio 2.78, p = 0.042), and two specific signatures stand out: methanotrophy genes pmoA/pmoB (the particulate methane monooxygenase) are upregulated in heated soils across both horizons, and the glyoxylate cycle (with its key enzyme isocitrate lyase) is upregulated in heated mineral soil. The metabolomics side is consistent with faster turnover: heated mineral soils carry significantly fewer detectable metabolites than controls (155 vs 167 ChEBI species per sample, Mann-Whitney p = 0.012), as expected if substrates are consumed more quickly under warming.

A methodological contribution of this project is its careful handling of the DNA-versus-RNA comparison. It presents what it describes as the first explicit paired-sample comparison of DNA-pool and RNA-pool warming-response variance at this site, and shows that an apparent imbalance between the two pools is largely a horizon-by-incubation artifact rather than a real biological difference. Once that confound is removed, the DNA and RNA functional pools show comparable treatment R-squared (10-13%), meaning the transcript pool is not more warming-sensitive than the genome pool — a useful negative result for anyone designing future warming studies.

The project is unusually explicit about its limitations, and these caveats matter for how much weight to put on the findings. All samples come from a single timepoint (2017-05-24) with small cohorts (n = 28 metagenomes, n = 39 metatranscriptomes), which limits per-KO false-discovery power and rules out any seasonal interpretation. Coverage is unbalanced because the NMDC processing pipeline did not produce metagenomes for the organic-direct samples, which confounds horizon with incubation and is the direct source of the DNA/RNA sensitivity caveat above. The metatranscriptome KO counts are transcript-pool composition from contig annotations, not TPM-quantified expression, so they are biased by assembly quality (a medium-confidence caveat). And critically, the in-lakehouse abiotic_features table is all zeros for these samples, leaving the +5 C treatment label as the only environmental contrast — there is no in-lakehouse soil temperature, pH, or nitrogen to model. Two opportunities follow directly from these gaps: adding the excluded [Nmdc Arkin](../data/nmdc-arkin.md) quantitative layers (natural organic matter chemistry, metabolomics, proteomics) would put direct soil-chemistry evidence behind the carbon-cycling story, and linking to other NMDC warming studies (SPRUCE peatland and Alaskan permafrost thaw) would test whether the methanotrophy-up signature reproduces across sites.

### SNIPE defense system

This project characterizes SNIPE, a bacterial and archaeal antiphage defense system, by combining pangenome-scale distribution mapping with gene-level fitness evidence. Its most consequential outcome is a piece of functional-dark-matter resolution: InterPro has renamed entry IPR025280 from a generic domain of unknown function (DUF4041) to the "SNIPE associated domain," converting a previously dark domain into a defined antiphage marker on the strength of the underlying functional characterization. Using that diagnostic domain (DUF4041, Pfam PF13250) the project detects SNIPE in 4,572 gene clusters across 1,696 species spanning 33 bacterial and archaeal phyla in the [Kbase Ke Pangenome](../data/kbase-ke-pangenome.md) — far more than the 500-plus homologues reported in the original paper.

The distribution analysis ties SNIPE firmly to [Mobile Genetic Elements](../topics/mobile-genetic-elements.md) and [Pangenome Architecture](../topics/pangenome-architecture.md). SNIPE genes are predominantly accessory — only 13.3% are core, with a combined 86.7% accessory-plus-singleton fraction — meaning the system is typically gained or lost rather than vertically inherited. Its patchy spread across many phyla and families is consistent with horizontal gene transfer of defense islands rather than vertical descent. SNIPE-bearing species also occupy statistically distinct environmental niches: 22 of 64 AlphaEarth dimensions differ significantly (Bonferroni-corrected p < 0.05), though only at small-to-medium effect sizes, so the environmental shift is consistent but modest.

The mechanistic and fitness work explains why SNIPE is biologically interesting. SNIPE appears to resolve the classic phage-resistance-versus-metabolic-cost trade-off: phage lambda enters E. coli through the ManYZ transporter pore, so losing ManYZ confers resistance but at a steep metabolic price. RB-TnSeq (random-barcode transposon sequencing) fitness data from 168 E. coli K-12 experiments quantify that cost — ManXYZ knockouts suffer severe, substrate-specific defects (worst fitness -3.93 to -4.14) on mannose and glucosamine. Those same Fitness Browser data also overturn a database annotation: they directly contradict UniProt's "fructose transporter" label, showing ManXYZ mutants grow normally on fructose but are crippled on mannose and glucosamine, confirming ManYZ is a mannose/glucosamine-specific transporter. SNIPE's apparent solution is to cleave incoming phage DNA at the ManYZ pore while leaving the transporter functional, delivering the resistance benefit of a man knockout without its metabolic cost. The project also reports the first SNIPE homologue with genome-wide knockout phenotypes: Methanococcus maripaludis JJ carries a full two-domain SNIPE protein (locus MMJJ_RS01635, PF13250 plus PF13455) with 129 fitness experiments in the [Kescience Fitnessbrowser](../data/kescience-fitnessbrowser.md).

The nuclease-domain identity is pinned down precisely: the SNIPE nuclease is PF13455 (Mug113), a distinct Pfam family within the GIY-YIG clan CL0418, and notably not the canonical GIY-YIG family PF01541. That distinction means the complete absence of DUF4041-plus-PF01541 co-occurrences is a genuine biological result, not an annotation artifact. The project also flags clinical relevance through [Microbiome Engineering](../topics/microbiome-engineering.md): SNIPE was detected in the phage-therapy target Klebsiella, co-occurring with its mannose PTS transporter in the same genome (datasets such as [Phagefoundry](../data/phagefoundry.md) are relevant here), which matters because SNIPE could blunt phage-therapy efficacy in that pathogen.

Two annotation caveats temper the distribution numbers, and both should be read as honest uncertainty rather than fine print. About 20% of DUF4041 clusters carry non-defense primary annotations (for example histidine kinase or seryl-tRNA aminoacylation) that likely reflect annotation noise where DUF4041 is only a secondary domain, so a stricter T5orf172/DUF4041 description filter is needed to define a truly high-confidence SNIPE set. In the opposite direction, eggNOG Pfam annotations may miss divergent SNIPE homologues: only 54 of 4,572 DUF4041 clusters show Mug113 (PF13455) co-annotation, suggesting many SNIPE nuclease domains go undetected. The standing opportunity here is curation-driven — generating Klebsiella-specific SNIPE or ManYZ fitness data would require building new mutant libraries in natural SNIPE-carrying K. pneumoniae strains, a gap for which a fitness-data curation plan has been developed.

- [Functional Dark Matter](../topics/functional-dark-matter.md) — the conversion of DUF4041 into the named SNIPE associated domain, and the annotation-sensitivity caveats around detecting divergent homologues.
