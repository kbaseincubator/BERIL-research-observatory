# Mark Andrew Miller

Mark Andrew Miller is a contributor whose work in this wiki is documented through the *pseudomonas_carbon_ecology* project — a genus-scale study of how carbon source utilization varies across the *Pseudomonas* genus and what that variation reveals about microbial lifestyle and ecological adaptation. This page exists to orient readers to the scientific topics and data resources Miller's contribution touches, and to provide entry points into adjacent wiki pages.

## Overview

Miller's contribution centers on a single, tightly scoped project that nonetheless spans several cross-cutting themes: [metabolic pathways](../topics/metabolic-pathways.md), [microbial ecotypes](../topics/microbial-ecotypes.md), [environment biogeography](../topics/environment-biogeography.md), and [pangenome architecture](../topics/pangenome-architecture.md). The work draws on the [KBase KE Pangenome](../data/kbase-ke-pangenome.md) data collection — a compendium of genome-scale pangenome statistics and pathway predictions — and proposes integration with the [KE Science Fitness Browser](../data/kescience-fitnessbrowser.md), a database of gene fitness measurements from transposon-sequencing experiments. Together these resources make Miller's project an example of how large-scale genomic inventories can be mined to test ecological hypotheses without new sequencing.

The central analytical question is whether carbon metabolic pathway content, as predicted computationally across thousands of genomes, reflects the ecological lifestyle (free-living versus host-associated) and the isolation environment (soil, freshwater, rhizosphere, plant surface) of *Pseudomonas* species. GapMind, the pathway-prediction tool used here, is a curated database that scores whether a bacterium's genome encodes a complete biosynthetic or catabolic route for a given compound; the 62 carbon pathways it covers span sugars, sugar alcohols, organic acids, and amino acids. The study assembled GapMind predictions for 12,732 genomes across 433 *Pseudomonas* species clades (GTDB r214) from the BERDL kbase_ke_pangenome collection [\[1\]](#references), making it the first genus-scale quantification of carbon pathway profiles in *Pseudomonas* using standardized predictions [\[2\]](#references).

## Projects

### pseudomonas_carbon_ecology

The project's headline finding is a dramatic metabolic divide between the two major *Pseudomonas* subgenera. *Pseudomonas* sensu stricto (the *P. aeruginosa* group, dominated by clinical isolates) shows near-complete loss of plant-derived sugar catabolism relative to *Pseudomonas_E* (the *P. fluorescens/putida* group): 43 of 62 GapMind pathways differ significantly between the two [\[3\]](#references). The pathways most dramatically absent from the *P. aeruginosa* group are those for plant-derived pentose sugars and sugar alcohols — xylose (0% vs 74% in *P. fluorescens* group), arabinose (0% vs 63%), and myo-inositol (0% vs 59%) [\[4\]](#references). These are precisely the compounds found in plant cell walls and rhizosphere carbon cycling, consistent with the idea that *P. aeruginosa* ancestors abandoned a plant-associated lifestyle. By contrast, amino acid catabolism (arginine, histidine, serine, glutamate) and core organic acid pathways (citrate, succinate, pyruvate) remain near-universal (>99%) across both subgenera [\[5\]](#references), consistent with *P. aeruginosa* thriving in host environments such as cystic fibrosis (CF) lung sputum where amino acids dominate the available carbon.

A principal component analysis (PCA — a dimensionality-reduction method that finds the axes of greatest variation across many pathway profiles simultaneously) confirms that the *P. aeruginosa* / *P. fluorescens* phylogenetic split is the dominant signal across the genus, with lifestyle categories within *Pseudomonas_E* showing substantial overlap rather than clean separation [\[6\]](#references). Correspondingly, free-living and plant-associated species maintain modestly higher overall pathway richness (median 57 pathways complete in >50% of genomes) than host-associated species (median 55) [\[7\]](#references).

Within the free-living species, a PERMANOVA test (a permutation-based multivariate analysis of variance that tests whether group centroids in metabolic-profile space differ more than expected by chance) showed that carbon pathway profiles are significantly associated with isolation environment (p = 0.006, 999 permutations) [\[8\]](#references). A Random Forest classifier trained to predict four environment classes (soil, freshwater, plant surface, rhizosphere) from the 62-pathway profiles achieved a balanced accuracy of 0.41, well above the 0.25 chance baseline but only modestly predictive in absolute terms [\[9\]](#references). The top discriminating pathways were D-serine (associated with rhizosphere niches), arabinose, rhamnose, fucose, and xylose — all plant-derived substrates.

The project also makes an interpretive claim with implications for clinical microbiology: the metabolic versatility loss observed in *P. aeruginosa* during chronic infections may largely reflect ancestral streamlining of the *P. aeruginosa* lineage rather than pathway loss acquired during infection, since these pathways were already absent at the species level across thousands of isolates [\[10\]](#references).

## Topics

Miller's contribution sits at the intersection of several wiki topics. The [metabolic-pathways](../topics/metabolic-pathways.md) topic covers the biochemical routes at the core of this analysis. The [microbial-ecotypes](../topics/microbial-ecotypes.md) topic is adjacent because the project explicitly frames lifestyle-associated metabolic differences as candidate ecotype-defining traits; a proposed future direction is to analyze within-species pathway variation in widely distributed species such as *P. fluorescens* and *P. putida* to reveal metabolic ecotypes adapted to different niches [\[11\]](#references). The [environment-biogeography](../topics/environment-biogeography.md) topic captures the geographic and ecological dimension of the isolation-source metadata used to assign species to lifestyle categories. The [pangenome-architecture](../topics/pangenome-architecture.md) topic is linked because the underlying data come from pangenome collections and the pathway predictions vary across the accessory genome.

The [functional-dark-matter](../topics/functional-dark-matter.md) topic appears as an adjacent concern: GapMind's 62 carbon pathways miss genus-specific catabolic capabilities, particularly aromatic degradation pathways (toluene, naphthalene, benzoate) that are central to *P. putida* ecology [\[12\]](#references), representing a category of metabolic function that existing prediction tools do not yet capture systematically. The [gene-fitness](../topics/gene-fitness.md) topic is relevant through the proposed integration with RB-TnSeq (randomized-barcode transposon sequencing — a pooled mutant fitness assay that measures the growth advantage or disadvantage of gene knockouts across many conditions simultaneously) data from the Fitness Browser: cross-referencing pathway predictions with experimental fitness measurements could validate which predicted pathways are genuinely used in specific carbon sources [\[13\]](#references). Finally, the [microbiome-engineering](../topics/microbiome-engineering.md) topic connects to Miller's work through the retained amino-acid catabolism in *P. aeruginosa*, which has implications for engineering or predicting the behavior of this pathogen in host environments.

### Caveats and limitations

Several caveats qualify how far these findings can be generalized. First, *P. aeruginosa* comprises 53% of all 12,732 genomes in the dataset due to its clinical importance, while many environmental species have fewer than 10 sequenced genomes [\[14\]](#references) — an imbalance that limits the statistical power of species-level comparisons. Second, phylogenetic signal dominates: the dominant axis in PCA separates subgenera rather than lifestyles, and within *Pseudomonas_E* the lifestyle-associated differences are subtle, with no explicit control for phylogenetic non-independence [\[15\]](#references). Third, the GapMind pathway set does not cover aromatic degradation, which may be precisely the functional niche where environmental *P. putida* strains specialize [\[12\]](#references).
