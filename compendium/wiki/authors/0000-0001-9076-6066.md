# Mark Andrew Miller

Mark Andrew Miller (ORCID `0000-0001-9076-6066`) is a contributor in this compendium whose work centers on the comparative carbon metabolism of the bacterial genus *Pseudomonas*. This page exists to summarize that footprint: a single but data-rich project, `pseudomonas_carbon_ecology`, that asks how the genes encoding carbon-source catabolism are distributed across the breadth of the genus, and whether that distribution tracks where each species lives. The work sits at the intersection of pangenome analysis (cataloging which metabolic capabilities a clade has gained or lost), metabolic-pathway inference, and microbial ecology, and it leans heavily on standardized pathway predictions computed at genome scale from a public data lakehouse.

## Overview

Miller's contributions are best read as one coherent study built on a large genomic resource. The project extracted [GapMind](../topics/metabolic-pathways.md) carbon-pathway predictions for **12,732 genomes spanning 433 *Pseudomonas* species clades** (GTDB release r214) drawn from the BERDL [Kbase Ke Pangenome](../data/kbase-ke-pangenome.md) collection. GapMind is an automated tool that scores, gene by gene, whether a genome encodes the steps of a defined catabolic pathway; running it uniformly across thousands of genomes turns a messy comparative-genomics question into a standardized presence/absence matrix of 62 carbon pathways. The project frames this as the first systematic, genus-wide quantification of carbon-pathway profiles at this scale, made tractable precisely because the predictions were pre-computed in the pangenome resource rather than reconstructed from scratch.

The headline result is structural. The dominant axis of variation in carbon metabolism does not separate ecological lifestyles directly; instead it splits the genus along its deepest taxonomic seam — *Pseudomonas* sensu stricto (the *P. aeruginosa* group) versus the much broader *Pseudomonas_E* subgenus (the *P. fluorescens* group). The pathways most responsible for that split are the catabolic routes for plant-derived sugars and sugar alcohols such as xylose, arabinose, and myo-inositol, which are essentially absent (0% complete) in the *P. aeruginosa* group but present in 59–74% of the *P. fluorescens* group. Across the full pathway set, 43 of 62 GapMind pathways differ significantly between the two subgenera. Meanwhile, amino-acid and core organic-acid catabolism (arginine, histidine, serine, glutamate, citrate, succinate, pyruvate) stays near-universal (>99%) in both — consistent with the clinically important *P. aeruginosa* keeping the catabolic machinery it needs to grow in host niches like cystic-fibrosis (CF) sputum, even as it sheds plant-sugar pathways.

From this the project advances a notable interpretive claim: much of *P. aeruginosa*'s reduced metabolic versatility looks like **ancestral streamlining of the lineage** rather than gene loss acquired during chronic infection, because the missing pathways are already absent at the species level across thousands of isolates, not just in chronically adapted clinical strains. The project labels this a medium-confidence claim, and it pairs naturally with the more cautious ecological signal: among 54 free-living and plant-associated species, carbon-pathway profiles are significantly associated with isolation environment (permutation test p = 0.006), and free-living/plant-associated species carry slightly higher pathway richness (median 57 pathways) than host-associated ones (median 55), a trend that holds even inside *Pseudomonas_E* alone. A Random Forest classifier trained to predict four environment classes from pathway profiles reached balanced accuracy of 0.408 ± 0.169 — above the 0.250 chance baseline but only modestly predictive, which is an honest reminder that carbon-pathway content alone does not pin down a habitat.

The project is candid about its limits. The genome sampling is badly imbalanced: *P. aeruginosa* alone accounts for 53% of all genomes (a consequence of clinical sequencing priorities), while many environmental species have fewer than ten sequenced genomes, weakening species-level comparisons. The GapMind panel of 62 carbon pathways also misses genus-specific catabolic talents — notably the aromatic-degradation pathways (toluene, naphthalene, benzoate) that are central to *P. putida* ecology — so absence in the panel is not absence in the organism. And because the strongest signal separates subgenera, lifestyle differences within a subgenus are subtle and the analyses do not explicitly control for phylogenetic non-independence among species; some apparent lifestyle signal may simply be shared ancestry. The project points to two ways forward: dissecting within-species pathway variation in species sampled from many environments (e.g., *P. fluorescens*, *P. putida*) to look for true metabolic ecotypes, and cross-referencing the predictions against RB-TnSeq fitness data in the [Kescience Fitnessbrowser](../data/kescience-fitnessbrowser.md) to experimentally check which predicted pathways actually matter for growth on specific carbon sources.

## Projects

All of Miller's statements in this compendium belong to a single project, **`pseudomonas_carbon_ecology`**, a comparative-genomics study of carbon catabolism across the *Pseudomonas* genus.

The project's methodological backbone is the pairing of two public resources. Its input is the [Kbase Ke Pangenome](../data/kbase-ke-pangenome.md) collection in BERDL, from which it pulled GapMind carbon-pathway predictions for 12,732 genomes across 433 GTDB r214 species clades — this is what makes a genus-wide, standardized comparison possible. Its proposed validation route is the [Kescience Fitnessbrowser](../data/kescience-fitnessbrowser.md), the RB-TnSeq (randomly barcoded transposon sequencing) fitness resource: RB-TnSeq measures, in pooled growth experiments, how much each gene contributes to fitness under a given condition, so it can directly test whether a pathway GapMind predicts as "present" is genuinely required when the organism grows on, say, xylose or arabinose. That validation is framed as an opportunity rather than a completed result.

Analytically, the project combines a presence/absence pathway matrix with ordination and statistics: principal component analysis (PCA) to find the dominant aeruginosa-vs-*Pseudomonas_E* axis, PERMANOVA-style permutation testing to link profiles to isolation environment, and a Random Forest classifier to gauge how predictively environment can be read off pathway content. The findings (sugar-pathway loss, retained amino-acid catabolism, the subgenus split), the streamlining claim, and the three explicit caveats all flow from this one analysis pipeline.

## Topics

Miller's statements map onto a tight cluster of compendium topics, reflecting a study that is simultaneously about metabolism, ecology, and genome architecture.

- **[Metabolic Pathways](../topics/metabolic-pathways.md)** is the central thread. Nearly every statement concerns the presence, loss, or richness of GapMind-defined carbon-catabolism pathways — the sugar-pathway loss in *P. aeruginosa*, the near-universal retention of amino-acid catabolism, and the genome-scale extraction of pathway predictions all live here.
- **[Microbial Ecotypes](../topics/microbial-ecotypes.md)** captures the ecological question: whether subgroups of *Pseudomonas* are metabolically specialized to their habitats. The subgenus split, the modest environment-classifier accuracy, the ancestral-streamlining claim, and the proposed within-species ecotype analysis all sit at this topic. The caveat that the dominant signal is taxonomic rather than lifestyle-based is an important hedge against over-reading ecotype structure.
- **[Environment Biogeography](../topics/environment-biogeography.md)** is adjacent because the project tests carbon-profile associations with isolation source and environment class — the p = 0.006 environment signal and the Random Forest classifier belong here, alongside the sampling-bias and phylogenetic-confounding caveats that qualify any biogeographic inference.
- **[Pangenome Architecture](../topics/pangenome-architecture.md)** connects through the data foundation: the genus-scale tally of pathways across 433 species clades and 12,732 genomes is fundamentally a statement about how metabolic capability is partitioned across a pangenome.
- **[Functional Dark Matter](../topics/functional-dark-matter.md)** is touched by the GapMind-resolution caveat — the pathways the 62-pathway panel cannot see (aromatic degradation in *P. putida*) are exactly the kind of unannotated or out-of-scope function that genus-wide screens systematically under-count.
- **[Microbiome Engineering](../topics/microbiome-engineering.md)** is adjacent through the host-niche reading of retained amino-acid catabolism in *P. aeruginosa* (growth in CF sputum), which bears on how *Pseudomonas* metabolism is exploited or managed in host and applied settings.
- **[Gene Fitness](../topics/gene-fitness.md)** enters via the proposed RB-TnSeq validation: fitness data are the bridge from predicted pathway presence to demonstrated functional importance, which is why the Fitness Browser opportunity links this topic to the metabolic core of the work.
