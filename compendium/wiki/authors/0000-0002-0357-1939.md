# Priya Ranjan

Priya Ranjan (ORCID 0000-0002-0357-1939) is a contributor to the [`pgp_pangenome_ecology`](../projects/pgp-pangenome-ecology.md) project, a large-scale comparative genomics study that examines how plant growth-promoting (PGP) traits are distributed across bacterial pangenomes and environmental niches. This page exists in the wiki because the findings from that project challenge widely held assumptions about how PGP genes are inherited and what the canonical soil bacterium looks like, making Ranjan's contribution directly relevant to researchers working on microbiome engineering and agricultural inoculants.

## Overview

The [`pgp_pangenome_ecology`](../projects/pgp-pangenome-ecology.md) project analyzed 11,272 bacterial species carrying at least one of 13 focal PGP gene markers, drawn from a pangenome database of over 27,000 species. Its central question was whether PGP traits cluster non-randomly, whether the soil/rhizosphere environment shapes which traits predominate, and whether those traits spread through horizontal gene transfer (HGT) — the movement of genes between unrelated organisms — or through vertical inheritance from parent to offspring.

The answer to all three questions proved surprising. PGP traits do co-occur non-randomly, forming a coherent syndrome dominated by a tight pairing of **pqqC** (which encodes a step in PQQ cofactor biosynthesis, enabling phosphate solubilization) and **acdS** (ACC deaminase, which degrades the ethylene precursor ACC and thereby reduces plant stress responses). These two genes show the strongest positive co-occurrence of any pair tested (OR = 7.24, n = 286 co-occurring species) [\[1\]](#references). The soil/rhizosphere environment strongly selects for both: acdS is 7-fold more prevalent in soil species than in other environments (OR = 7.02), and the enrichment survives phylum-level statistical controls and a strict rhizosphere-only sensitivity analysis [\[2\]](#references). Together, the pqqC–acdS pairing is interpreted as a stable, vertically inherited niche adaptation characteristic of rhizosphere specialists rather than a recently assembled multi-trait package [\[3\]](#references).

The inheritance mode question yielded a decisive result. Across all 13 PGP genes, core fractions (the proportion of species in which a gene is present in nearly all sequenced strains) are dramatically higher than the genome-wide baseline: the mean accessory fraction for PGP genes is 29.7%, versus 53.2% genome-wide, which clearly rejects the hypothesis that PGP genes spread primarily by HGT [\[4\]](#references). Corroborating this, pangenome openness — measured as the singleton fraction, the proportion of genes unique to a single strain — correlates negatively with PGP gene richness (Spearman ρ = −0.195, p = 2.0e-97): PGP-rich species have more closed, less fluid genomes consistent with ecological specialization rather than generalist gene scavenging [\[5\]](#references).

One gene, **pqqD**, is an exception worth noting: its core fraction (55.5%) is lower than for other PGP genes (63–81%), and it shows the highest singleton fraction (27.5%), suggesting it occasionally moves horizontally as a standalone gene even though the functional pqqB–pqqC operon as a whole remains predominantly core [\[6\]](#references).

A separate line of analysis asked whether **nitrogen fixation** — the conversion of atmospheric N₂ to bioavailable ammonia, encoded by *nifH* — belongs to the same ecological guild as pqqC/acdS-bearing rhizobacteria. The answer is clearly no: nifH is negatively associated with both pqqC (OR = 0.57) and hcnC (OR = 0.23), forming a distinct ecological guild [\[7\]](#references). Strikingly, nifH is actually depleted in soil-classified genomes (OR = 0.60), because most nitrogen-fixing bacteria in the reference database are aquatic, marine, or host-associated (cyanobacteria, Azotobacter, and rhizobia) rather than free-living bulk-soil organisms [\[8\]](#references).

The project also examined the link between tryptophan biosynthesis capacity and the indole-3-pyruvate decarboxylase gene **ipdC**, which catalyzes a key step in auxin (IAA) biosynthesis from tryptophan. GapMind pathway completeness scores ≥ 0.9 for the tryptophan pathway significantly predict ipdC presence (2.5% vs 0.9% in trp-incomplete species; OR = 2.81) [\[9\]](#references). However, the tyrosine biosynthesis pathway — used as a negative control — predicts ipdC at a similar effect size (OR = 3.62), indicating that the association is not trp-specific [\[10\]](#references). The mechanistic explanation is that ipdC in *Enterobacter cloacae* and relatives is regulated by TyrR, a transcription factor that responds to all three aromatic amino acids (tryptophan, tyrosine, and phenylalanine). Species with complete aromatic amino acid biosynthesis are more likely to maintain the full TyrR regulatory circuit.

A stratified analysis adds further nuance: in soil/rhizosphere species, the trp–ipdC association reverses (OR = 0.30), whereas in non-soil species it holds strongly (OR = 3.56) [\[11\]](#references). The low confidence assigned to this finding reflects the small number of ipdC-carrying soil species (only 214 species carry ipdC at all, 1.9% of the dataset), so the soil reversal should be interpreted cautiously.

## Projects

All findings on this page derive from the **pgp_pangenome_ecology** project. That project queried the KBase pangenome database (see [Kbase Ke Pangenome](../data/kbase-ke-pangenome.md), which provides the genomic foundation for the species-level pangenome comparisons) across 291,279 genomes to assign environmental labels and compute pangenome statistics. PGP gene detection relied on annotation-level gene name matching (nifH, acdS, pqqC, etc.), which means genes annotated only by product description or with variant names may have been missed — a methodological limitation particularly affecting less-characterized PGP genes. Functional validation was not performed, so an annotated pqqC cluster is not guaranteed to encode a functional protein [\[12\]](#references). Environmental classification depended on NCBI isolation source metadata, which is noisy and biased toward clinical and host-associated isolates: only 5.9% of species could be confidently classified as soil/rhizosphere-dominant, meaning the reported soil enrichment effects are likely underestimates of the true environmental signal.

## Topics

The project's findings span several interconnected research themes.

**Pangenome architecture and mobile genetic elements.** The demonstration that PGP genes are predominantly core rather than accessory, and that pqqC–acdS co-occurrence reflects vertical inheritance rather than recent HGT, is a central contribution to understanding [pangenome architecture](../topics/pangenome-architecture.md). The pqqD exception illustrates that the accessory genome and [mobile genetic elements](../topics/mobile-genetic-elements.md) still play a minor role, and the open question of whether pqqC and acdS are physically co-located on a genomic island or operon would clarify whether their co-selection is driven by linked inheritance [\[13\]](#references).

**Environment and biogeography.** The soil/rhizosphere environment shapes PGP gene prevalence in a highly gene-specific way, with acdS and pqqC enriched but nifH depleted — a pattern that makes this project directly relevant to [environment biogeography](../topics/environment-biogeography.md) research. The NCBI sampling bias caveat means true soil enrichment is probably underestimated, and deeper study of how marine, host-associated, and soil diazotroph guilds differ structurally is identified as a priority.

**Microbial ecotypes.** The separation of the pqqC/acdS rhizosphere specialist guild from the diazotrophic guild, each with distinct co-occurrence partners, environmental distributions, and genome architectures, is a concrete example of [microbial ecotypes](../topics/microbial-ecotypes.md) — ecologically coherent subpopulations with distinct genomic signatures. PGP-rich specialists have more closed pangenomes consistent with long-term niche fidelity.

**Metabolic pathways.** The tryptophan–ipdC analysis connects pathway completeness (assessed with GapMind) to functional gene presence, illustrating how [metabolic pathways](../topics/metabolic-pathways.md) and their regulatory architecture (TyrR responding to all aromatic amino acids) constrain which organisms can produce IAA. The soil reversal suggests that rhizosphere bacteria may decouple autonomous trp biosynthesis from ipdC retention by scavenging tryptophan from root exudates.

**Microbiome engineering.** The pqqC–acdS module's tight co-selection, soil enrichment, and stable vertical inheritance make it a promising genomic marker for screening and designing agricultural inoculants — microorganisms deliberately applied to soil to promote plant growth [microbiome engineering](../topics/microbiome-engineering.md). The project proposes cross-referencing species carrying the pqqC + acdS + hcnC combination against commercially used inoculant strains as a direct test of whether this genomic signature predicts field efficacy [\[14\]](#references).
