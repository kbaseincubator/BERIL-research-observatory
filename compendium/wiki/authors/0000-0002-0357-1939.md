# Priya Ranjan

## Overview

Priya Ranjan is a contributor whose work in this compendium is concentrated in a single, tightly focused study: the comparative-genomics dissection of plant growth-promoting bacteria (PGPB) across a very large bacterial pangenome dataset. This page exists to summarize that contributor's footprint — which project they touched and which scientific topics their statements span — so a reader can navigate from a person to the ideas and evidence they helped produce. The recurring question across all of their statements is mechanistic and ecological at once: are the genes that make a bacterium "plant-growth-promoting" a recently shuffled, horizontally acquired toolkit, or a stable, niche-adapted feature of a lineage's core genome? The work answers, repeatedly and from several angles, that the canonical PGPB suite behaves like a stable, vertically inherited niche adaptation rather than a mobile accessory package.

The analyses sit at the intersection of [Pangenome Architecture](../topics/pangenome-architecture.md) (how genes partition into core versus accessory fractions), [Mobile Genetic Elements](../topics/mobile-genetic-elements.md) (whether genes move by horizontal gene transfer), [Microbiome Engineering](../topics/microbiome-engineering.md) (designing and selecting useful inoculant strains), [Metabolic Pathways](../topics/metabolic-pathways.md) (the biosynthetic context that gates a trait), [Microbial Ecotypes](../topics/microbial-ecotypes.md) (ecologically distinct guilds), and [Environment Biogeography](../topics/environment-biogeography.md) (where in nature these organisms are sampled). Because the underlying evidence is drawn from a pangenome resource, the work is adjacent to the [Kbase Ke Pangenome](../data/kbase-ke-pangenome.md) data collection.

## Projects

All fourteen of this contributor's statements belong to one project, **pgp_pangenome_ecology**, a pangenome-scale survey of plant growth-promoting traits. The project's logic is to take a panel of canonical PGP genes — for example *pqqC* (pyrroloquinoline quinone biosynthesis, underpinning phosphate solubilization), *acdS* (ACC deaminase, which lowers plant ethylene stress), *nifH* (nitrogenase, the marker of nitrogen fixation), *ipdC* (indole-3-pyruvate decarboxylase, a step in auxin/IAA biosynthesis), and *hcnC* (hydrogen cyanide synthesis) — and ask how those genes distribute across thousands of species, how they co-occur, where they are isolated from, and how "open" the pangenomes that carry them tend to be.

The central, high-confidence finding is structural. Across 11,272 species carrying at least one PGP gene, the traits do not co-occur randomly; *pqqC* and *acdS* form the strongest positive association (odds ratio 7.24), behaving as a coherent rhizosphere-effectiveness module. Crucially, all 13 PGP genes have significantly higher core-genome fractions than the genome-wide baseline of 46.8% — their mean accessory fraction is 29.7% against 53.2% genome-wide. (The "core genome" is the set of genes present in essentially every member of a species; the "accessory" fraction varies between strains and is the part most plausibly shuffled by horizontal transfer.) That result directly rejects the intuitive hypothesis that PGP genes spread predominantly by horizontal gene transfer. A second, independent line of evidence reinforces it: pangenome openness — measured here as the singleton fraction, the share of genes seen in only one genome — correlates negatively with PGP gene richness (Spearman ρ = −0.195, p = 2.0e-97). PGP-rich species therefore have more *closed* pangenomes, the signature of a stable specialized niche rather than a promiscuous generalist acquiring traits by transfer. Synthesizing these, the project frames the canonical PGPB phenotype as a tightly co-selected, rhizosphere-enriched, vertically inherited *pqqC* + *acdS* module — a stable niche adaptation, not a freshly assembled multi-trait cassette.

The project does not treat that conclusion as universal. Several findings introduce structure and exceptions. *nifH* sits apart: nitrogen fixation is *negatively* associated with the *pqqC*/*acdS* module, implying that diazotrophs (nitrogen fixers) form an ecologically separate guild and that the classical PGPB suite is largely a non-diazotrophic phenotype. The metabolic-context analyses add nuance to auxin biosynthesis. Tryptophan-biosynthesis completeness significantly predicts *ipdC* presence (2.5% versus 0.9% in trp-incomplete species; OR ≈ 2.81), but a control argues the link is not tryptophan-specific: a tyrosine-pathway negative control predicts *ipdC* at a similar effect size (OR = 3.62), consistent with TyrR-mediated regulation responding to all three aromatic amino acids rather than tryptophan alone. The project also flags two open, lower-confidence opportunities that would sharpen the picture: testing whether a *pqqC* + *acdS* + *hcnC* genomic signature predicts the field efficacy of commercial inoculant strains, and determining whether co-occurring *pqqC* and *acdS* are physically co-located on the same genomic island or operon — which would distinguish true operon linkage from mere statistical co-selection.

## Topics

**Pangenome Architecture.** This topic carries the project's load-bearing claims about where PGP genes live in the genome. The headline is that PGP genes are predominantly core, and that PGP-rich species have closed pangenomes — both pointing to vertical inheritance and niche stability rather than accessory churn.

**Mobile Genetic Elements.** The flip side of the same coin. The data broadly reject HGT-dominated spread of the PGP suite, yet the topic is not vacuous here: *pqqD* is an explicit outlier to the vertical-inheritance pattern, with the lowest core fraction (55.5%) and highest singleton fraction (27.5%) of the panel. That medium-confidence caveat suggests *pqqD* occasionally moves horizontally as a standalone gene even while the functional *pqqB–pqqC* operon stays predominantly core — a reminder that "the module is vertical" is a statement about the dominant tendency, not every component.

**Microbiome Engineering.** This is the applied frame. The *pqqC* + *acdS* module is presented as a candidate genomic marker for rhizosphere effectiveness, and the inoculant-efficacy opportunity proposes using it (plus *hcnC*) to predict which strains actually work in the field. The separation of *nifH* into its own guild also matters here: an engineer selecting for the classical PGPB phenotype should not expect nitrogen fixation to come bundled with it.

**Metabolic Pathways.** The auxin-biosynthesis story lives here. Tryptophan completeness predicts *ipdC*, but the tyrosine control reframes the association as general aromatic-amino-acid regulation (likely via TyrR) rather than a tryptophan-specific dependency.

**Microbial Ecotypes.** Several findings define ecologically coherent groups: diazotrophs as a separate guild from the *pqqC*/*acdS* PGPB, and the negative openness–PGP correlation read as evidence for stable specialized niches over generalist lifestyles.

**Environment Biogeography.** Where organisms are sampled drives some counterintuitive results. Soil/rhizosphere environments strongly select for *acdS* (about 7× more prevalent, OR = 7.02) and *pqqC* (OR = 2.90), with the *acdS* enrichment robust to phylum-level fixed effects and a strict rhizosphere-only sensitivity check. Yet *nifH* is significantly *depleted* in soil-classified species (OR = 0.60), because most database nitrogen fixers are aquatic/marine or host-associated rather than free-living soil diazotrophs. Two caveats temper the biogeography. First, the environmental signal is conservative: only 5.9% of species are classified soil/rhizosphere-dominant because the `ncbi_isolation_source` field is noisy and NCBI is biased toward clinical and host-associated isolates, so the reported soil enrichments are likely underestimates. Second, and at low confidence, the trp→*ipdC* positive coupling that holds in non-soil species (OR = 3.56) appears to *reverse* within soil/rhizosphere species (OR = 0.30) — possibly because soil PGPB scavenge tryptophan from plant root exudates and relax selection on autonomous trp biosynthesis. That reversal should be read cautiously given its low confidence, but it is the kind of environment-dependent mechanism the project is built to surface.
