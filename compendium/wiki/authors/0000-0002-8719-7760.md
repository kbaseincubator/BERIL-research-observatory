# Sierra Moxon

Sierra Moxon is a contributor whose work in this corpus focuses on the intersection of microbial genomics, metabolic pathway analysis, and evolutionary theory. This page exists in the wiki to track the scientific claims, methods, and open questions arising from her project contributions, and to connect her work to the broader cross-project themes of metabolic pathway ecology and pangenome evolution.

## Overview

Moxon's contribution centers on a systematic analysis of *metabolic capability versus metabolic dependency* — the question of whether a bacterium that encodes a complete metabolic pathway in its genome actually relies on that pathway for fitness. A pathway is called a **latent capability** when it is genomically complete (all the biosynthetic genes are present and intact) yet shows no detectable contribution to the organism's fitness under tested laboratory conditions. Quantifying how common such latent capabilities are, and what predicts them, is the central goal of this work.

Drawing on two large data resources — GapMind pathway predictions across roughly 293,000 genomes and fitness scores from the Fitness Browser covering 48 organisms — the project assigned each of 1,695 pathway-organism pairs to one of three dependency classes: active, intermediate, or latent. Across this dataset, 15.8% of genomically complete pathways were classified as latent capabilities [\[1\]](#references). The most striking predictor of latency was pathway category: carbon source utilization pathways were latent at a rate of 24.3%, while amino acid biosynthesis pathways were predominantly active (63.5% active, 6.5% latent), yielding a 3.7-fold enrichment of latency in carbon pathways [\[2\]](#references). The project identifies this as the first systematic quantification of the latent capability fraction across a taxonomically diverse set of bacteria, generating a testable evolutionary prediction: organisms in nutrient-rich environments should preferentially lose carbon-pathway genes over time while amino acid biosynthesis genes remain under stronger purifying selection [\[3\]](#references).

The classification itself is moderately sensitive to the fitness thresholds used. Varying the active and latent cutoffs by ±25% shifts the overall latent fraction from 4.7% to 21.1% across 16 combinations, but the qualitative conclusion — that a non-trivial fraction of complete pathways are fitness-neutral and that carbon pathways are most affected — holds throughout the tested range [\[4\]](#references).

## Projects

The single project represented here is [`metabolic_capability_dependency`](../projects/metabolic-capability-dependency.md). Its core hypotheses address three questions:

**H1 — Are latent capabilities common?** Yes: the 15.8% latent fraction across 1,695 pairs is supported with high confidence, and the carbon-vs-amino-acid contrast is statistically striking (χ²=163.6, p=2.5×10⁻³⁴) [\[2\]](#references).

**H2 — Do latent pathways erode faster (the Black Queen framework)?** Results are mixed. The **Black Queen Hypothesis** proposes that genes providing a public good to the community will be preferentially lost by free-riders who benefit from neighbors without paying the metabolic cost. Tested at the pathway-conservation level (H2a), conservation rates did not distinguish latent capabilities from active dependencies — indeed, latent capabilities showed slightly *higher* mean conservation (0.869 vs. 0.829), so H2a is not supported [\[5\]](#references). Tested at the clade level using **pangenome openness** — a measure of how rapidly new genes enter a species' pangenome, indicating how dynamic and streamlining-prone the gene pool is — the result is positive: the per-clade latent fraction correlates with pangenome openness at Spearman ρ = 0.69 (p = 0.0004, n = 22 clades), supporting the Black Queen framework at the species level (H2b) [\[6\]](#references).

The divergence between H2a and H2b is itself an interpretive finding: the Black Queen signal appears to operate at the level of genome dynamics and community context rather than being legible in per-pathway cross-genome conservation ratios [\[7\]](#references). This framing connects naturally to the [Pangenome Architecture](../topics/pangenome-architecture.md) topic page, which covers the broader literature on what pangenome openness means for gene flux and evolutionary streamlining.

**H3 — Do within-species metabolic pathway profiles form discrete ecotypes?** Yes, and broadly: all 10 target species with at least 50 genomes and 15 variable pathways showed meaningful metabolic clustering with silhouette scores above 0.2, ranging up to 0.89 for *Salmonella enterica* [\[8\]](#references). A **metabolic ecotype** is a within-species cluster of strains that share a distinctive repertoire of complete metabolic pathways, analogous to ecotypes defined by 16S or genomic phylogeny but based on functional gene content. These ecotypes were linked to isolation environment in nutrient-rich (copiotrophic) species such as *Salmonella enterica* and *Phenylobacterium* sp., but the same environment-cluster correlation was absent in marine oligotrophs (*Stutzerimonas*, *Alteromonas*) [\[9\]](#references). The [Microbial Ecotypes](../topics/microbial-ecotypes.md) and [Environment Biogeography](../topics/environment-biogeography.md) topic pages provide cross-project context for these patterns.

Data for the project flows from two linked collections. The [Kbase Ke Pangenome](../data/kbase-ke-pangenome.md) dataset supplies GapMind pathway completeness predictions (305 million rows, 80 pathways, 293,000 genomes) along with pangenome openness values derived from the pangenome table. The [Kescience Fitnessbrowser](../data/kescience-fitnessbrowser.md) dataset supplies gene-level fitness scores (t-scores and essentiality calls) for 48 organisms. Linking these two resources required mapping Fitness Browser organisms to GapMind species clades via NCBI taxonomy IDs, a step that encountered a data-quality failure: the relevant column in `gtdb_metadata` contained boolean strings rather than numeric taxids, returning zero matches and forcing analyses to proceed at the organism level without an explicit clade-level linkage [\[10\]](#references).

## Topics

The project spans five topic areas tracked in this wiki.

**[Metabolic Pathways](../topics/metabolic-pathways.md)** is the central theme. GapMind predicts pathway completeness across a vast genome database; the project's contribution is to annotate each complete pathway with a fitness-based dependency class, creating a genome-scale map of which pathways are actually used. The [Gene Fitness](../topics/gene-fitness.md) page is directly adjacent because the latency classification depends entirely on fitness t-scores and essentiality rates from RB-TnSeq experiments (**RB-TnSeq**, or randomly barcoded transposon sequencing, is a pooled fitness assay in which thousands of barcoded transposon insertion mutants compete under a test condition; the resulting t-scores measure each gene's fitness contribution in that condition).

**[Pangenome Architecture](../topics/pangenome-architecture.md)** enters through the H2b test. A pangenome is the union of all genes found in any sequenced strain of a species; "openness" measures how much the pangenome grows with each new genome added, and high openness implies ongoing gene gain and loss. The H2b result — that clades with more latent capabilities also have more open pangenomes — suggests a mechanistic connection between functional dispensability and pangenome flux. A caveat on this correlation deserves explicit attention: multiple Fitness Browser organisms often map to the same GapMind species clade and therefore share identical openness values, violating the independence assumption of the Spearman test and potentially inflating the apparent significance [\[11\]](#references).

**[Microbial Ecotypes](../topics/microbial-ecotypes.md)** and **[Environment Biogeography](../topics/environment-biogeography.md)** are addressed through the H3 analysis. The finding that ecotype-environment associations are strong in copiotrophs but absent in marine oligotrophs hints at a lifestyle-dependent dynamic: in oligotrophic (low-nutrient) marine environments, metabolic repertoire may be shaped more by nutrient scarcity than by habitat identity, producing ecotypes that do not track isolation source in the same way.

Future directions identified in the project include replacing pathway-level conservation with per-gene dN/dS ratios or gene presence/absence across a phylogeny to directly test the Black Queen prediction of progressive gene loss [\[12\]](#references), and identifying lineages with recent singleton or auxiliary-gene losses in latent pathways and verifying these against phylogenetic deletion events [\[13\]](#references). These next steps would shift the evidence from a cross-sectional snapshot to a time-resolved test of the evolutionary mechanism proposed by the Black Queen Hypothesis.
