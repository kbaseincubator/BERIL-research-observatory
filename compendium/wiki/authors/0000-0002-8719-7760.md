# Sierra Moxon

Sierra Moxon (ORCID `0000-0002-8719-7760`) is a contributor to the
compendium whose recorded work sits entirely within a single line of
inquiry: distinguishing the metabolic pathways that a bacterium *can*
run from the ones it actually *needs*. This page exists to summarize
that contributor's footprint across projects and topics, gathering the
findings, claims, caveats, and open opportunities attributed to them in
one place so the rest of the wiki can link back to a single author
profile.

## Overview

All of the statements on this page come from one project,
[metabolic capability dependency](#projects), which asks a deceptively
simple question: when a genome encodes a complete metabolic pathway,
does that pathway matter for the organism's fitness? The work combines
two complementary data resources. [GapMind](../data/kbase-ke-pangenome.md)-style
pathway calls (which infer whether a genome can, in principle, carry out
a given carbon-utilization or amino-acid-biosynthesis pathway) are
cross-referenced against the [Fitness Browser](../data/kescience-fitnessbrowser.md),
a large compendium of genome-wide RB-TnSeq fitness measurements that
report whether knocking out a gene actually hurts the organism under
specific growth conditions.

The headline result is that a substantial slice of metabolic potential
is dormant. Across 1,695 pathway-organism pairs drawn from 48 organisms,
roughly 15.8% of pathways that are genomically complete are nonetheless
*latent capabilities* — fully encoded by the genome but showing no
detectable fitness importance under the conditions tested. The
contributor's work frames this latent fraction as a quantifiable
property of bacterial genomes and connects it to ecological theory,
testing whether the Black Queen Hypothesis (the idea that microbes
progressively shed costly functions they can rely on neighbors to
provide) explains why so many encoded pathways go unused. The picture
that emerges is nuanced: the Black Queen signal shows up at the level of
genome dynamics and species clades, but not at the finer per-pathway
conservation level, and several measurement caveats temper how strongly
the supporting correlation can be read.

## Projects

The contributor's single project,
[metabolic capability dependency](../data/kbase-ke-pangenome.md), builds
a dependency classification for metabolic pathways and then probes its
ecological meaning.

The core finding is the latent-capability fraction itself: of the
pathways that are present and complete in a genome, about 15.8% leave no
fitness signature, meaning the organism carries the genes but does not
appear to depend on them under tested conditions. This fraction is not a
fragile artifact of one cutoff. When the classification thresholds that
decide "complete" and "fitness-important" are swept across 16
combinations, the latent fraction moves between 4.7% and 21.1%, yet the
qualitative conclusion — that a non-trivial share of complete pathways
is fitness-neutral — holds across the entire range. The project frames
this as a systematic quantification of latent capability across diverse
bacteria, and it yields a concrete, testable prediction: carbon-source
utilization pathways are roughly 3.7-fold more likely to be latent than
amino-acid-biosynthesis pathways (24.3% versus 6.5%).

That contrast between pathway categories is one of the strongest signals
in the work. Pathway category strongly predicts dependency class:
carbon-source utilization pathways are the most likely to be latent
(24.3%), whereas amino-acid-biosynthesis pathways are predominantly
active (63.5% active, only 6.5% latent). The biological reading is
intuitive — biosynthetic routes that an organism needs to make its own
building blocks tend to be load-bearing, while the ability to consume a
particular carbon source is more often an optional extra that only pays
off when that substrate is around.

The project then asks whether the Black Queen Hypothesis can explain the
latent fraction, and the answer is split across two sub-hypotheses.
Tested at the per-pathway conservation level (H2a), it fails: pathway
conservation rates do not differ between latent capabilities and active
dependencies, so there is no sign that latent pathways are being
preferentially conserved or lost gene-by-gene in the way a naive Black
Queen reading would predict. Tested at the species-clade level (H2b),
it succeeds: after aggregating 41 organisms into 22 unique species
clades, the per-clade latent fraction correlates positively with
pangenome openness (Spearman rho = 0.69, p = 0.0004). Pangenome openness
measures how readily a species keeps acquiring new genes across its
genomes; the positive correlation says clades with more open, fluid gene
content also tend to carry more dormant metabolic capability. The
contributor reconciles these two results by arguing the Black Queen
signal operates at the level of genome dynamics and community context
rather than in per-pathway cross-genome conservation ratios.

Two honest caveats restrain how far the clade-level result should be
pushed. First, the H2b correlation has a non-independence problem:
several Fitness Browser organisms map onto the same GapMind species
clade and therefore share an identical pangenome-openness value, so the
22 clade points are not fully independent observations. Second, a
planned linkage between Fitness Browser organisms and GapMind clades via
NCBI taxonomy IDs returned zero matches because the `gtdb_metadata` taxid
column held boolean strings instead of numeric taxids; as a workaround
the analyses leaned on organism-level aggregates without explicit clade
linkage. Both caveats are surfaced directly in the project and are worth
keeping in mind when interpreting the correlation strength.

Finally, the project points to longitudinal follow-ups it could not yet
run. One opportunity is to find lineages with recent gene-loss events in
pathways already classified as latent and confirm, on the phylogeny,
that these are genuine deletions — a direct longitudinal test of the
latent-capability framework. A related opportunity is to replace
coarse pathway-level conservation with per-gene dN/dS or gene
presence/absence mapped across a phylogeny, which would test the Black
Queen prediction of *progressive* gene loss far more directly than the
static conservation ratios used here.

## Topics

The contributor's work touches five topics in the wiki, and the way they
interlock is most of the story.

[Metabolic pathways](../topics/metabolic-pathways.md) is the spine of
every statement: pathways are the unit being classified as latent or
active, and the carbon-versus-amino-acid contrast lives entirely here.
This connects tightly to [gene fitness](../topics/gene-fitness.md),
because "latent" is defined by the *absence* of a fitness signature in
the Fitness Browser RB-TnSeq data — a complete pathway with no
measurable cost when disrupted under tested conditions. The robustness
analysis across 16 thresholds and the 15.8% headline fraction both sit
at the intersection of these two topics.

[Pangenome architecture](../topics/pangenome-architecture.md) is where
the ecological theory is tested. Pangenome openness is the variable that
correlates with the latent fraction at the clade level, and the
per-pathway conservation analysis — the one that did *not* support the
Black Queen Hypothesis — is also a pangenome-architecture question. The
opportunities to use dN/dS and gene presence/absence across phylogenies
would deepen exactly this topic, moving from pangenome-summary statistics
toward gene-level evolutionary signals.

[Microbial ecotypes](../topics/microbial-ecotypes.md) enters through a
separate but complementary result: within-species metabolic
heterogeneity. All 10 target species with at least 50 genomes and at
least 15 variable pathways showed meaningful metabolic clustering (every
silhouette score above 0.2), establishing that bacteria of the same
species often split into distinct metabolic ecotypes. The link to
[environment biogeography](../topics/environment-biogeography.md) is that
these clusters are not always ecologically neutral — they correlate
significantly with isolation environment in copiotrophs such as
*Salmonella enterica* and *Phenylobacterium* sp., but not in marine
oligotrophs such as *Stutzerimonas* and *Alteromonas*. That asymmetry
suggests environment shapes metabolic specialization more strongly in
nutrient-rich settings than in the dilute open ocean, though it is
reported as a medium-confidence pattern rather than a firm rule.
