# Christopher Neely

Christopher Neely (ORCID 0000-0002-2620-8948) is a contributor to a cluster of projects that investigate how microbial metabolic capabilities are shaped by genomic architecture and environmental context. This page exists in the wiki because Neely's work spans three related computational projects — each using large public data resources to probe the relationship between pangenome structure, metabolic pathway completeness, and ecological niche — and his statements supply much of the quantitative evidence underpinning the wiki's topics on [metabolic pathways](../topics/metabolic-pathways.md), [pangenome architecture](../topics/pangenome-architecture.md), and [microbial ecotypes](../topics/microbial-ecotypes.md).

## Overview

Neely's projects share a common data foundation: GapMind pathway completeness predictions (a tool that scores whether each of up to 80 biosynthetic pathways is encoded in a given genome), the KBase pangenome database, the Fitness Browser (gene-fitness scores from transposon-sequencing experiments across 48 organisms), and the NMDC multi-omics collection. Together these resources enabled three interlinked lines of inquiry:

**Latent metabolic capabilities and the Black Queen Hypothesis.** The Black Queen Hypothesis (BQH) proposes that microbes can lose biosynthetic genes for a metabolite when other community members reliably supply it — an evolutionary "freeloading" dynamic. A central finding from the [`metabolic_capability_dependency`](../projects/metabolic-capability-dependency.md) project is that 15.8% of genomically complete pathways are "latent capabilities" — the genome encodes them, but they show no detectable fitness importance under tested laboratory conditions [\[1\]](#references). Pathway category strongly predicted this classification: carbon source utilization pathways were 3.7-fold more likely to be latent (24.3%) than amino acid biosynthesis pathways (6.5%) [\[2\]](#references). This is a specific, testable prediction — that carbon-pathway genes should be preferentially lost in nutrient-rich environments over evolutionary time [\[3\]](#references). Across 16 threshold combinations for the classification cutoffs, the latent fraction ranged from 4.7% to 21.1%, but the qualitative conclusion held throughout [\[4\]](#references).

**Community-scale BQH signal in environmental metabolomics.** The [`nmdc_community_metabolic_ecology`](../projects/nmdc-community-metabolic-ecology.md) project extended this single-organism logic to whole communities. By computing community-weighted GapMind pathway completeness for 220 NMDC samples spanning soil and freshwater habitats, and integrating matched LC-MS metabolomics, this work provided what is believed to be the first application of GapMind completeness scores to predict environmental metabolite pools at scale [\[5\]](#references). Across 13 testable amino acid biosynthesis pathways, 11 (85%) showed negative Spearman correlations between community completeness and ambient amino acid metabolite intensity — the direction BQH predicts [\[6\]](#references). Leucine (r = −0.390, q = 0.022) and arginine (r = −0.297, q = 0.049) reached FDR significance [\[7\]](#references); these are both energetically expensive pathways (leucine costs 37 ATP equivalents to synthesize), consistent with BQH's prediction that costly functions are most likely to be lost when environmental supply is reliable [\[8\]](#references). A principal-component analysis of the full 220-sample × 80-pathway matrix captured 49.4% of variance in PC1 alone, with soil and freshwater communities occupying nearly non-overlapping PC space [\[9\]](#references). Carbon utilization pathways, not amino acid pathways, dominated PC1 loading, suggesting that carbon substrate availability is the primary axis differentiating ecosystem metabolic type [\[10\]](#references). Seventeen of 18 amino acid pathways differed significantly across ecosystem types after FDR correction, only tyrosine being non-significant [\[11\]](#references).

**Pangenome openness, niche breadth, and pathway completeness.** The [`pangenome_pathway_geography`](../projects/pangenome-pathway-geography.md) project scaled up to 1,872 species with complete data across three datasets. All three hypotheses under study received statistically significant support [\[12\]](#references): (1) pangenome openness correlated weakly but significantly with mean pathway completeness (r = 0.107, p = 3.6×10⁻⁶) [\[13\]](#references); (2) ecological niche breadth — quantified using AlphaEarth satellite-derived habitat embeddings rather than raw geographic distance — was a much stronger predictor of pathway completeness (r = 0.392, p = 7.1×10⁻⁷⁰) [\[14\]](#references), with embedding variance providing the strongest signal of all (r = 0.412, p = 1.8×10⁻⁷⁷) [\[15\]](#references); and (3) pangenome openness correlated positively with niche breadth (r = 0.324, p = 5.6×10⁻⁴⁷) [\[16\]](#references). Strikingly, a high core-genome fraction — the fraction of gene families shared by all members of a species — was strongly negatively correlated with niche breadth (r = −0.445, p = 1.4×10⁻⁹¹) [\[17\]](#references), providing converging evidence that pangenome flexibility enables ecological adaptation [\[18\]](#references). The accessory genome (genes present in some but not all members of a species) is interpreted as enabling metabolic heterogeneity within a species [\[19\]](#references), which is consistent with the observation that ecological diversity predicts metabolic completeness better than spatial spread alone [\[20\]](#references).

Within-species metabolic ecotypes emerged as a general feature: all 10 target bacterial species with at least 50 genomes and 15 variable pathways showed meaningful metabolic clustering (silhouette scores > 0.2) [\[21\]](#references). Metabolic clusters correlated with isolation environment in copiotrophs (nutrient-rich-adapted organisms) such as *Salmonella enterica* and *Phenylobacterium* sp., but not in marine oligotrophs such as *Stutzerimonas* and *Alteromonas* [\[22\]](#references). At the clade level, the fraction of latent capabilities correlated positively with pangenome openness (Spearman ρ = 0.69, p = 0.0004) [\[23\]](#references), supporting the BQH at the species level, even though pathway-level conservation rates did not differ between latent and active pathways [\[24\]](#references). This divergence suggests the Black Queen signal operates at the level of genome dynamics and community context rather than in per-pathway cross-genome conservation ratios [\[25\]](#references).

The NMDC-to-GTDB taxonomy bridge that enables connecting community metagenomes to pangenome data achieved mean coverage of 94.6%, with 92% of samples mapping at least 85% of community abundance to GTDB pangenome species [\[26\]](#references).

## Projects

**[`metabolic_capability_dependency`](../projects/metabolic-capability-dependency.md)** quantified latent metabolic capabilities across 48 organisms from the [Fitness Browser](../data/kescience-fitnessbrowser.md) (a database of transposon-sequencing gene-fitness scores), mapped to GapMind pathway completeness predictions from the [KBase pangenome](../data/kbase-ke-pangenome.md). The key contribution is the first systematic quantification of the "latent capability fraction" — complete-but-fitness-neutral pathways — across diverse bacteria. The project also characterized metabolic ecotypes within 10 target species using hierarchical clustering of pathway profiles across thousands of genomes.

**[`nmdc_community_metabolic_ecology`](../projects/nmdc-community-metabolic-ecology.md)** linked the same GapMind pathway completeness framework to environmental metabolomics from [NMDC (National Microbiome Data Collaborative)](../data/nmdc-arkin.md). It integrated 305M GapMind pathway records (27,690 GTDB species × 80 pathways) with NMDC multi-omics for 220 samples. The project tests community-scale BQH dynamics and ecosystem metabolic niche separation across soil and freshwater habitats.

**[`pangenome_pathway_geography`](../projects/pangenome-pathway-geography.md)** scales the pangenome-pathway connection to the full breadth of the KBase pangenome database, adding AlphaEarth ecological context embeddings to measure niche breadth beyond simple geographic distance. The three-stage GapMind aggregation pipeline (305M rows → 27.6M genome-pathway pairs → 293K genomes → 27.7K species) is a notable technical contribution [\[27\]](#references).

## Topics

Neely's work contributes quantitative evidence to several topics in this wiki:

- **[Metabolic Pathways](../topics/metabolic-pathways.md)**: The central empirical thread — GapMind pathway completeness as a genome-scale predictor of both biosynthetic potential and community metabolite pools.
- **[Pangenome Architecture](../topics/pangenome-architecture.md)**: The finding that open pangenomes (species with many accessory genes) tend to have higher latent fractions and broader ecological niches links this structural property to functional ecology.
- **[Microbial Ecotypes](../topics/microbial-ecotypes.md)**: Within-species metabolic clustering across thousands of genomes establishes metabolic ecotypes as a general bacterial phenomenon, related to but distinct from phylogenetic subspecies.
- **[Environment Biogeography](../topics/environment-biogeography.md)**: The consistent finding that ecological diversity (AlphaEarth embedding variance) predicts metabolic completeness better than geographic distance is a key result for this topic.
- **[Gene Fitness](../topics/gene-fitness.md)**: The Fitness Browser data underpin the classification of pathways as latent versus active, and the BQH prediction that energetically costly pathways are preferentially lost.
- **[Functional Dark Matter](../topics/functional-dark-matter.md)**: Latent capabilities — genes present but apparently fitness-neutral — are a form of genomic functional dark matter whose evolutionary fate is a central open question.

Several important caveats constrain interpretation across these projects. On the data side, only 6.8% of the full 27,690 species had complete data for the niche-breadth analysis due to AlphaEarth embedding sparsity (AlphaEarth requires lat/lon coordinates, often missing for clinical isolates) [\[28\]](#references). The reported correlations do not correct for phylogenetic non-independence or for unequal genome counts per species, meaning shared ancestry and sampling intensity could confound the relationships [\[29\]](#references). In the BQH openness analysis, multiple Fitness Browser organisms map to the same GapMind species clade and thus share an identical pangenome openness value, potentially inflating the apparent Spearman correlation [\[30\]](#references). For the community metabolomics work, all 33 freshwater samples lacked paired metabolomics in NMDC, so the BQH community signal is effectively a soil-only test [\[31\]](#references). Additionally, abiotic features such as pH and temperature were absent for the analysis matrix, preventing partial correlation tests that would control for environmental gradients [\[32\]](#references). GapMind pathway completeness reflects genomic potential (gene presence), not active expression, so transcriptomic data would be needed to establish whether expressed biosynthesis is what drives the BQH metabolomics signal [\[33\]](#references). A data engineering caveat: correct GapMind aggregation requires taking the best (MAX) score per genome-pathway pair, since GapMind stores one row per pathway step, not per pathway; ignoring this corrupts completeness counts [\[34\]](#references). Finally, an attempt to link Fitness Browser organisms to GapMind species clades via NCBI taxonomy IDs returned zero matches because the GTDB metadata column contained boolean strings rather than numeric taxids, forcing organism-level rather than clade-level aggregation [\[35\]](#references).

Open directions include: extending the community BQH test to freshwater samples [\[36\]](#references); pairing metatranscriptomics with metabolomics to test whether gene expression (not just presence) drives the signal [\[37\]](#references); replacing pathway-level conservation measures with per-gene dN/dS to directly test the BQH gene-loss prediction [\[38\]](#references); and adding genome-count-stratified partial correlations to rule out sampling-intensity confounding [\[39\]](#references).
