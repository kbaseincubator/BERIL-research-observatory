# Christopher Neely

Christopher Neely (ORCID `0000-0002-2620-8948`) is a contributor whose work in this compendium asks a single connecting question across three projects: when a bacterium *can* perform a metabolic function according to its genome, does it actually *need* to, and what does the answer reveal about how microbes adapt to their environments? This page exists to gather that footprint in one place — linking the projects, the datasets they draw on, and the recurring topics that tie them together — so a reader can see how findings about individual genomes, whole species pangenomes, and entire communities reinforce (and sometimes complicate) one another.

## Overview

Neely's three projects share a common analytical backbone: GapMind, a tool that infers whether a genome encodes a *complete* metabolic pathway (such as the biosynthesis of a particular amino acid or the utilization of a carbon source) by checking for the presence of the required enzyme steps. The recurring tension throughout the work is that genomic completeness is necessary but not sufficient — a pathway can be fully present yet contribute nothing to fitness, or be selectively lost when the environment reliably supplies its product. This idea is captured formally by the [Black Queen Hypothesis](../topics/gene-fitness.md), the prediction that costly biosynthetic functions are preferentially shed when a community member can rely on neighbors or the environment to provide the metabolite.

The work spans three biological scales. At the **single-genome scale**, pairing GapMind completeness with experimentally measured [gene fitness](../topics/gene-fitness.md) from the Fitness Browser reveals that a meaningful fraction of complete pathways are "latent" — encoded but fitness-neutral under tested conditions. At the **species/pangenome scale**, the same notion of latency and loss is examined against [pangenome architecture](../topics/pangenome-architecture.md) and [ecological niche breadth](../topics/environment-biogeography.md). At the **community scale**, GapMind scores aggregated over whole microbial communities are tested against environmental metabolomics to see whether the Black Queen signature appears in nature.

The footprint is heavy on honest caveats. Several analyses rest on incomplete data coverage, missing environmental covariates, or statistical non-independence, and the work is consistent about flagging where conclusions are provisional. These limitations are surfaced explicitly in the sections below rather than buried.

## Projects

**`metabolic_capability_dependency`** is the single-genome anchor of Neely's work. By joining GapMind pathway calls to fitness measurements from the [Fitness Browser](../data/kescience-fitnessbrowser.md), the project quantifies *latent capabilities*: pathways the genome can complete but that show no detectable fitness importance. Across 1,695 pathway-organism pairs from 48 organisms, 15.8% of genomically complete pathways were latent. This fraction is sensitive to where one draws the classification threshold — it ranges from 4.7% to 21.1% across sixteen threshold combinations — but the qualitative conclusion that a non-trivial share of complete pathways are fitness-neutral holds across the full range. The project frames this as a systematic, novel quantification of the latent fraction across diverse bacteria, with a sharp testable prediction: carbon-source utilization pathways are 3.7-fold more likely to be latent than amino-acid biosynthesis pathways (24.3% vs 6.5%). Indeed, pathway category turned out to be a strong predictor of dependency class, with amino-acid biosynthesis predominantly active (63.5% active) and carbon utilization disproportionately latent.

This project also stress-tests the Black Queen Hypothesis directly, and the result is split. At the per-pathway conservation level (hypothesis H2a), conservation rates did not differ between latent and active pathways, so Black Queen was *not* supported there. But at the species level (H2b), after aggregating 41 organisms into 22 unique species clades, the per-clade latent fraction correlated positively with [pangenome openness](../topics/pangenome-architecture.md) (Spearman rho = 0.69, p = 0.0004). The interpretation is that the Black Queen signal lives at the level of genome dynamics and community context rather than in per-pathway cross-genome conservation ratios. Two caveats temper this: an intended NCBI-taxonomy link between Fitness Browser organisms and GapMind clades returned zero matches because the GTDB metadata taxid column held boolean strings instead of numeric IDs (forcing reliance on organism-level aggregates), and the H2b correlation suffers non-independence because several Fitness Browser organisms collapse onto the same clade and share an identical openness value. The project also found that within-species metabolic heterogeneity is general — all ten species with sufficient genomes showed meaningful metabolic clustering — and that those clusters track isolation environment in copiotrophs (*Salmonella enterica*, *Phenylobacterium* sp.) but not in marine oligotrophs (*Stutzerimonas*, *Alteromonas*), pointing toward genuine [metabolic ecotypes](../topics/microbial-ecotypes.md).

**`nmdc_community_metabolic_ecology`** scales the question up to whole communities, integrating 305 million GapMind records with NMDC multi-omics for 220 samples — described as the first application of community-weighted GapMind completeness scores to predict environmental metabolomics across a large cross-habitat dataset, drawing on the [NMDC Arkin](../data/nmdc-arkin.md) collection. The community metabolic profile cleanly separates ecosystems: PCA of a 220-sample by 80-pathway matrix put 49.4% of variance on PC1, with Soil and Freshwater communities occupying nearly non-overlapping regions, and 17 of 18 amino-acid pathways differed significantly in completeness across [ecosystem types](../topics/environment-biogeography.md). Carbon-utilization pathways (not amino-acid pathways) loaded almost entirely on PC1, suggesting carbon-substrate availability is the primary axis differentiating community metabolic type. The headline Black Queen test held up: across 13 testable amino-acid biosynthesis pathways, 11 (85%) showed the predicted negative correlation between community completeness and ambient metabolite intensity (binomial sign test p = 0.011), with leucine (r = -0.390, q = 0.022) and arginine (r = -0.297, q = 0.049) reaching FDR significance. Both are energetically expensive to synthesize, matching the prediction that costly functions are lost first when environmental supply is reliable. The signal survived sensitivity checks and is effectively single-study (95% of samples from one NMDC study), so cross-study protocol heterogeneity does not confound it, and the NMDC-to-GTDB taxonomy bridge achieved 94.6% mean coverage. The honest limits are substantial: abiotic features (pH, temperature, organic carbon) were entirely absent for the analysis matrix, preventing partial-correlation control for environmental gradients; all 33 Freshwater samples lacked paired metabolomics, making this effectively a soil-only test; and GapMind completeness reports genomic potential, not expression, so transcriptomics would be needed to know whether *expressed* biosynthesis tracks metabolite pools more tightly.

**`pangenome_pathway_geography`** examines the same triad — pangenome openness, metabolic completeness, and ecological niche breadth — across 1,872 bacterial species, using GapMind pathways, [KBase KE pangenome](../data/kbase-ke-pangenome.md) data, and AlphaEarth satellite embeddings as a proxy for environmental context. All three hypotheses linking these axes showed statistically significant support. Ecological niche breadth (AlphaEarth embedding diversity) was the strongest predictor of mean pathway completeness (r = 0.392), and embedding variance was even stronger (r = 0.412); ecology beat raw geography, since geographic distance alone gave a weaker signal (r = 0.360). Open-pangenome species had broader niche breadth (r = 0.324), and a high core-genome fraction was strongly *negatively* associated with niche breadth (r = -0.445, p = 1.4e-91), converging on the reading that pangenome flexibility — accessory genes enabling within-species metabolic heterogeneity — supports ecological adaptation. Notably, pangenome openness itself correlated only weakly with pathway completeness (r = 0.107), so the openness-to-niche link is much firmer than the openness-to-completeness link. Two methodological notes carry beyond this project: GapMind stores multiple rows per genome-pathway pair (one per step), so correct aggregation requires taking the MAX score to avoid corrupting completeness counts; and the full three-stage pipeline (305M rows to 27.6M genome-pathway pairs to 293K genomes to 27.7K species) is a reusable pattern for working with the 305M-row pathway table at scale. The major caveat is coverage and confounding: only 6.8% of species had complete data across all three datasets (AlphaEarth embeddings cover just 28% of genomes), and the correlations control for neither phylogenetic non-independence nor genome count per species, so shared ancestry and uneven sampling could inflate the relationships.

Read together, the projects triangulate the same claim at three scales: a single genome can carry pathways it does not need; species with more flexible (open) pangenomes range over wider niches; and communities lose the biosynthetic pathways their environment makes redundant.

## Topics

[Metabolic pathways](../topics/metabolic-pathways.md) is the connective tissue of every project — GapMind pathway completeness is the shared measurement, and the central distinction throughout is between a pathway being *present* and a pathway being *used*. [Gene fitness](../topics/gene-fitness.md) supplies the experimental ground truth that makes the latent-capability concept measurable, and it is also the home of the Black Queen Hypothesis that motivates the loss of costly, environmentally-redundant functions.

[Pangenome architecture](../topics/pangenome-architecture.md) appears wherever the work moves from single genomes to species: pangenome openness, core-genome fraction, and accessory-gene content are the variables that link genome flexibility to adaptation. [Microbial ecotypes](../topics/microbial-ecotypes.md) captures the finding that within-species metabolic heterogeneity is widespread and sometimes tracks isolation environment, suggesting that a "species" often comprises distinct metabolic strategies. [Environment biogeography](../topics/environment-biogeography.md) is where the work tests whether ecological context — ecosystem type, niche breadth, satellite-derived environmental diversity — predicts metabolic content, and it is the source of the strongest single predictor (niche breadth) in the cross-species analysis. [Functional dark matter](../topics/functional-dark-matter.md) enters through the recognition that genomic potential is not expression: pathways present in DNA may be silent, a gap that transcriptomic data would be needed to close.
