# Justin Reese

Justin Reese is a contributor to this research observatory whose work spans three interrelated projects investigating how bacterial pangenome structure shapes functional gene composition, antimicrobial resistance (AMR) co-mobilization with prophages, and within-species ecotype differentiation. This page exists to give a scientist-engineer new to the niche a synthesized entry point to Reese's project footprint and the topical threads connecting his contributions.

## Overview

Reese's work sits at the intersection of [pangenome architecture](../topics/pangenome-architecture.md), [mobile genetic elements](../topics/mobile-genetic-elements.md), and [microbial ecotypes](../topics/microbial-ecotypes.md). Across his three projects, a common methodological theme is large-scale comparative genomics: all analyses draw on the BERDL pangenome database, which contains tens of thousands of species-level pangenomes built from hundreds of thousands of genomes, enabling statistical patterns to be detected at a scale not previously attempted.

A foundational backdrop for two of his projects is the "two-speed genome" concept — the empirically established pattern in which a bacterium's novel (accessory) genes are enriched for mobile-element functions (COG category L: replication, recombination, and repair machinery, including transposases and integrases; +10.88% relative to core genes) and defense functions (COG category V: +2.83%) [\[1\]](#references). This pattern had been established across 32 species and 9 phyla in a prior cog_analysis project, but whether it scales with how "open" a species' pangenome is — that is, how rapidly it continues to acquire new genes — remained untested [\[2\]](#references).

## Projects

### Ecotype Functional Differentiation

The [`ecotype_functional_differentiation`](../projects/ecotype-functional-differentiation.md) project asks whether within-species gene-content subpopulations (ecotypes — strains that cluster together by which accessory genes they carry, reflecting local niche adaptation) show non-random functional differentiation. Using PCA and KMeans clustering on auxiliary gene presence/absence matrices, valid gene-content ecotypes were identified in 12 of 15 sampled bacterial species (80%), with species averaging 3.7 ecotypes each [\[3\]](#references). The finding that such clustering is widespread confirms within-species pangenome structure as a general feature of bacteria, extending prior observatory work to a broader set of phyla.

Critically, the ecotype differences are not functionally random. Chi-square and Fisher's exact tests revealed that 170 of 257 species-by-COG-category tests (66.1%) were statistically significant after multiple-testing correction, and all 12 species showed at least one significantly differentiated COG category [\[4\]](#references). The most frequently differentiated categories included amino acid metabolism (E), unknown function (S), and defense (V), each differentiated in 11 of 12 species. Furthermore, adaptive COG categories — defense, transport, secondary metabolism, cell wall — show effect sizes 2.1 times larger than housekeeping categories (translation, energy production), a highly significant difference [\[5\]](#references).

A nuance worth flagging: the two COG categories with the largest mean effect sizes were unknown function (S) and replication/mobile elements (L), meaning a large share of the niche-specific signal remains difficult to interpret biologically [\[6\]](#references). Because category L encompasses transposases, integrases, and other mobile genetic element machinery, its prominence echoes the two-speed genome pattern described above. The role of mobile elements in shaping ecotype differences connects this project directly to the [mobile genetic elements](../topics/mobile-genetic-elements.md) topic and to the ecotype-level unknowns that fall under [functional dark matter](../topics/functional-dark-matter.md).

### Openness vs Functional Composition

The [`openness_functional_composition`](../projects/openness-functional-composition.md) project is in progress — notebooks have been created but not yet executed on the BERDL JupyterHub [\[7\]](#references). Its central question is whether pangenome openness (a species-level metric capturing how many new genes appear with each additional sampled genome) predicts the magnitude of COG functional enrichment in novel versus core genes [\[8\]](#references).

The project is designed around two competing hypotheses. H1 predicts that open pangenomes show higher mobile-element (L) and defense (V) COG enrichment in novel genes, because species that accumulate novel genes more readily would also receive more mobile and defense cargo from horizontal gene transfer (HGT) [\[9\]](#references). H2 predicts that closed pangenomes — those that rarely acquire new genes — concentrate metabolic diversity (COG categories E, C, and G: amino acid, energy, and carbohydrate metabolism) in their core genomes [\[10\]](#references). The method stratifies roughly 40 species (each with at least 50 genomes) into openness quartiles and computes COG enrichment across core, auxiliary, and singleton gene clusters, drawing on BERDL pangenome tables that join openness metrics, gene-cluster classifications, and eggNOG COG categories at billion-row scale [\[11\]](#references) [\[12\]](#references).

The two possible outcomes carry distinct theoretical implications. If H1 is supported, the two-speed genome pattern would be shown to scale with genome fluidity rather than being a fixed property of HGT [\[13\]](#references). If H0 is not rejected — meaning enrichment is independent of openness — the two-speed pattern would be confirmed as truly universal, driven by the nature of HGT events rather than their frequency [\[14\]](#references). Potential confounders include genome count (more genomes yield better pangenome estimates), phylum-level effects, and genome size; the design incorporates phylogenetic controls to address these [\[15\]](#references). A prior pangenome_openness project had found no correlation between openness and eco-phylo dynamics, motivating the functional stratification approach now being taken [\[16\]](#references).

This project connects directly to the [pangenome architecture](../topics/pangenome-architecture.md) and [metabolic pathways](../topics/metabolic-pathways.md) topics, since its hypotheses concern how the metabolic content of core genomes co-varies with acquisition rate.

### Prophage-AMR Co-mobilization Atlas

The [`prophage_amr_comobilization`](../projects/prophage-amr-comobilization.md) project examines whether prophages — bacteriophage genomes that have integrated into bacterial chromosomes and may be induced to excise and replicate — facilitate the spread of AMR genes. Prophages are identified using keyword and Pfam-domain matching on Bakta gene annotations, detecting terminase large subunit genes, structural protein genes, and holin/lysin genes as markers. A pangenome-scale census across the full BERDL inventory found 83,008 AMR gene clusters and 3.47 million prophage marker clusters, with 14,669 of 27,702 species (52.9%) carrying both [\[17\]](#references). Both prophage markers and AMR genes are predominantly accessory rather than core — prophage markers are accessory in 83.8% of cases and singleton in 53.8%, while AMR genes are accessory in 69.7% — reflecting the mobile character of both [\[18\]](#references).

The co-mobilization analysis has three main hypotheses. The first (H1) asks whether gene-level proximity to prophage markers predicts AMR gene mobility. Over half (55.7%) of AMR gene instances in the top-100 AMR-burdened species reside on contigs that also carry strict prophage markers [\[19\]](#references). However, when proximity is made the test — genes within 10 positions of a prophage marker versus more distant genes — the effect is statistically significant but modest: prophage-proximal AMR genes are accessory 67.6% of the time versus 65.5% for distal ones (Fisher's exact OR=1.10, p=0.005) [\[20\]](#references). The effect is also strongly threshold-dependent: it reverses at very close range (OR=0.78 at 3 genes) and strengthens at broader distances (OR=1.28 at 50 genes), with per-species heterogeneity — only 33 of 74 testable species show OR>1 [\[21\]](#references). The most natural interpretation of the reversal at very short range is that genes immediately adjacent to phage structural genes are core phage components, not recently acquired cargo.

The second hypothesis (H2) — and the project's strongest result — concerns species-level breadth: do species with more prophages carry more diverse AMR gene repertoires? The answer is yes. Across 4,770 species, prophage marker density is strongly correlated with AMR breadth (Spearman rho=0.572, p<10^-300), with prophage density explaining roughly 30% of variance in AMR breadth [\[22\]](#references). This association persists after controlling for genome count (partial Spearman rho=0.464, p=1.0x10^-253) and is consistent across all five major phyla [\[23\]](#references). The finding extends prior work at far larger scale: where Rendueles et al. (2018) showed the prophage-AMR correlation across roughly 100 pangenomes controlling for capsule presence, this analysis covers 4,770 species controlling for genome count [\[24\]](#references). Two non-exclusive mechanisms are consistent with this pattern: prophages directly mobilizing resistance genes via transduction (a process in which a phage accidentally packages host DNA, including AMR genes, and injects it into a new cell), or species with generally high recombination potential independently accumulating both prophages and AMR genes [\[25\]](#references).

The third hypothesis (H3) — whether prophage-proximal AMR genes carry distinct fitness costs measurable by RB-TnSeq (a pooled transposon-insertion fitness assay using randomly barcoded Tn insertions) — could not be tested. The [BERDL fitness browser](../data/kescience-fitnessbrowser.md) covers only 48 model organisms with RB-TnSeq data, which do not overlap well with the GTDB species analyzed here [\[26\]](#references). This gap is why the [gene fitness](../topics/gene-fitness.md) topic appears in Reese's footprint but remains unresolved for this project.

The scale and scope of this project — spanning 100 species, 1,953 genomes, and 36,041 AMR gene instances for gene-neighborhood analysis — constitutes the first pangenome-scale analysis of prophage-AMR gene-neighborhood co-localization [\[27\]](#references), and connects directly to the [AMR resistome](../topics/amr-resistome.md) topic page.

## Topics

Reese's work touches seven topics. [Pangenome architecture](../topics/pangenome-architecture.md) and [mobile genetic elements](../topics/mobile-genetic-elements.md) run through all three projects, since the pangenome framework — distinguishing core genes shared by all strains from accessory genes present in only some — is the analytic scaffold for every analysis. The [AMR resistome](../topics/amr-resistome.md) topic is the focus of the prophage co-mobilization project. [Microbial ecotypes](../topics/microbial-ecotypes.md) arises from the ecotype differentiation project, which shows that within-species gene-content subpopulations have non-random functional composition. [Functional dark matter](../topics/functional-dark-matter.md) is adjacent because the S (unknown function) COG category consistently appears in the most differentiated positions across both ecotype and pangenome analyses, highlighting annotation gaps in niche-specific genes. [Metabolic pathways](../topics/metabolic-pathways.md) is relevant because the openness project's H2 predicts that closed pangenomes concentrate metabolic diversity in core genes, and this remains testable. Finally, [gene fitness](../topics/gene-fitness.md) appears as a topic because the prophage co-mobilization project intended to test whether AMR gene position relative to prophages affects fitness cost — a question bridged by the [BERDL fitness browser data](../data/kescience-fitnessbrowser.md) and the [KBase KE pangenome data](../data/kbase-ke-pangenome.md) — but this remains an open direction.

## Caveats and Open Directions

Several important limitations qualify the conclusions above. Prophage detection in the co-mobilization project relies on keyword and Pfam matching on Bakta annotations rather than dedicated tools such as geNomad or PHASTER, which may introduce false positives (phage-defense systems can match some markers) and miss divergent prophages [\[28\]](#references). Replacing this with validated tool predictions is the first recommended future step [\[29\]](#references). The species-level prophage-AMR breadth correlation cannot by itself establish phage-mediated transfer causally, since species with open pangenomes may independently accumulate both [\[30\]](#references). The fitness cost hypothesis (H3) remains untestable until fitness browser coverage expands to include species also represented in the GTDB pangenome; when that coverage grows, the question can be revisited [\[31\]](#references). The openness functional composition project has not yet produced empirical results, so H1 and H2 remain unconfirmed hypotheses [\[7\]](#references). Confounders including genome count, phylum, and genome size will need to be addressed as execution proceeds [\[15\]](#references). Across all projects, the large effect sizes observed for the unknown-function (S) COG category underscore how much niche-specific biology remains unannotated — a recurring challenge in microbial ecology [\[6\]](#references).
