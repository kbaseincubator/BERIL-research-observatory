# Justin Reese

Justin Reese (ORCID 0000-0002-2170-2250) is a contributor to this compendium whose work
sits at the intersection of bacterial pangenome structure, mobile genetic elements, and
the functional organization of accessory genes. This page exists to summarize his project
and topic footprint across the wiki: it gathers the claims, findings, caveats, and open
opportunities recorded under his three projects so a reader can see, in one place, what
questions he has pursued and how confident the evidence behind them is. The recurring thread
across his work is a single comparative-genomics question asked in different guises: when a
bacterial species acquires new genes, *which* functions does it gain, and what does that tell
us about how those genes move and what they are for.

## Overview

Reese's footprint spans three projects, all built on large collections of bacterial genomes
and their gene-cluster (pangenome) decompositions. A **pangenome** is the union of genes
found across many genomes of a species, split into a stable *core* shared by nearly all
strains and a variable *accessory* set carried by only some. **Pangenome openness** measures
how readily a species keeps adding new genes as more genomes are sequenced; an *open*
pangenome keeps growing (high gene flux, frequent horizontal gene transfer), while a *closed*
one saturates quickly.

Three angles recur across the projects. The first asks whether **pangenome openness** predicts
the *kind* of functions a species accumulates, scored by COG categories (the Clusters of
Orthologous Groups functional scheme, e.g. category L for replication/recombination/mobile
elements, V for defense, and E/C/G for metabolism). The second asks whether **prophages** —
bacteriophage genomes integrated into the host chromosome — co-localize with antimicrobial
resistance (AMR) genes at pangenome scale, probing whether phages help mobilize resistance.
The third asks whether within-species **ecotypes** (sub-populations adapted to distinct niches)
differ in gene content in a functionally structured way. Together these projects connect to
the wiki topics of [Pangenome Architecture](../topics/pangenome-architecture.md),
[Mobile Genetic Elements](../topics/mobile-genetic-elements.md),
[Amr Resistome](../topics/amr-resistome.md),
[Microbial Ecotypes](../topics/microbial-ecotypes.md),
[Metabolic Pathways](../topics/metabolic-pathways.md),
[Functional Dark Matter](../topics/functional-dark-matter.md), and
[Gene Fitness](../topics/gene-fitness.md), and draw on the
[Kbase Ke Pangenome](../data/kbase-ke-pangenome.md) and
[Kescience Fitnessbrowser](../data/kescience-fitnessbrowser.md) data sources.

A consistent feature of this body of work is its honesty about limits. Several headline
results are correlational, one project's central hypothesis could not be tested at all for
lack of overlapping data, and another project is still in progress with no empirical results
yet. Those caveats are surfaced throughout rather than buried.

## Projects

### Prophage–AMR co-mobilization

The largest completed effort, `prophage_amr_comobilization`, is described as the first
pangenome-scale analysis of prophage–AMR gene-neighborhood co-localization, spanning 100
species, 1,953 genomes, and 36,041 AMR gene instances. A broader census across the genome
collection identified 83,008 AMR gene clusters and millions of prophage marker clusters, with
14,669 of 27,702 species carrying both — establishing that prophages and resistance genes
routinely coexist in the same genomes.

The structural findings paint a coherent picture. Both prophage markers and AMR genes are
predominantly *accessory* rather than core, with prophage markers the more strongly accessory
of the two and frequently singleton (present in just one genome) — the signature of recently
or rarely acquired DNA. At the sequence level, over half of AMR gene instances in the most
AMR-burdened species sit on contigs that also carry strict prophage markers such as terminase
(a phage DNA-packaging enzyme) and phage structural proteins, placing resistance genes in
direct physical company with phage machinery. At the species level, prophage marker density
predicts AMR breadth strongly: species with more prophage markers carry significantly broader
resistance repertoires, with prophage density explaining roughly 30% of the variance in AMR
breadth. This density effect is robust — it persists after controlling for genome count and
holds consistently across all five major phyla, arguing against a purely phylogenetic
explanation. It also extends prior capsule-pangenome work (the Rendueles line of research) by
demonstrating the prophage–AMR correlation at much larger scale and showing it survives
controlling for genome count rather than capsule presence.

The proximity (fine-scale neighborhood) evidence is weaker and more nuanced. Prophage-proximal
AMR genes are only slightly more likely to be accessory than distal ones — a statistically
significant but modest effect — and the association is threshold-dependent and heterogeneous,
absent or even reversed at very close range and strengthening at broader gene-distance
thresholds. The project is careful about interpretation: the density signal is equally
consistent with prophages directly mobilizing resistance via transduction (phage-mediated DNA
transfer) and with high-recombination species independently accumulating both prophages and
AMR genes.

The caveats are explicit. The species-level association does not prove phage-mediated AMR
transfer, because species with open pangenomes may independently accumulate both prophages and
AMR genes — correlation, not causation. Prophages were identified by keyword/Pfam matching
rather than dedicated prediction tools, which risks false positives (e.g. phage-defense
systems mistaken for prophages) and misses divergent prophages. And the fitness-cost
hypothesis could not be tested at all: the RB-TnSeq fitness browser — a resource of genome-wide
gene fitness scores derived from randomly barcoded transposon sequencing — covers only a few
dozen model organisms that do not overlap well with the GTDB pangenome species analyzed here.
The two clearest next steps follow directly from those gaps: apply dedicated prophage
predictors such as geNomad or PHASTER to replace keyword detection and reduce false positives,
and revisit the fitness-cost question as fitness-browser coverage expands, to test whether
prophage-proximal AMR genes carry distinct fitness costs.

### Openness and functional composition

The `openness_functional_composition` project asks whether pangenome openness predicts the
*magnitude* of COG functional enrichment in novel versus core genes. Its motivation comes from
two prior results. A prior `cog_analysis` project found a universal "two-speed genome" pattern
— in which the core evolves slowly and the accessory rapidly — where novel genes are markedly
enriched in mobile-element functions (COG L: +10.88%) and defense (COG V: +2.83%) across 32
species and 9 phyla, but it never tested whether that enrichment *scales* with openness.
Separately, a prior `pangenome_openness` project found no correlation between openness and
eco-phylo dynamics, which motivated stratifying by gene function as a next step.

From these, the project frames two hypotheses. H1 predicts that open pangenomes show higher
mobile-element (L) and defense (V) enrichment in novel genes than closed pangenomes; H2
predicts that closed pangenomes concentrate higher metabolic diversity (COG E, C, G) in core
genes. The overarching hypothesis is that species acquiring more novel genes also show stronger
mobile-element and defense enrichment, linking genome fluidity to functional specialization of
the accessory genome. Methodologically, the analysis draws openness metrics and COG annotations
from BERDL pangenome tables — joining openness metrics, gene-cluster core/auxiliary/singleton
classifications, and eggNOG COG categories at billion-row scale — and stratifies roughly 40
species (each with at least 50 genomes) into openness quartiles, computing COG enrichment per
quartile across core, auxiliary, and singleton clusters.

Crucially, this work is still in progress: notebooks have been created but not yet executed, so
no empirical enrichment results are available. The project also flags that genome count, phylum
effects, and genome size are potential confounders that must be controlled when relating
openness to COG enrichment. Two contrasting outcomes are framed as forward-looking
opportunities (and carry low confidence accordingly): if open pangenomes show stronger L/V
enrichment, the two-speed model would be extended from "universal" to "scaling with genome
fluidity"; if openness does not modulate enrichment, the two-speed pattern would be confirmed as
truly universal and driven by the *nature* of HGT events rather than their *frequency*.

### Ecotype functional differentiation

The `ecotype_functional_differentiation` project examines within-species ecotypes — sub-groups
adapted to distinct niches — and asks whether their gene-content differences are functionally
meaningful. The findings are that ecotype gene-content variation is functionally structured
rather than random, with COG categories differentiating across all sampled species, and that
such gene-content ecotypes are widespread, representing a recurring within-species pangenome
structure rather than a quirk of a few taxa. The accompanying claim is that adaptive functions
differentiate more strongly between ecotypes than housekeeping functions, though both classes
can vary. An important caveat tempers this: the strongest ecotype differences include
unknown-function and mobile-element categories, so much of the niche-specific signal remains
hard to interpret biologically — the differentiation is real but partly opaque.

## Topics

Reese's projects collectively touch most of the comparative-genomics topics in this wiki.
[Pangenome Architecture](../topics/pangenome-architecture.md) is the connective tissue — core
versus accessory structure, openness, and the two-speed genome appear in all three projects.
[Mobile Genetic Elements](../topics/mobile-genetic-elements.md) feature heavily through
prophages, transduction, and the COG L enrichment of novel genes, and they link directly to the
[Amr Resistome](../topics/amr-resistome.md) via the prophage–AMR co-mobilization question.
[Microbial Ecotypes](../topics/microbial-ecotypes.md) anchor the ecotype project and recur in
the openness work's eco-phylo motivation. [Metabolic Pathways](../topics/metabolic-pathways.md)
enter through hypothesis H2's prediction about metabolic COG categories concentrating in closed
pangenomes' core. [Functional Dark Matter](../topics/functional-dark-matter.md) — genes of
unknown function — surfaces as both a methodological hazard (unknown-function categories driving
ecotype signals) and a recurring fraction of the novel-gene pool. Finally,
[Gene Fitness](../topics/gene-fitness.md) is the topic where the work is currently blocked:
testing whether prophage-proximal AMR genes carry fitness costs awaits broader RB-TnSeq
coverage from the [Kescience Fitnessbrowser](../data/kescience-fitnessbrowser.md), while the
pangenome analyses themselves rest on the [Kbase Ke Pangenome](../data/kbase-ke-pangenome.md)
data.
