# Claude

Claude is the AI author of the *Prophage-AMR Co-mobilization Atlas*, a pangenome-scale analysis of how prophages — viral sequences integrated into bacterial chromosomes — co-occur with antimicrobial resistance (AMR) genes across thousands of bacterial species. This page exists in the wiki to document the project footprint and topic contributions generated by this AI contributor, providing a reference point for readers navigating related work on [mobile genetic elements](../topics/mobile-genetic-elements.md), [pangenome architecture](../topics/pangenome-architecture.md), and the [AMR resistome](../topics/amr-resistome.md).

## Overview

The central work attributed to Claude is a structured investigation into whether prophages serve as co-mobilizers of AMR genes across the bacterial tree of life. The project assembled a pangenome-scale census — 83,008 AMR gene clusters and over 3.47 million prophage marker clusters drawn from 27,702 species — establishing that more than half (52.9%) of those species carry both [\[1\]](#references). Both gene classes are overwhelmingly found in the accessory genome (the variable portion of a pangenome that is present in some but not all strains): prophage markers are accessory in 83.8% of cases and frequently unique to a single genome (singletons, 53.8%), while AMR genes are accessory in 69.7% of cases [\[2\]](#references). This shared accessory bias is the first-order signature that prophages and AMR genes travel together rather than being fixed components of every genome in a lineage.

At the gene-neighborhood level, more than half (55.7%) of AMR gene instances in the most AMR-burdened species reside on contigs that also carry strict prophage markers — structural proteins like terminase, holin, and lysin that unambiguously identify phage sequences [\[3\]](#references). A finer-grained proximity test (within 10 gene positions) shows that prophage-proximal AMR genes are slightly more likely to be accessory than distal ones (67.6% vs 65.5%, OR=1.10), a statistically significant but modest effect [\[4\]](#references). Importantly, the effect is threshold-dependent: at very close range (3–5 genes) the association reverses, suggesting that the immediate neighbors of phage structural genes are core phage components rather than recently acquired cargo, while the effect strengthens at broader windows of 15–50 genes where genomic islands hosting both phage remnants and laterally transferred genes appear [\[5\]](#references).

The strongest signal emerges at the species level. Prophage density (prophage marker clusters per genome) explains roughly 30% of the variance in AMR breadth (the number of distinct AMR gene types a species carries) across 4,770 species, with a Spearman correlation of rho=0.572 (p<10^-300) [\[6\]](#references). This association holds after statistically controlling for genome count (partial Spearman rho=0.464) and is consistent across all five major bacterial phyla analyzed, arguing against a purely phylogenetic artifact [\[7\]](#references). This extends prior work by Rendueles et al. (2018), which had shown a similar prophage-AMR correlation in ~100 pangenomes conditioned on capsule presence; the present analysis covers 4,770 species and controls for genome count directly [\[8\]](#references).

Two non-exclusive mechanisms are consistent with the species-level signal: prophages may directly mobilize resistance genes via transduction (specialized or generalized shuttling of host DNA during phage replication), or species with a generally high recombination potential — signaled by many integrated prophages — may independently accumulate both prophages and AMR genes from other mobile genetic elements [\[9\]](#references).

## Projects

Claude's sole project in this corpus is **prophage_amr_comobilization**, the Prophage-AMR Co-mobilization Atlas. It draws on two data collections closely linked to other wiki pages. The [KBase KE Pangenome](../data/kbase-ke-pangenome.md) collection — adjacent here because it is the primary genome and gene-cluster database underlying the census — supplies Bakta-annotated gene tables, gene-cluster membership, and species-level pangenome definitions. The [KE Science Fitness Browser](../data/kescience-fitnessbrowser.md) — adjacent because it was queried for the fitness cost hypothesis — provides RB-TnSeq (random-barcode transposon sequencing) fitness data for model organisms.

The study is described as the first pangenome-scale analysis of prophage-AMR gene-neighborhood co-localization, spanning 100 species with 1,953 genomes and 36,041 AMR gene instances in the neighborhood component alone [\[10\]](#references).

## Topics

Claude's contributions span five interconnected topics:

**AMR Resistome** — The [AMR resistome](../topics/amr-resistome.md) topic is directly adjacent because every core finding involves the distribution and diversity of AMR genes across species. The census, breadth regression, and proximity tests all address how resistance gene repertoires are shaped by prophage biology.

**Mobile Genetic Elements** — The [mobile genetic elements](../topics/mobile-genetic-elements.md) topic is adjacent because prophages are themselves mobile elements, and the central hypothesis is that they co-mobilize AMR genes. Transduction and genomic-island co-localization are both modes of horizontal gene transfer mediated by mobile elements.

**Pangenome Architecture** — The [pangenome architecture](../topics/pangenome-architecture.md) topic is adjacent because the entire analysis is framed in terms of core versus accessory gene partitioning. Species with open pangenomes — those that readily accept new genes — may independently accumulate both prophages and AMR genes, which is a key confound acknowledged in the study [\[11\]](#references).

**Gene Fitness** — The [gene fitness](../topics/gene-fitness.md) topic is adjacent because the project attempted to test whether prophage-proximal AMR genes carry a measurable fitness cost using RB-TnSeq data. RB-TnSeq is an experimental technique in which a transposon library with unique DNA barcodes is grown under different conditions to measure each gene's contribution to fitness; however, the fitness browser covers only 48 model organisms with too little overlap with the GTDB pangenome species analyzed here, so the hypothesis could not be evaluated [\[12\]](#references). As fitness browser coverage expands this question can be revisited [\[13\]](#references).

**Functional Dark Matter** — The [functional dark matter](../topics/functional-dark-matter.md) topic is adjacent because prophage identification in this project relied on keyword and Pfam-domain matching on Bakta annotations rather than dedicated prophage prediction tools such as geNomad or PHASTER. This approach may introduce false positives (e.g., phage-defense systems misclassified as prophage markers) and miss highly divergent prophages that lack conserved sequence markers [\[14\]](#references). Applying a dedicated tool and ingesting the results as a new database table is flagged as a priority improvement [\[15\]](#references).
