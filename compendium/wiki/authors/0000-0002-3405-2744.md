# William J. Riehl

## Overview

William J. Riehl is a contributor to this knowledge graph through two
closely related research projects that both ask how the structure of
microbial pangenomes shapes ecology and antibiotic resistance. A
pangenome is the union of all genes seen across the genomes of a species:
a stable **core** set carried by nearly every isolate plus a variable
**accessory** set present in only some. Riehl's work centers on a single
quantitative handle on that variability — pangenome openness, formalized
as the fraction of variable genes, `openness = (aux_genes +
singleton_genes) / total_genes`, a standard measure that independent
review judged sound. Both projects use openness as the spine connecting
metabolic capability, niche breadth, and resistance gene content.

This page exists to summarize that footprint and to be honest about its
current maturity. Both projects are early-stage research plans rather
than completed analyses, and the most important thing a reader should
take away is that the conclusions here are *hypotheses and designs*, not
results. The resistance project has zero completed analysis: all six of
its notebooks contain only skeleton code with TODO placeholders, no data
files have been generated, and no findings exist yet. The pangenome
ecology project is similarly at the proposal stage, with only its Phase 1
notebook implemented and no figures or data produced. Where this page
states a relationship — for example, that open pangenomes track
generalist lifestyles — it is reporting an intended test, not an observed
effect.

The shared substrate for both efforts is BERDL (the BER Data Lakehouse)
and its `kbase_ke_pangenome` collection, which spans 293,059 genomes
across 27,690 microbial species mapped to the GTDB r214 taxonomy. Working
at this scale is itself a stated constraint: `COUNT(*)` and `SHOW TABLES`
queries on the billion-row pangenome tables time out, so exploration must
fall back to `LIMIT`-based sampling run inside JupyterHub with Spark SQL
rather than through the REST API.

## Projects

### Pangenome pathway ecology

The pangenome ecology project tests a single central hypothesis: that
open pangenomes (high auxiliary-plus-singleton gene ratios) mark
**generalist** species with broad niche adaptation, more horizontal gene
transfer within their ecological niches, and greater metabolic pathway
diversity, while closed pangenomes (low variable ratios) mark
**specialists** with reduced gene acquisition and conserved core
metabolic pathways. The design computes openness distributions and
[metabolic pathway](../topics/metabolic-pathways.md) coverage across all
27,690 species, drawing together four kinds of BERDL tables: the
pangenome gene-cluster data, GapMind pathway predictions, AlphaEarth
structural embeddings, and phylogenetic-tree distances. GapMind is a tool
that predicts whether a genome encodes a complete metabolic pathway; a
key caveat is that its predictions are probabilistic and vary in
confidence rather than being experimental, so metabolic-completeness
inferences carry real uncertainty.

A second, more speculative test asks whether open pangenomes also show
greater *within-species structural diversity*, by correlating AlphaEarth
embedding distances against openness. This connects to
[environment biogeography](../topics/environment-biogeography.md), since
AlphaEarth embeddings encode structural/environmental signal. Coverage is
the main limit: AlphaEarth embeddings span only about 28% of genomes, so
structural-distance analyses must be filtered to well-covered species and
may be biased by underrepresented isolate types. The design also takes
phylogeny seriously — because closely related species are not independent
data points, it relates openness to evolutionary distance using
phylogenetic independent contrasts (PIC) or PGLS so that any association
is not just an artifact of shared ancestry. Two clear next steps are
flagged: adding multiple-testing correction (Bonferroni or FDR) when
screening many openness-pathway correlations, and simply executing the
Phase 1 notebook on BERDL JupyterHub to generate baseline data, surface
bugs, and seed the later phases.

### Resistance hotspots

The resistance project asks which microbial species and environments
harbor the highest concentrations of antibiotic resistance genes (ARGs),
and whether resistance accumulation can be predicted from phylogenetic
and ecological features. Its central planned analysis is to map ARGs
across the full 293,059-genome, 27,690-species pangenome and test whether
resistance genes sit preferentially in the core or the accessory genome,
relating openness to ARG diversity. The reusable hypothesis worth
pursuing is that open-pangenome species (high accessory-to-core ratio)
carry higher ARG diversity than closed-pangenome species, which would
make resistance accumulation statistically testable.

This project is candid about methodological and infrastructure
roadblocks. The planned approach of identifying ARGs by keyword-matching
gene descriptions is underspecified and expected to produce high
false-positive and false-negative rates with no validation strategy
defined; a more robust alternative would be sequence-similarity search
(BLAST/DIAMOND) against CARD protein sequences using an identity
threshold. Validation against the BERDL API exposed harder blockers: the
`eggnog_mapper_annotations` table times out over REST and the
`orthogroup` table does not exist or is inaccessible. The recommended
workaround is to join eggNOG functional annotations on `gene_cluster_id`
(not `gene_id`) using the species-specific `gene_cluster` table with
species-level grouping. Environmental hotspot analysis faces the same
AlphaEarth coverage ceiling (28.4% of genomes) compounded by sparse NCBI
environment metadata stored awkwardly in entity-attribute-value form. A
planned extension cross-references ARG-carrying genes against
`kescience_fitnessbrowser` transposon-mutant fitness data to weigh the
fitness trade-offs of carrying resistance — but this is itself fraught,
because the Fitness Browser uses different model organisms (such as *E.
coli* K-12) and gene-ID schemes than the wild-type pangenome strains, so
the mapping is not straightforward.

## Topics

Riehl's two projects converge on
[pangenome architecture](../topics/pangenome-architecture.md), which is
the connective tissue of essentially every statement here: openness is
the operational metric, and both the ecology and the resistance questions
are framed as core-versus-accessory contrasts at 27,690-species scale.
From that hub the work branches into several adjacent topics.

The [AMR resistome](../topics/amr-resistome.md) is the focus of the
resistance project — mapping ARGs, asking where they concentrate, and
testing whether openness predicts their diversity. The
[generalist-specialist hypothesis](../topics/microbial-ecotypes.md)
(microbial ecotypes) frames the ecology project's central claim, linking
pangenome openness to niche breadth.
[Metabolic pathways](../topics/metabolic-pathways.md) enter through
GapMind coverage as the phenotypic readout of generalist versus
specialist metabolism, and
[environment biogeography](../topics/environment-biogeography.md) supplies
the AlphaEarth structural/environmental embeddings used to test
within-species diversity and to locate resistance hotspots.

Two further topics appear as mechanism and method.
[Mobile genetic elements](../topics/mobile-genetic-elements.md) and
horizontal gene transfer are the proposed driver of open-pangenome
accessory gene flux. [Gene fitness](../topics/gene-fitness.md) is the lens
of the planned fitness-trade-off analysis, tempered by the cross-organism
gene-ID mapping caveat above.
[Functional dark matter](../topics/functional-dark-matter.md) — the large
fraction of microbial genes lacking confident functional assignment —
surfaces wherever annotation is the bottleneck, including the probabilistic
nature of GapMind predictions and the false-positive risk of keyword-based
ARG calls. Across all of these, the recurring theme is the same: a
well-specified set of questions and metrics whose answers still await
execution.
