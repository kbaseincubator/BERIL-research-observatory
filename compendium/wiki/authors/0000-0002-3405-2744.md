# William J. Riehl

William J. Riehl (ORCID 0000-0002-3405-2744) is the author of two projects in this corpus that both aim to understand microbial pangenome structure at planetary scale. This page collects Riehl's project and topic footprint so readers can see the intellectual threads connecting his work and the current state of execution.

## Overview

Both of Riehl's projects share a common substrate: the BERDL `kbase_ke_pangenome` database, which holds 293,059 genomes spanning 27,690 microbial species classified according to the Genome Taxonomy Database (GTDB release r214) [\[1\]](#references). A pangenome is the union of all genes found across sequenced strains of a species; it is conventionally divided into a "core genome" (genes present in nearly all strains) and an "accessory genome" (genes found in only some strains, including singletons unique to one strain). "Pangenome openness" measures the accessory fraction: the ratio (auxiliary_genes + singleton_genes) / total_genes. A high ratio indicates an open pangenome that keeps acquiring new genes from the environment, while a low ratio indicates a closed pangenome with a stable, conserved gene set [\[2\]](#references).

The analytical infrastructure used across both projects is BERDL (KBase BER Data Lakehouse), a Delta Lakehouse that exposes this pangenome data through Spark SQL. Queries against billion-row tables cannot use simple REST-API count queries and must instead be run inside the BERDL JupyterHub environment using Spark SQL with LIMIT-based sampling [\[3\]](#references).

At the time of review, neither project had produced executed outputs: both remain at the proposal-and-skeleton-notebook stage with no data files or figures generated [\[4\]](#references) [\[5\]](#references).

## Projects

### Pangenome Openness, Metabolic Pathways, and Phylogenetic Distances ([`pangenome_pathway_ecology`](../projects/pangenome-pathway-ecology.md))

This project investigates whether pangenome openness correlates with metabolic pathway completeness, protein structural diversity, and ecological specialization across all 27,690 species in the resource [\[6\]](#references). The central hypothesis — developed in the planning documents — is that open pangenomes mark generalist species with broader niche adaptation and greater metabolic flexibility, while closed pangenomes mark specialists that maintain a conserved core of metabolic pathways [\[7\]](#references). More specifically, open pangenomes are predicted to show greater horizontal gene transfer within ecological niches [\[8\]](#references), while closed pangenomes reflect reduced gene acquisition rates and core pathway conservation [\[9\]](#references).

The planned data sources are the BERDL `pangenome`, `gapmind_pathways`, `alphaearth_embeddings_all_years`, and `phylogenetic_tree_distance_pairs` tables [\[10\]](#references). GapMind is a metabolic pathway completion scoring system that predicts which biosynthetic routes are present in a genome; its predictions are probabilistic rather than experimentally confirmed, which limits the certainty of any metabolic-completeness inferences drawn from the analysis [\[11\]](#references). AlphaEarth is a protein structural embedding model; a planned phase tests whether open pangenomes show greater within-species structural diversity by correlating pairwise AlphaEarth embedding distances with openness [\[12\]](#references). A critical caveat is that AlphaEarth embeddings currently cover only about 28% of genomes in the collection, so any structural-distance analysis must filter to species with sufficient coverage and may be biased by underrepresented isolate types [\[13\]](#references).

To avoid spurious correlations driven by evolutionary relatedness, the design calls for phylogenetic signal control using phylogenetic independent contrasts (PIC) or phylogenetic generalized least squares (PGLS) — statistical methods that partial out the shared ancestry component so that observed associations between openness and pathway diversity can be attributed to ecology rather than phylogeny [\[14\]](#references). Reviews noted that the analysis should also apply multiple-testing correction (Bonferroni or FDR adjustment) when testing multiple openness-pathway correlations simultaneously [\[15\]](#references). The most immediate next step is executing the Phase 1 notebook on BERDL JupyterHub to generate baseline data and expose any query bugs before later phases begin [\[16\]](#references).

### Antibiotic Resistance Hotspots in Microbial Pangenomes ([`resistance_hotspots`](../projects/resistance-hotspots.md))

This project asks which microbial species and ecological environments harbor the highest concentrations of antibiotic resistance genes (ARGs) and whether resistance accumulation can be predicted from phylogenetic and ecological features [\[17\]](#references). The planned six-phase pipeline moves from data exploration through ARG identification, distribution analysis, pangenome characterization, fitness trade-off analysis, and visualization, using the same `kbase_ke_pangenome` collection as the pangenome ecology project [\[18\]](#references).

A key analytical question is whether ARGs cluster in the core or the accessory genome. The proposal plans to test whether species with open pangenomes carry higher ARG diversity than species with closed pangenomes, which would parallel the generalist-specialist hypothesis from the companion project [\[19\]](#references). A suggested refinement is to crystallize this into an explicit testable hypothesis — "species with open pangenomes will have higher ARG diversity than species with closed pangenomes" — rather than leaving the research question purely exploratory [\[20\]](#references).

Several technical obstacles were identified in review. The project's planned ARG identification by keyword matching of gene descriptions is underspecified and is expected to produce high false-positive and false-negative rates, with no validation strategy defined for confirming true resistance genes [\[21\]](#references). A stronger approach would use sequence similarity searches (BLAST or DIAMOND) against CARD (the Comprehensive Antibiotic Resistance Database) protein sequences, setting an identity threshold of at least 80% for close homologs [\[22\]](#references). At the data infrastructure level, the `eggnog_mapper_annotations` table times out via the REST API and the `orthogroup` table does not exist, blocking the planned functional annotation queries; the workaround is to join against the `gene_cluster` table on `gene_cluster_id` and run inside the JupyterHub Spark environment [\[23\]](#references) [\[24\]](#references).

Phase 5 of the pipeline proposes cross-referencing ARG-containing genes against the `kescience_fitnessbrowser` dataset, which contains transposon-mutant (RB-TnSeq) fitness measurements — experiments where random-barcode transposon insertions are used to measure the growth defect of knocking out each gene under hundreds of conditions. The idea is to estimate the fitness trade-off of carrying resistance genes. However, a critical mapping problem exists: the Fitness Browser covers model organisms such as E. coli K12, while the pangenome collection uses wild-type environmental strains, and gene ID schemes differ between the two databases [\[25\]](#references). The plan to cross-reference ARG fitness costs [\[26\]](#references) therefore requires either restricting the analysis to the overlapping organisms or treating Phase 5 as deferred future work. An additional limitation for the environmental hotspot analysis is that NCBI environment metadata is sparse and stored in an entity-attribute-value (EAV) format, making filtering by ecological habitat more complex than a simple column filter [\[27\]](#references).

## Topics

Riehl's work touches a dense cluster of interconnected topics. The [Pangenome Architecture](../topics/pangenome-architecture.md) thread is central to both projects: openness metrics, core-vs-accessory gene partitioning, and the correlation of structural diversity with pangenome flexibility run through every phase. Because open pangenomes are hypothesized to accumulate genes via horizontal transfer, the work is adjacent to [Mobile Genetic Elements](../topics/mobile-genetic-elements.md), which is the mechanism by which new accessory genes enter a pangenome across species boundaries.

The metabolic dimension links to [Metabolic Pathways](../topics/metabolic-pathways.md): GapMind pathway completeness is used as a readout of metabolic repertoire, making pathway prediction a core analytical layer. The question of whether pangenome structure predicts ecological niche connects to [Microbial Ecotypes](../topics/microbial-ecotypes.md) and [Environment Biogeography](../topics/environment-biogeography.md), since the generalist-specialist axis is inherently an ecological classification and the planned environmental filtering phases in both projects aim to map genome content patterns onto habitat types.

The resistance hotspots project brings in [Amr Resistome](../topics/amr-resistome.md), the full inventory of antibiotic resistance determinants in a microbial community, and its connection to [Gene Fitness](../topics/gene-fitness.md) via the planned fitness trade-off analysis. Finally, the annotation challenge in the resistance project — where many gene descriptions are uninformative or missing and the `eggnog_mapper_annotations` table is inaccessible — connects to [Functional Dark Matter](../topics/functional-dark-matter.md), the large fraction of microbial genes whose functions are unknown or cannot be reliably inferred from sequence homology alone.
