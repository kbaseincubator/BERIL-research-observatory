# Janaka N. Edirisinghe

Janaka N. Edirisinghe is a contributor whose work in this corpus centers on discovering and resolving metabolic annotation gaps in bacterial genomes. This page exists in the wiki because Edirisinghe is the primary author of the **annotation_gap_discovery** project, which produced most of the cross-project findings tracked here around [metabolic pathways](../topics/metabolic-pathways.md), [functional dark matter](../topics/functional-dark-matter.md), and [gene fitness](../topics/gene-fitness.md) data integration.

## Overview

The annotation_gap_discovery project tackles a fundamental problem in systems biology: genome-scale metabolic models built from automated annotations are incomplete, and the missing reactions — called annotation gaps — cannot be filled by any single computational method alone. Edirisinghe's approach assembles five independent lines of evidence and triangulates them to nominate candidate genes for reactions that the model requires but the genome annotation does not supply.

The method begins by constructing draft metabolic models for 14 bacteria using ModelSEED (a platform that automatically builds genome-scale metabolic models from RAST genome annotations) and testing them against phenotypic growth data from the Fitness Browser (a database of RB-TnSeq gene fitness scores across hundreds of growth conditions; RB-TnSeq, or randomly barcoded transposon sequencing, measures how much each gene contributes to growth under each condition). Flux balance analysis (FBA) — a constraint-based mathematical method for predicting whether a metabolic network can support growth — was run across 574 organism–carbon source combinations. Baseline accuracy was 42.5%, but this headline number conceals a striking asymmetry: recall was 86.5% while precision was only 42.5%, because draft models predicted growth on carbon sources the organisms cannot actually use [\[1\]](#references). This false-positive burden drove a targeted gapfilling step — adding reactions needed to reproduce observed growth in false-negative cases — which inserted 219 reactions across 38 cases.

The core innovation is then to assign candidate genes to those 201 gapfilled enzymatic reactions by cross-referencing five evidence streams: EC-number matching against Fitness Browser gene annotations via pangenome gene clusters, Bakta alternative annotations, RB-TnSeq gene fitness scores, BLAST homology against Swiss-Prot exemplars, and GapMind pathway completeness predictions [\[2\]](#references). Leave-one-out cross-validation showed that each stream contributes uniquely, so no single data type is sufficient on its own [\[3\]](#references). BLAST homology (run via DIAMOND against 328 reviewed bacterial sequences) was the strongest single stream at 34.8% resolution, but the full integrated pipeline reached 47.8% — 13 percentage points higher [\[4\]](#references). Overall, 96 of 201 gapfilled reaction–organism pairs received candidate gene assignments with tiered confidence scoring [\[5\]](#references).

## Projects

The single project represented here is **annotation_gap_discovery**. Its central contribution — the first systematic integration of five evidence types to resolve metabolic annotation gaps across multiple organisms simultaneously — sets it apart from prior single-organism or single-method approaches [\[2\]](#references).

Within this project, the most consistently resolved reactions were two sequential steps in branched-chain amino acid (BCAA) biosynthesis. Reaction rxn02185 (2-acetolactate pyruvate-lyase, EC 2.2.1.6) and rxn03436 (acetohydroxy acid isomeroreductase, EC 1.1.1.86) were each resolved at high confidence in 9 of the 14 organisms [\[6\]](#references). The co-resolution of adjacent pathway steps validates the triangulation logic: once one step's gene is identified, the flanking step is typically recoverable through pangenome co-occurrence. BLAST hits meeting the high-confidence threshold (≥30% identity, ≥70% coverage, e-value ≤1e-10) clustered precisely in these BCAA and polyamine metabolism reactions [\[7\]](#references).

The 44 high-confidence assignments from this project are considered directly testable: targeted gene knockouts (e.g., CRISPRi-based silencing on specific carbon sources) could confirm or refute each candidate [\[8\]](#references). The GapMind tool (a curated database of carbon and amino acid utilization pathways) partially corroborated the gapfilling conclusions, flagging pathways as incomplete for many of the same carbon sources where ModelSEED required gapfilling, though exact concordance was limited because GapMind reports pathway-level step counts rather than individual step identities [\[9\]](#references).

The project also drew on three data collections that are adjacent in this wiki: [Kescience Fitnessbrowser](../data/kescience-fitnessbrowser.md) for per-gene, per-condition fitness scores; [Kbase Ke Pangenome](../data/kbase-ke-pangenome.md) for pangenome gene clusters and EC/KEGG annotations; and [Kbase Msd Biochemistry](../data/kbase-msd-biochemistry.md) for reaction definitions and stoichiometry. All three were essential inputs to the evidence integration pipeline.

## Topics

**Functional dark matter.** "Dark reactions" are gapfilled reactions that ModelSEED has not assigned any EC number — their biochemical function is known only by stoichiometry, not by enzyme classification. Of 201 gapfilled reactions, 50 (24.9%) fell into this category, and only 8 of those 50 (16%) could be resolved, compared with 58.3% resolution for reactions with known EC numbers [\[10\]](#references). The difficulty is structural: without an EC number, neither sequence-homology searches nor functional annotation cross-referencing can proceed in the usual way. These 50 reactions, together with the 105 unresolved reaction–organism pairs that have EC numbers, represent high-value targets for computational enzyme prediction tools such as DeepEC or CLEAN and for experimental biochemistry [\[11\]](#references). This connects to the [Functional Dark Matter](../topics/functional-dark-matter.md) topic, which tracks unannotated or poorly characterized biological functions across the corpus.

**Metabolic pathways.** Resolution was not uniform across organisms. Rates ranged 3.5-fold, from 20% in *Bacteroides thetaiotaomicron* to 71% in *Klebsiella michiganensis*, tracking reference-genome annotation quality and Fitness Browser experimental coverage [\[12\]](#references). The [Metabolic Pathways](../topics/metabolic-pathways.md) topic page covers the broader landscape of pathway reconstruction across the corpus.

**Gene fitness.** The FBA gene-knockout validation attempted after annotation assignment was inconclusive: because the gapfilled reactions are themselves required to enable growth on the test carbon sources, removing any one of them prevents model growth regardless of whether it is the correct gene, making the analysis circular [\[13\]](#references). This limitation highlights the dependency between model structure and validation strategy and is discussed further on the [Gene Fitness](../topics/gene-fitness.md) topic page.

A methodological caveat worth noting: 12 of the 14 organisms studied are Proteobacteria, and the one Bacteroidetes in the set (*B. thetaiotaomicron*) had the lowest resolution rate [\[14\]](#references). The pipeline's effectiveness for phylogenetically distant lineages with more divergent metabolism remains an open question, and extending the study to organisms outside Proteobacteria is a stated future direction.
