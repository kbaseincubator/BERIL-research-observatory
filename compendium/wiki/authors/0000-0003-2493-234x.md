# Janaka N. Edirisinghe

Janaka N. Edirisinghe is a contributor whose work in this compendium centers on a single, focused effort: the `annotation_gap_discovery` project, which asks how to fill the "missing enzyme" problem in bacterial metabolic models. When a draft genome-scale model is built automatically, the reconstruction software almost always has to invent reactions — a step called gapfilling — so that the model can simulate growth at all. Those invented reactions have no gene assigned to them: the chemistry is needed, but the protein that carries it out is unknown. This page exists to summarize Edirisinghe's project and topic footprint, gathering the findings, caveats, and open opportunities that emerge from trying to put genes back onto those orphan reactions.

## Overview

The throughline of Edirisinghe's contributions is **evidence triangulation** for metabolic annotation gaps. Rather than trust any one computational signal, the work integrates five independent evidence types — gapfilling, gene fitness data, pangenome occurrence, GapMind pathway predictions, and BLAST homology — to nominate candidate genes for reactions that draft models added without supporting annotation. The project frames this five-way integration as a first-of-its-kind approach, claiming to be the first systematic combination of all five streams to resolve gaps across many organisms at once, though that novelty claim is held at only medium confidence and should be read as the authors' own framing rather than an external verdict.

The headline result is that the integrated pipeline resolved 47.8% (96 of 201) of gapfilled enzymatic reaction-organism pairs by attaching candidate genes, comfortably clearing the project's pre-registered 30% target. Crucially, this is more than any single line of evidence could achieve on its own: BLAST homology alone reached 34.8%, and no individual stream broke 35%, so the full integration added roughly 13 percentage points of resolution on top of the best single signal. A leave-one-out cross-validation reinforces this, showing that each evidence stream contributes something unique and that no single data type is sufficient by itself. Several findings here connect to the broader topic of [Functional Dark Matter](../topics/functional-dark-matter.md) — genes and reactions whose chemical role is real but molecularly unidentified — and to [Metabolic Pathways](../topics/metabolic-pathways.md), the reconstructed reaction networks the whole exercise is trying to complete.

The starting point that motivated all of this was a baseline flux-balance-analysis sweep across 574 organism-carbon-source combinations. Flux balance analysis (FBA) simulates whether a metabolic model can produce biomass on a given medium. That baseline reached only 42.5% accuracy: recall was high (86.5%) but precision was low (42.5%), the signature of overly permissive draft models that say "yes, it grows" far too often. Reducing that false-positive rate by correctly grounding gapfilled reactions in real genes is exactly the problem the rest of the project tackles.

## Projects

### annotation_gap_discovery

Edirisinghe's work in this compendium is entirely within the [annotation_gap_discovery](../topics/metabolic-pathways.md) effort, and the findings cluster into a few coherent stories.

**Where resolution succeeds, and where it fails.** Not all gaps are equally tractable. Reactions that already carry a known EC number (an Enzyme Commission identifier that names the chemistry) were resolved 58.3% of the time, but EC-less "dark reactions" — 50 of 201, about a quarter of the set — were resolved only 16% of the time, making them the single hardest class of annotation gap. Resolution also varied 3.5-fold across organisms, ranging from 20% in *Bacteroides thetaiotaomicron* to 71% in *Klebsiella michiganensis*. That spread tracks two practical factors: how well the reference genome was annotated, and how much coverage the organism has in the Fitness Browser, the experimental gene-fitness resource that supplies one of the five evidence streams (see [Kescience Fitnessbrowser](../data/kescience-fitnessbrowser.md) and the [Gene Fitness](../topics/gene-fitness.md) topic).

**Which evidence corroborates which.** The streams complement rather than duplicate one another. A DIAMOND BLAST search against 328 Swiss-Prot exemplar proteins returned 154 hits, with the strongest matches (>=30% identity, >=70% coverage, e-value <=1e-10) concentrating in branched-chain amino acid biosynthesis and polyamine metabolism. GapMind, a tool that scores the completeness of catabolic pathways, partially corroborated the gapfilling signal by independently flagging many of the same carbon sources as incomplete — though its coarse, pathway-level granularity limited how tightly the two could be matched. The most convincing internal validation came from two sequential branched-chain amino acid biosynthesis reactions, rxn02185 and rxn03436, each resolved with high confidence in 9 of 14 organisms; because these two steps lie on the same pathway, their independent co-resolution acts as a built-in consistency check on the triangulation logic.

**Honest limits.** Two caveats deserve emphasis. First, the attempt to validate gene-reaction assignments with FBA gene-knockout simulations was inconclusive, and for a structural reason: the models cannot grow on carbon-source minimal media *without* the gapfilled reactions in the first place, so knocking out a single gene tied to one of those reactions makes the test circular. Computational knockout could not cleanly confirm the assignments. Second, the organism panel is phylogenetically narrow — 12 of the 14 organisms are Proteobacteria, and the lone Bacteroidetes representative (*B. thetaiotaomicron*) had the lowest resolution rate of all. That pattern suggests the method may weaken for clades distant from the well-studied Proteobacteria, so the 47.8% headline should not be assumed to generalize across the bacterial tree.

**What comes next.** The project ends with concrete, testable proposals. The 44 high-confidence gene-reaction assignments are directly amenable to targeted knockout experiments such as CRISPRi, with rxn02185 and rxn03436 across the 9 organisms named as priority validation targets. Separately, the 50 EC-less dark reactions and the 105 pairs that stayed unresolved are flagged as high-value targets for computational enzyme-prediction tools and for direct experimental enzyme characterization — precisely the kind of unidentified chemistry that [Functional Dark Matter](../topics/functional-dark-matter.md) is about.

## Topics

Three topics anchor Edirisinghe's footprint in this compendium.

- **[Functional Dark Matter](../topics/functional-dark-matter.md)** is the conceptual heart of the work: reactions and genes whose biochemical role is real but whose molecular identity is unknown. The dark-reactions findings — the 16% resolution rate, the high-value-target designation — live squarely in this space, and the difficulty of resolving EC-less reactions is essentially a measurement of how stubborn this dark matter is.

- **[Metabolic Pathways](../topics/metabolic-pathways.md)** is the substrate being repaired. Every gapfilled reaction sits in a reconstructed network, and signals like GapMind's pathway-completeness scoring and the branched-chain amino acid biosynthesis chain are interpreted at the pathway level. The biochemistry underlying these reactions draws on resources such as [Kbase Msd Biochemistry](../data/kbase-msd-biochemistry.md).

- **[Gene Fitness](../topics/gene-fitness.md)** supplies one of the five evidence streams. Experimental fitness data from the Fitness Browser links genes to the conditions where they matter, and the per-organism resolution rates depend in part on how much fitness coverage each organism has. Pangenome occurrence — gene presence-and-absence across related genomes, drawn from resources like [Kbase Ke Pangenome](../data/kbase-ke-pangenome.md) — provides a complementary comparative-genomics signal in the same integration.
