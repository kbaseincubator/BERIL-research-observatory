# Paramvir S. Dehal

## Overview

Paramvir S. Dehal is a contributor to the BERIL compendium, credited on a broad sweep of computational microbial-genomics projects. This page exists to give a single map of that footprint: which research questions the projects ask, which data resources they draw on, and which recurring themes tie them together. The work spans 39 projects and touches twelve topic areas, but it is unusually coherent. Almost every project is an attempt to connect three kinds of evidence about bacterial genes -- how *important* a gene is (its measured fitness contribution), how *conserved* it is (its distribution across a pangenome), and where the organism that carries it *lives* (its ecological niche) -- and to ask what those three axes reveal when read against one another.

Concretely, that means leaning on a small number of large data collections. The [Kescience Fitnessbrowser](../data/kescience-fitnessbrowser.md) supplies genome-wide gene-fitness measurements from RB-TnSeq (random-barcode transposon sequencing, in which a pooled library of insertion mutants is grown under many conditions and each mutant's abundance change reports the gene's fitness cost). The [Kbase Ke Pangenome](../data/kbase-ke-pangenome.md) supplies gene-cluster conservation across tens of thousands of species -- the basis for calling a gene *core* (present in essentially all strains) versus *accessory*. Niche and biogeography come from [Enigma Coral](../data/enigma-coral.md) field communities, [Nmdc Arkin](../data/nmdc-arkin.md) metadata, and satellite-imagery embeddings, while [Kbase Uniref](../data/kbase-uniref.md), [Kbase Msd Biochemistry](../data/kbase-msd-biochemistry.md), and [Phagefoundry](../data/phagefoundry.md) supply annotation, biochemistry, and mobile-element context. The topic footprint follows directly: [Gene Fitness](../topics/gene-fitness.md), [Pangenome Architecture](../topics/pangenome-architecture.md), [Functional Dark Matter](../topics/functional-dark-matter.md), [Metabolic Pathways](../topics/metabolic-pathways.md), [Amr Resistome](../topics/amr-resistome.md), [Metal Resistance](../topics/metal-resistance.md), [Microbial Ecotypes](../topics/microbial-ecotypes.md), [Environment Biogeography](../topics/environment-biogeography.md), [Mobile Genetic Elements](../topics/mobile-genetic-elements.md), [Subsurface Genomics](../topics/subsurface-genomics.md), [Microbiome Engineering](../topics/microbiome-engineering.md), and the [Adp1 Model System](../topics/adp1-model-system.md).

A reader should treat this page as an index with interpretation, not as a list of results. The sections below group the projects by question, draw out the threads that connect them, and -- importantly -- carry forward the many caveats the projects themselves flag, because a recurring feature of this body of work is its willingness to report null results, statistical artifacts, and the limits of the underlying data honestly.

## Projects

### Connecting fitness to conservation

The conceptual spine of the contributor's work is the link between how important a gene is in the lab and how widely it is conserved in nature. Several projects build this bridge from different angles and largely agree. The synthesis work joins RB-TnSeq fitness for roughly 194,000 genes across 43 bacteria to pangenome conservation across tens of thousands of species and finds a *gradient*: essential genes are about 82% core and always-neutral genes about 66% core, so more important genes tend to be more conserved [\[1\]](#references). The companion [`fitness_effects_conservation`](../projects/fitness-effects-conservation.md) project sharpens the framing -- the relationship is a continuous gradient across the whole essential-to-neutral spectrum rather than a binary essential/non-essential split [\[2\]](#references). The most distinctive product is a "selection-signature matrix" that crosses lab fitness cost against pangenome conservation: the ~28,000 genes that are simultaneously costly to lose in the lab and conserved in the wild are read as the strongest available evidence for purifying selection acting in natural environments [\[3\]](#references).

This synthesis is presented with unusual self-scrutiny. The contributor flags a figure-versus-text discrepancy in the headline gradient (Figure 1 implies an ~8 percentage-point gap rather than the 16pp stated in prose, because percentages were computed only over successfully mapped genes), and notes that two separate essential-gene core rates (82% vs 86%) are reported without full reconciliation [\[4\]](#references). The broader caveat is that conservation is only *weakly* predicted by fitness importance, and single-gene-knockout fitness does not capture epistasis -- so the gradient, while robust, is a modest signal, not a deterministic rule.

### The core-genome burden paradox

A counterintuitive thread runs through [`core_gene_tradeoffs`](../projects/core-gene-tradeoffs.md), [`costly_dispensable_genes`](../projects/costly-dispensable-genes.md), and the fitness-distribution analyses. The "streamlining" model predicts that accessory genes are the metabolic burden a cell would shed. The data point the other way: core genes are *more* likely than accessory genes to show a positive fitness effect when deleted (i.e., to look costly under lab conditions), contradicting streamlining [\[5\]](#references). Motility is the canonical example -- flagellar machinery is energetically expensive and looks like dead weight in a shake flask, yet it is conserved because chemotaxis matters in real environments [\[6\]](#references). The resolution offered is that lab "burden" is condition-dependent, and natural selection maintains many genes the lab penalizes.

The flip side is the accessory genome. [`costly_dispensable_genes`](../projects/costly-dispensable-genes.md) characterizes genes that are both costly in the lab and dispensable in the pangenome, finding them poorly annotated, taxonomically restricted, singleton-enriched, and short [\[7\]](#references) -- the hallmarks of recently acquired DNA. The interpretation is that these are "genomic debris" of horizontal gene transfer: insertion sequences, prophage remnants, transposases, and defense systems [\[8\]](#references), which links this work to [Mobile Genetic Elements](../topics/mobile-genetic-elements.md). The caveats are appropriately structural: core genes *cannot* be singletons by construction, so part of the singleton enrichment is mechanical rather than biological.

### Antimicrobial resistance across the pangenome

A large AMR cluster of projects extends the fitness-and-conservation logic to the resistome -- the full complement of resistance genes ([Amr Resistome](../topics/amr-resistome.md)). The [`amr_pangenome_atlas`](../projects/amr-pangenome-atlas.md) is a pan-bacterial atlas showing AMR genes are markedly depleted from the core genome (30.3% core versus a 46.8% baseline) and enriched in the accessory genome [\[9\]](#references). The [`amr_fitness_cost`](../projects/amr-fitness-cost.md) project then asks whether carrying resistance is costly and finds, perhaps surprisingly, a small *positive* fitness effect for AMR-gene knockouts under non-antibiotic conditions (pooled +0.086, with all 25 organisms agreeing in sign) [\[10\]](#references) -- and, against the expectation that recently acquired resistance should be costlier, that core (intrinsic) and accessory (acquired) AMR genes have essentially identical fitness distributions [\[11\]](#references).

Niche structure is the other half of the AMR story. The [`amr_environmental_resistome`](../projects/amr-environmental-resistome.md) project shows clinical and human-gut species carry roughly 2.5x more AMR clusters than environmental species [\[12\]](#references), and that different niches select different *strategies* -- target modification dominates in the human gut and clinic while efflux dominates in aquatic settings [\[13\]](#references). The [`amr_strain_variation`](../projects/amr-strain-variation.md) project finds AMR genes organized into ~1,517 tightly co-inherited "resistance islands" [\[14\]](#references), and [`amr_cofitness_networks`](../projects/amr-cofitness-networks.md) produces a first pan-bacterial map of AMR co-fitness neighborhoods, finding support networks that are organism-specific rather than mechanism-specific [\[15\]](#references).

The AMR conclusions come heavily caveated, and honestly so. The clinical-carries-more finding is partly confounded by NCBI sampling bias -- clinical isolates are massively overrepresented precisely because they were sequenced for their resistance [\[16\]](#references). The cost estimates rest on lab-adapted strains in which compensatory evolution may have already eroded the true cost [\[17\]](#references). These are not afterthoughts; they bound what the numbers can be read to mean.

### The Acinetobacter baylyi ADP1 model system

A focused cluster of projects builds out the [Adp1 Model System](../topics/adp1-model-system.md) -- *Acinetobacter baylyi* ADP1, a naturally transformable bacterium used to study aromatic-compound catabolism. Its value here is partly that ADP1 is absent from the Fitness Browser, making its condition-specific deletion-mutant growth data a resource unavailable elsewhere in BERDL [\[18\]](#references). The [`adp1_deletion_phenotypes`](../projects/adp1-deletion-phenotypes.md) project partitions eight carbon sources into demanding, moderate, and robust tiers (urea most demanding, with 97.9% of genes defective) [\[19\]](#references). [`adp1_triple_essentiality`](../projects/adp1-triple-essentiality.md) cross-checks flux-balance analysis (FBA, a constraint-based metabolic model that predicts growth) against experimental knockouts, finding moderate concordance (Cohen's kappa ~0.49) but a systematic blind spot for aromatic-degradation genes [\[20\]](#references).

Two mechanistic projects round this out. [`aromatic_catabolism_network`](../projects/aromatic-catabolism-network.md) shows that quinate catabolism requires a 51-gene support network organized into four subsystems beyond the core beta-ketoadipate pathway [\[21\]](#references), and [`respiratory_chain_wiring`](../projects/respiratory-chain-wiring.md) finds ADP1 carries three parallel NADH dehydrogenases with distinct, non-overlapping condition profiles, implying condition-dependent respiratory wiring [\[22\]](#references). The ADP1 work flags clear limits: 87% of the explorer's growth predictions depend on at least one gapfilled reaction, tightly coupling accuracy to gapfilling quality [\[23\]](#references), and a binary "essentiality fraction" score is explicitly warned against as a classifier because it performs worse than random against knockout truth [\[24\]](#references).

### Metals: resistance, validation, and specificity

A set of metal-focused projects ([Metal Resistance](../topics/metal-resistance.md)) builds and validates a "Metal Fitness Atlas" of genome-based metal-tolerance predictions. [`bacdive_metal_validation`](../projects/bacdive-metal-validation.md) provides external validation: bacteria isolated from heavy-metal-contaminated sites have predicted tolerance scores a full standard deviation above baseline (Cohen's d = +1.00) [\[25\]](#references). [`bacdive_phenotype_metal_tolerance`](../projects/bacdive-phenotype-metal-tolerance.md) then shows that classical microbiology phenotypes add nothing beyond taxonomy, whereas genome-encoded metal-resistance gene content is the true predictor (raising the model to R-squared = 0.63) [\[26\]](#references). A recurring reframing across [`metal_fitness_atlas`](../projects/metal-fitness-atlas.md) and [`metal_specificity`](../projects/metal-specificity.md) is that metal tolerance is largely a *core-genome robustness* problem rather than mainly an accessory-resistance one [\[27\]](#references). [`counter_ion_effects`](../projects/counter-ion-effects.md) checks whether shared metal/NaCl gene responses are an artifact of counter ions and concludes they reflect genuine shared stress biology [\[28\]](#references), while [`metal_cross_resistance`](../projects/metal-cross-resistance.md) describes cross-resistance as a two-layer phenomenon: a universal stress layer plus a chemistry-specific magnitude layer [\[29\]](#references). Several metal projects are explicitly underpowered (small numbers of matched isolates, detection-limit effect sizes), and those limits are stated rather than buried.

### Ecology, niche, and biogeography

Projects under [Microbial Ecotypes](../topics/microbial-ecotypes.md) and [Environment Biogeography](../topics/environment-biogeography.md) test whether environment shapes gene content -- and largely return well-controlled null results. The original [`ecotype_analysis`](../projects/ecotype-analysis.md) finds phylogenetic relatedness predicts gene-content similarity more strongly than environmental similarity [\[30\]](#references), and the [`ecotype_env_reanalysis`](../projects/ecotype-env-reanalysis.md) finds, contrary to the hypothesis, that human-associated species show *slightly higher* environment correlations than environmental species -- the opposite of the predicted direction [\[31\]](#references). The [`pangenome_openness`](../projects/pangenome-openness.md) project finds pangenome openness (operationalized as one minus the core fraction) does not predict whether environment or phylogeny dominates a species' gene-content variation [\[32\]](#references). [`env_embedding_explorer`](../projects/env-embedding-explorer.md) brings satellite-imagery embeddings to bear and confirms they encode real spatially autocorrelated environmental signal, but only over 28.4% of genomes with a clinical sampling bias. The [`enigma_contamination_functional_potential`](../projects/enigma-contamination-functional-potential.md) and [`lab_field_ecology`](../projects/lab-field-ecology.md) projects connect lab data to ENIGMA field communities; notably, [`lab_field_ecology`](../projects/lab-field-ecology.md) is the first to directly link Fitness Browser lab fitness with ENIGMA CORAL field composition along a geochemical gradient [\[33\]](#references), yet its central message is cautionary -- lab metal tolerance does not simply predict field abundance, because field niches are multidimensional and 16S resolves only to genus [\[34\]](#references).

### Modules, functional dark matter, and metabolism

The remaining projects probe gene function and its limits. [`fitness_modules`](../projects/fitness-modules.md) and [`module_conservation`](../projects/module-conservation.md) apply Independent Component Analysis (ICA) to RB-TnSeq matrices to decompose them into latent modules of co-regulated genes -- a fitness-based complement to sequence-based annotation [\[35\]](#references) -- and find those module genes enriched in the conserved core (86.0% vs 81.5%) [\[36\]](#references). [`cog_analysis`](../projects/cog-analysis.md) documents a universal "two-speed genome" in which core and novel genes occupy distinct COG functional profiles across 32 species. [`essential_genome`](../projects/essential-genome.md) and [`essential_metabolome`](../projects/essential-metabolome.md) map the irreducible core of bacterial life, identifying fifteen gene families -- dominated by ribosomal proteins -- that are essential in every one of 48 surveyed bacteria [\[37\]](#references). The [`paperblast_explorer`](../projects/paperblast-explorer.md) quantifies [Functional Dark Matter](../topics/functional-dark-matter.md) directly: at 50% identity, 55% of protein families are functionally dark or dim in the literature [\[38\]](#references), and *Homo sapiens* alone accounts for 46.7% of all gene-paper records [\[39\]](#references) -- a stark reminder of how skewed the knowledge base is. [`fw300_metabolic_consistency`](../projects/fw300-metabolic-consistency.md) and [`webofmicrobes_explorer`](../projects/webofmicrobes-explorer.md) cross-check metabolite data across collections.

One project is explicitly incomplete: [`temporal_core_dynamics`](../projects/temporal-core-dynamics.md), which would track how core genomes erode over time, reports that no conclusions can yet be drawn because its notebooks contain code without outputs and its data directories are empty [\[40\]](#references). It is included here as scaffolding, not as a result.

## Topics

The twelve linked topics organize the projects above. [Gene Fitness](../topics/gene-fitness.md) and [Pangenome Architecture](../topics/pangenome-architecture.md) are the two load-bearing themes -- nearly every project measures one against the other, which is why the core-genome burden paradox and the fitness-conservation gradient recur. The [Adp1 Model System](../topics/adp1-model-system.md) is the contributor's deep-dive organism, supplying the deletion phenotypes, FBA comparisons, aromatic-catabolism network, and respiratory-wiring results. The [Amr Resistome](../topics/amr-resistome.md) and [Metal Resistance](../topics/metal-resistance.md) topics apply the fitness-conservation lens to two specific selective pressures, and both connect to [Mobile Genetic Elements](../topics/mobile-genetic-elements.md) via horizontally acquired genes. [Microbial Ecotypes](../topics/microbial-ecotypes.md), [Environment Biogeography](../topics/environment-biogeography.md), and [Subsurface Genomics](../topics/subsurface-genomics.md) cover the environment-versus-phylogeny question and the ENIGMA field work, while [Metabolic Pathways](../topics/metabolic-pathways.md) and [Functional Dark Matter](../topics/functional-dark-matter.md) cover the metabolic-capability and annotation-coverage projects. [Microbiome Engineering](../topics/microbiome-engineering.md) is the applied horizon the field work points toward. Treated as a whole, the topic footprint is not a scattering of unrelated interests but a single methodological program applied across selective pressures, organisms, and scales -- one that is as careful to report what the data *cannot* show as what it can. A representative low-confidence example is the suggestion that accessory module families are horizontally transferred niche-specific operons, which the project itself labels speculative and not directly supported by the data [\[41\]](#references).
