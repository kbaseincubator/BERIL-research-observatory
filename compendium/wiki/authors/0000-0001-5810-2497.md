# Paramvir S. Dehal

## Overview

Paramvir S. Dehal is a contributor to the BERIL compendium, credited on a broad
sweep of computational microbial-genomics projects. This page exists to give a
single map of that footprint: which research questions the projects ask, which
data resources they draw on, and which recurring themes tie them together. The
work spans 39 projects and touches twelve topic areas, but it is unusually
coherent. Almost every project is an attempt to connect three kinds of evidence
about bacterial genes -- how *important* a gene is (its measured fitness
contribution), how *conserved* it is (its distribution across a pangenome), and
where the organism that carries it *lives* (its ecological niche) -- and to ask
what those three axes reveal when read against one another.

Concretely, that means leaning on a small number of large data collections. The
[Kescience Fitnessbrowser](../data/kescience-fitnessbrowser.md) supplies
genome-wide gene-fitness measurements from RB-TnSeq (random-barcode transposon
sequencing, in which a pooled library of insertion mutants is grown under many
conditions and each mutant's abundance change reports the gene's fitness cost).
The [Kbase Ke Pangenome](../data/kbase-ke-pangenome.md) supplies gene-cluster
conservation across tens of thousands of species -- the basis for calling a gene
*core* (present in essentially all strains) versus *accessory*. Niche and
biogeography come from [Enigma Coral](../data/enigma-coral.md) field communities,
[Nmdc Arkin](../data/nmdc-arkin.md) metadata, and satellite-imagery embeddings,
while [Kbase Uniref](../data/kbase-uniref.md),
[Kbase Msd Biochemistry](../data/kbase-msd-biochemistry.md), and
[Phagefoundry](../data/phagefoundry.md) supply annotation, biochemistry, and
mobile-element context. The topic footprint follows directly:
[Gene Fitness](../topics/gene-fitness.md),
[Pangenome Architecture](../topics/pangenome-architecture.md),
[Functional Dark Matter](../topics/functional-dark-matter.md),
[Metabolic Pathways](../topics/metabolic-pathways.md),
[Amr Resistome](../topics/amr-resistome.md),
[Metal Resistance](../topics/metal-resistance.md),
[Microbial Ecotypes](../topics/microbial-ecotypes.md),
[Environment Biogeography](../topics/environment-biogeography.md),
[Mobile Genetic Elements](../topics/mobile-genetic-elements.md),
[Subsurface Genomics](../topics/subsurface-genomics.md),
[Microbiome Engineering](../topics/microbiome-engineering.md), and the
[Adp1 Model System](../topics/adp1-model-system.md).

A reader should treat this page as an index with interpretation, not as a list of
results. The sections below group the projects by question, draw out the threads
that connect them, and -- importantly -- carry forward the many caveats the
projects themselves flag, because a recurring feature of this body of work is its
willingness to report null results, statistical artifacts, and the limits of the
underlying data honestly.

## Projects

### Connecting fitness to conservation

The conceptual spine of the contributor's work is the link between how important a
gene is in the lab and how widely it is conserved in nature. Several projects
build this bridge from different angles and largely agree. The synthesis work
joins RB-TnSeq fitness for roughly 194,000 genes across 43 bacteria to pangenome
conservation across tens of thousands of species and finds a *gradient*: essential
genes are about 82% core and always-neutral genes about 66% core, so more
important genes tend to be more conserved
([stmt:fitness-conservation-gradient; conservation_fitness_synthesis]). The
companion `fitness_effects_conservation` project sharpens the framing -- the
relationship is a continuous gradient across the whole essential-to-neutral
spectrum rather than a binary essential/non-essential split
([stmt:continuous-not-binary-claim; fitness_effects_conservation]). The most
distinctive product is a "selection-signature matrix" that crosses lab fitness
cost against pangenome conservation: the ~28,000 genes that are simultaneously
costly to lose in the lab and conserved in the wild are read as the strongest
available evidence for purifying selection acting in natural environments
([stmt:selection-signature-purifying; conservation_fitness_synthesis]).

This synthesis is presented with unusual self-scrutiny. The contributor flags a
figure-versus-text discrepancy in the headline gradient (Figure 1 implies an ~8
percentage-point gap rather than the 16pp stated in prose, because percentages
were computed only over successfully mapped genes), and notes that two separate
essential-gene core rates (82% vs 86%) are reported without full reconciliation
([stmt:figure1-text-discrepancy; conservation_fitness_synthesis]). The broader
caveat is that conservation is only *weakly* predicted by fitness importance, and
single-gene-knockout fitness does not capture epistasis -- so the gradient, while
robust, is a modest signal, not a deterministic rule.

### The core-genome burden paradox

A counterintuitive thread runs through `core_gene_tradeoffs`,
`costly_dispensable_genes`, and the fitness-distribution analyses. The
"streamlining" model predicts that accessory genes are the metabolic burden a cell
would shed. The data point the other way: core genes are *more* likely than
accessory genes to show a positive fitness effect when deleted (i.e., to look
costly under lab conditions), contradicting streamlining
([stmt:core-burden-paradox-claim; core_gene_tradeoffs]). Motility is the canonical
example -- flagellar machinery is energetically expensive and looks like dead
weight in a shake flask, yet it is conserved because chemotaxis matters in real
environments ([stmt:motility-case-study-finding; core_gene_tradeoffs]). The
resolution offered is that lab "burden" is condition-dependent, and natural
selection maintains many genes the lab penalizes.

The flip side is the accessory genome. `costly_dispensable_genes` characterizes
genes that are both costly in the lab and dispensable in the pangenome, finding
them poorly annotated, taxonomically restricted, singleton-enriched, and short
([stmt:recent-acquisitions; costly_dispensable_genes]) -- the hallmarks of recently
acquired DNA. The interpretation is that these are "genomic debris" of horizontal
gene transfer: insertion sequences, prophage remnants, transposases, and defense
systems ([stmt:hgt-debris-interpretation; costly_dispensable_genes]), which links
this work to [Mobile Genetic Elements](../topics/mobile-genetic-elements.md). The
caveats are appropriately structural: core genes *cannot* be singletons by
construction, so part of the singleton enrichment is mechanical rather than
biological.

### Antimicrobial resistance across the pangenome

A large AMR cluster of projects extends the fitness-and-conservation logic to the
resistome -- the full complement of resistance genes
([Amr Resistome](../topics/amr-resistome.md)). The `amr_pangenome_atlas` is a
pan-bacterial atlas showing AMR genes are markedly depleted from the core genome
(30.3% core versus a 46.8% baseline) and enriched in the accessory genome
([stmt:amr-depleted-from-core; amr_pangenome_atlas]). The `amr_fitness_cost`
project then asks whether carrying resistance is costly and finds, perhaps
surprisingly, a small *positive* fitness effect for AMR-gene knockouts under
non-antibiotic conditions (pooled +0.086, with all 25 organisms agreeing in sign)
([stmt:amr-universal-cost; amr_fitness_cost]) -- and, against the expectation that
recently acquired resistance should be costlier, that core (intrinsic) and
accessory (acquired) AMR genes have essentially identical fitness distributions
([stmt:amr-core-accessory-identical; amr_fitness_cost]).

Niche structure is the other half of the AMR story. The `amr_environmental_resistome`
project shows clinical and human-gut species carry roughly 2.5x more AMR clusters
than environmental species ([stmt:clinical-amr-richness; amr_environmental_resistome]),
and that different niches select different *strategies* -- target modification
dominates in the human gut and clinic while efflux dominates in aquatic settings
([stmt:niche-selects-mechanism; amr_environmental_resistome]). The
`amr_strain_variation` project finds AMR genes organized into ~1,517 tightly
co-inherited "resistance islands"
([stmt:resistance-islands-coinherited; amr_strain_variation]), and
`amr_cofitness_networks` produces a first pan-bacterial map of AMR co-fitness
neighborhoods, finding support networks that are organism-specific rather than
mechanism-specific ([stmt:organism-specific-networks; amr_cofitness_networks]).

The AMR conclusions come heavily caveated, and honestly so. The clinical-carries-more
finding is partly confounded by NCBI sampling bias -- clinical isolates are
massively overrepresented precisely because they were sequenced for their
resistance ([stmt:ncbi-sampling-bias; amr_environmental_resistome]). The cost
estimates rest on lab-adapted strains in which compensatory evolution may have
already eroded the true cost ([stmt:amr-lab-adaptation-bias; amr_fitness_cost]).
These are not afterthoughts; they bound what the numbers can be read to mean.

### The Acinetobacter baylyi ADP1 model system

A focused cluster of projects builds out the [Adp1 Model System](../topics/adp1-model-system.md)
-- *Acinetobacter baylyi* ADP1, a naturally transformable bacterium used to study
aromatic-compound catabolism. Its value here is partly that ADP1 is absent from
the Fitness Browser, making its condition-specific deletion-mutant growth data a
resource unavailable elsewhere in BERDL
([stmt:adp1-fitness-unique-resource; acinetobacter_adp1_explorer]). The
`adp1_deletion_phenotypes` project partitions eight carbon sources into demanding,
moderate, and robust tiers (urea most demanding, with 97.9% of genes defective)
([stmt:adp1-three-tier-landscape; adp1_deletion_phenotypes]).
`adp1_triple_essentiality` cross-checks flux-balance analysis (FBA, a constraint-based
metabolic model that predicts growth) against experimental knockouts, finding
moderate concordance (Cohen's kappa ~0.49) but a systematic blind spot for
aromatic-degradation genes ([stmt:fba-moderate-ko-concordance; adp1_triple_essentiality]).

Two mechanistic projects round this out. `aromatic_catabolism_network` shows that
quinate catabolism requires a 51-gene support network organized into four
subsystems beyond the core beta-ketoadipate pathway
([stmt:support-network-51-genes-finding; aromatic_catabolism_network]), and
`respiratory_chain_wiring` finds ADP1 carries three parallel NADH dehydrogenases
with distinct, non-overlapping condition profiles, implying condition-dependent
respiratory wiring ([stmt:three-parallel-nadh-dehydrogenases; respiratory_chain_wiring]).
The ADP1 work flags clear limits: 87% of the explorer's growth predictions depend
on at least one gapfilled reaction, tightly coupling accuracy to gapfilling
quality ([stmt:gapfilling-dependence; acinetobacter_adp1_explorer]), and a binary
"essentiality fraction" score is explicitly warned against as a classifier because
it performs worse than random against knockout truth
([stmt:essentiality-fraction-worse-than-random; adp1_triple_essentiality]).

### Metals: resistance, validation, and specificity

A set of metal-focused projects ([Metal Resistance](../topics/metal-resistance.md))
builds and validates a "Metal Fitness Atlas" of genome-based metal-tolerance
predictions. `bacdive_metal_validation` provides external validation: bacteria
isolated from heavy-metal-contaminated sites have predicted tolerance scores a full
standard deviation above baseline (Cohen's d = +1.00)
([stmt:metal-contaminated-isolates-higher-scores; bacdive_metal_validation]).
`bacdive_phenotype_metal_tolerance` then shows that classical microbiology
phenotypes add nothing beyond taxonomy, whereas genome-encoded metal-resistance
gene content is the true predictor (raising the model to R-squared = 0.63)
([stmt:gene-content-is-true-predictor; bacdive_phenotype_metal_tolerance]). A
recurring reframing across `metal_fitness_atlas` and `metal_specificity` is that
metal tolerance is largely a *core-genome robustness* problem rather than mainly an
accessory-resistance one ([stmt:metal-atlas-core-robustness-finding; metal_fitness_atlas]).
`counter_ion_effects` checks whether shared metal/NaCl gene responses are an
artifact of counter ions and concludes they reflect genuine shared stress biology
([stmt:metal-nacl-overlap; counter_ion_effects]), while `metal_cross_resistance`
describes cross-resistance as a two-layer phenomenon: a universal stress layer plus
a chemistry-specific magnitude layer ([stmt:metal-cross-two-layer-claim; metal_cross_resistance]).
Several metal projects are explicitly underpowered (small numbers of matched
isolates, detection-limit effect sizes), and those limits are stated rather than
buried.

### Ecology, niche, and biogeography

Projects under [Microbial Ecotypes](../topics/microbial-ecotypes.md) and
[Environment Biogeography](../topics/environment-biogeography.md) test whether
environment shapes gene content -- and largely return well-controlled null results.
The original `ecotype_analysis` finds phylogenetic relatedness predicts gene-content
similarity more strongly than environmental similarity
([stmt:ecotype-phylogeny-dominance-finding; ecotype_analysis]), and the
`ecotype_env_reanalysis` finds, contrary to the hypothesis, that human-associated
species show *slightly higher* environment correlations than environmental species
-- the opposite of the predicted direction
([stmt:ecotype-opposite-direction-claim; ecotype_env_reanalysis]). The
`pangenome_openness` project finds pangenome openness (operationalized as one minus
the core fraction) does not predict whether environment or phylogeny dominates a
species' gene-content variation ([stmt:openness-does-not-predict-driver; pangenome_openness]).
`env_embedding_explorer` brings satellite-imagery embeddings to bear and confirms
they encode real spatially autocorrelated environmental signal, but only over 28.4%
of genomes with a clinical sampling bias. The `enigma_contamination_functional_potential`
and `lab_field_ecology` projects connect lab data to ENIGMA field communities;
notably, `lab_field_ecology` is the first to directly link Fitness Browser lab
fitness with ENIGMA CORAL field composition along a geochemical gradient
([stmt:first-link-fb-fitness-enigma-coral; lab_field_ecology]), yet its central
message is cautionary -- lab metal tolerance does not simply predict field abundance,
because field niches are multidimensional and 16S resolves only to genus
([stmt:lab-fitness-does-not-simply-predict-field; lab_field_ecology]).

### Modules, functional dark matter, and metabolism

The remaining projects probe gene function and its limits. `fitness_modules` and
`module_conservation` apply Independent Component Analysis (ICA) to RB-TnSeq
matrices to decompose them into latent modules of co-regulated genes -- a
fitness-based complement to sequence-based annotation
([stmt:ica-fitness-modules-approach; fitness_modules]) -- and find those module
genes enriched in the conserved core (86.0% vs 81.5%)
([stmt:module-genes-enriched-core; module_conservation]). `cog_analysis` documents
a universal "two-speed genome" in which core and novel genes occupy distinct COG
functional profiles across 32 species. `essential_genome` and `essential_metabolome`
map the irreducible core of bacterial life, identifying fifteen gene families --
dominated by ribosomal proteins -- that are essential in every one of 48 surveyed
bacteria ([stmt:fifteen-pan-essential-families; essential_genome]). The
`paperblast_explorer` quantifies [Functional Dark Matter](../topics/functional-dark-matter.md)
directly: at 50% identity, 55% of protein families are functionally dark or dim in
the literature ([stmt:dark-dim-families; paperblast_explorer]), and *Homo sapiens*
alone accounts for 46.7% of all gene-paper records
([stmt:human-dominates-literature; paperblast_explorer]) -- a stark reminder of how
skewed the knowledge base is. `fw300_metabolic_consistency` and
`webofmicrobes_explorer` cross-check metabolite data across collections.

One project is explicitly incomplete: `temporal_core_dynamics`, which would track
how core genomes erode over time, reports that no conclusions can yet be drawn
because its notebooks contain code without outputs and its data directories are
empty ([stmt:analysis-not-executed; temporal_core_dynamics]). It is included here
as scaffolding, not as a result.

## Topics

The twelve linked topics organize the projects above. [Gene Fitness](../topics/gene-fitness.md)
and [Pangenome Architecture](../topics/pangenome-architecture.md) are the two
load-bearing themes -- nearly every project measures one against the other, which is
why the core-genome burden paradox and the fitness-conservation gradient recur. The
[Adp1 Model System](../topics/adp1-model-system.md) is the contributor's deep-dive
organism, supplying the deletion phenotypes, FBA comparisons, aromatic-catabolism
network, and respiratory-wiring results. The [Amr Resistome](../topics/amr-resistome.md)
and [Metal Resistance](../topics/metal-resistance.md) topics apply the
fitness-conservation lens to two specific selective pressures, and both connect to
[Mobile Genetic Elements](../topics/mobile-genetic-elements.md) via horizontally
acquired genes. [Microbial Ecotypes](../topics/microbial-ecotypes.md),
[Environment Biogeography](../topics/environment-biogeography.md), and
[Subsurface Genomics](../topics/subsurface-genomics.md) cover the
environment-versus-phylogeny question and the ENIGMA field work, while
[Metabolic Pathways](../topics/metabolic-pathways.md) and
[Functional Dark Matter](../topics/functional-dark-matter.md) cover the
metabolic-capability and annotation-coverage projects.
[Microbiome Engineering](../topics/microbiome-engineering.md) is the applied
horizon the field work points toward. Treated as a whole, the topic footprint is
not a scattering of unrelated interests but a single methodological program applied
across selective pressures, organisms, and scales -- one that is as careful to
report what the data *cannot* show as what it can. A representative low-confidence
example is the suggestion that accessory module families are horizontally
transferred niche-specific operons, which the project itself labels speculative and
not directly supported by the data
([stmt:accessory-families-hgt-speculative; module_conservation]).
