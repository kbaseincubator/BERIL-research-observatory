# Adam Deutschbauer

Adam Deutschbauer (ORCID 0000-0003-2728-7622) is a researcher whose work in this corpus centers on pan-bacterial fitness genomics — the large-scale use of gene-fitness data from mutant library experiments to understand how microbes respond to environmental stressors across many species simultaneously. This page exists in the wiki because Deutschbauer is an author on the Metal Fitness Atlas project, which contributes the core quantitative findings about metal-stress gene conservation that underpin several topic and data pages in this knowledge graph.

## Overview

Deutschbauer's contribution to this corpus focuses on how bacteria cope with metal stress at a genome-wide, multi-species scale. The central question is whether metal tolerance is a specialized capability confined to a handful of "metal specialist" organisms, or whether it reflects vulnerabilities and robustness properties embedded in the core cellular machinery shared across diverse bacteria.

The Metal Fitness Atlas project, to which Deutschbauer contributes, assembles fitness data from RB-TnSeq experiments — a method in which a pooled library of randomly barcoded transposon mutants is grown under a test condition, and the relative fitness of each gene knockout is measured by sequencing. Applying this approach across 22 organisms and 14 metals yields a dataset of 383,349 gene-by-metal fitness records. The key finding is that genes showing significant fitness defects under metal stress are enriched in the core genome (genes shared across all or most strains of a species), running counter to the hypothesis that metal tolerance would be primarily an accessory-genome trait carried on mobile elements [\[1\]](#references). This reframes metal tolerance as a core-genome robustness problem: the processes most vulnerable to metal disruption — cell envelope integrity, DNA repair, central metabolism — are fundamental to bacterial life, not specialist adaptations.

Supporting this picture, analysis of ortholog groups across organisms identifies 1,182 conserved metal-phenotype gene families (present across at least two organisms), of which 601 span at least three organisms [\[2\]](#references). Among these, 149 families have conserved metal fitness signals but lack full functional annotation, representing prioritized candidates for experimental follow-up. The breadth of conservation itself correlates with pangenome-level conservation (the fraction of strains carrying the gene), reinforcing the view that the most evolutionarily persistent genes are also the ones most consistently impacted by metal stress.

Extending the analysis to 27,702 species in a large bacterial pangenome, the Metal Fitness Atlas uses the conserved gene families to define a metal functional signature of 1,286 KEGG ortholog terms and scores every species. After normalizing for genome size, known bioleaching specialists — organisms used industrially to extract metals from ore — do rank in the upper percentiles (e.g., *Leptospirillum* at the 91st percentile, *Acidithiobacillus* at the 77th), but bioleaching genera as a group are not significantly enriched over background (Mann-Whitney p = 0.17) [\[3\]](#references). This result is consistent with the core robustness model: metal tolerance genes are broadly distributed rather than concentrated in dedicated specialists.

## Projects

Deutschbauer's sole project in this corpus is the **Metal Fitness Atlas** ([`metal_fitness_atlas`](../projects/metal-fitness-atlas.md)). The project compiles fitness data across organisms and metals, clusters genes into cross-species ortholog families, tests for core vs. accessory enrichment, and projects the resulting metal gene signature onto a bacterial pangenome. Data accessed in this project includes the KBase KE Pangenome (see [Kbase Ke Pangenome](../data/kbase-ke-pangenome.md), which is adjacent because the pangenome-scale scoring analysis draws directly on this resource) and the Fitness Browser (see [Kescience Fitnessbrowser](../data/kescience-fitnessbrowser.md), which is the primary source of cross-species RB-TnSeq fitness measurements feeding into the atlas).

## Topics

All three statements attributed to Deutschbauer in this corpus address [Metal Resistance](../topics/metal-resistance.md). The topic is adjacent in the obvious sense — every finding here contributes to understanding how bacteria resist or are damaged by metal stress — but the specific contribution is to shift the conceptual framing: rather than treating metal resistance as an accessory, strain-specific capability, this work establishes that core cellular functions are the dominant locus of metal-stress phenotypes across bacteria.
