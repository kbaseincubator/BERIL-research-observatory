# Adam Deutschbauer

Adam Deutschbauer (ORCID 0000-0003-2728-7622) is a contributor to this knowledge graph, and this page collects the scientific statements attributed to him across the compendium. The wiki maintains a page per author so that a reader can see, in one place, which projects a person contributed to and which scientific topics their work touches. In Adam Deutschbauer's case the footprint is focused: a single project, the metal fitness atlas, all of whose findings here concern how bacteria cope with toxic metals.

## Overview

Adam Deutschbauer's contributions in this compendium all sit within one project and one topic. The project is the [metal fitness atlas](#projects), a cross-species effort to map the genes whose loss changes bacterial fitness under metal stress. The topic is [Metal Resistance](../topics/metal-resistance.md) — the cellular machinery and genetic determinants that let microbes survive exposure to toxic metal ions. The three statements on this page form a single coherent argument: that the genetic basis of metal tolerance is more deeply conserved, and less the province of specialist "resistance genes," than one might expect.

That argument matters because it reframes a common intuition. Metal resistance is often pictured as a set of dedicated efflux pumps and detoxification systems carried on mobile elements — accessory genes that a lineage either has or does not. The findings collected here push in the opposite direction, locating much of the relevant biology in genes that nearly all members of a species share.

## Projects

All of Adam Deutschbauer's statements here come from the **metal fitness atlas** (`metal_fitness_atlas`), which integrates fitness data across many bacterial species to ask which genes matter under metal stress. The atlas draws on two data resources that are themselves pages in this wiki: the [Kescience Fitnessbrowser](../data/kescience-fitnessbrowser.md), a compendium of genome-wide mutant fitness assays that supplies the per-gene phenotype evidence, and the [Kbase Ke Pangenome](../data/kbase-ke-pangenome.md), which provides the pangenome framework used to ask how widely a gene family is distributed across genomes. Both are adjacent to this page because the atlas's claims are built directly on top of them.

The atlas produces three connected results. First, when metal fitness defects are compared across species, they are *enriched in core genes* — the genes present in essentially every member of a species — rather than in the accessory genome. The pangenome concept used here divides a species' gene repertoire into a core shared by all strains and an accessory set that varies between strains; finding the metal signal concentrated in the core "reframes metal tolerance as a core-genome robustness problem rather than mainly an accessory-resistance problem." In other words, tolerating metal stress is less about possessing special resistance cassettes and more about keeping core cellular processes working under chemical insult.

Second, a genome-size-normalized pangenome scoring scheme suggests that metal tolerance genes are *broadly distributed across bacteria* rather than concentrated only in specialist taxa. Normalizing by genome size is what makes this a fair comparison — larger genomes carry more genes overall, so without correction they would appear to harbor more tolerance genes simply by virtue of size. The corrected view points to wide distribution.

Third, the atlas identifies *conserved metal-phenotype gene families*, including novel candidates that can be prioritized for follow-up purely from the cross-species fitness evidence. Because the same gene family shows a metal-related fitness effect in multiple species, the atlas can flag families that are likely to be functionally important even when they have not previously been annotated as metal-related — a way of turning comparative fitness signal into a ranked list of candidates.

## Topics

The single topic on this page is **Metal Resistance**: the biology of how bacteria withstand toxic concentrations of metal ions. Adam Deutschbauer's work here contributes a comparative, genome-scale perspective on that topic. Rather than characterizing one resistance system in one organism, the metal atlas asks the population-level question — across many bacteria, which genes carry the fitness burden of metal stress, and where do they sit in the pangenome? The consistent answer across the three findings is that the load falls heavily on conserved, core machinery. For a reader following the [Metal Resistance](../topics/metal-resistance.md) topic, this is the contribution that argues metal tolerance is partly a property of how robust a cell's shared, essential systems are, not only of which specialized resistance genes it happens to carry. All three statements are reported at high confidence.
