# David Lyon

David Lyon is a contributor in this compendium, identified by ORCID `0000-0002-1927-3565`. This page exists to summarize the footprint of his work across the wiki: which research projects he contributed to, which scientific topics those projects touch, and how the findings, caveats, and follow-up opportunities connect. His work clusters around one coherent question -- how microbial gene content reflects the chemistry and recoverability of subsurface and metal-rich environments -- approached through comparative pangenomics over the [Kbase Ke Pangenome](../data/kbase-ke-pangenome.md), the large genome collection that underlies all three of his projects.

## Overview

Lyon's three projects share a common method and a common skepticism. Each takes a hypothesis about how an environment should shape a genome, tests it against the BERDL pangenome with explicit statistics, and then asks whether the apparent signal survives two recurring confounders: **phylogeny** (a marker may track a lineage rather than a habitat) and **cultivation bias** (a cohort of cultured isolates may not represent the organisms that actually dominate a site). This makes his footprint as much about annotation reliability and study-design limits as about biology.

Three threads recur across the projects. The first is the **subsurface gene-content question** ([Subsurface Genomics](../topics/subsurface-genomics.md), [Pangenome Architecture](../topics/pangenome-architecture.md)): do deep-clay bacteria streamline their genomes the way ultra-small Patescibacteria do, or do they expand them? The second is the **metabolic-toolkit question** ([Metabolic Pathways](../topics/metabolic-pathways.md)): which anaerobic respiration markers genuinely mark a niche versus a phylum. The third is the **lanthanide-methylotrophy question** ([Metal Resistance](../topics/metal-resistance.md), [Microbial Ecotypes](../topics/microbial-ecotypes.md)): how widely the rare-earth-element-dependent methanol enzymes are distributed and where the literature's expectations break down.

A throughline that touches every project is annotation trust. Lyon repeatedly finds that automated functional callers disagree, and that the discrepancy itself is the result. eggNOG and bakta marker calls diverge sharply -- the eggNOG `Preferred_name` "lanM" alone produced 505 false positives concentrated in unrelated gut Bacillota that are not lanthanide users, so bakta-validated calls are the trustworthy ones. This [Functional Dark Matter](../topics/functional-dark-matter.md) problem -- where genuine functions hide behind unreliable or absent labels -- shapes nearly every conclusion below.

## Projects

### clay_confined_subsurface

This project asks what BERDL's cultured genomes reveal about the deep clay-confined subsurface, using the Mont Terri Opalinus claystone as the reference site. Its central finding is a clean metabolic split: deep clay-confined isolates carry a **sulfate-reduction-rich** marker profile, while shallow-clay isolates carry an **iron-reduction-rich** profile -- a near-perfect mirror image (Fisher odds ratio infinite, p = 2x10^-4 for complete sulfate reduction). At the cohort level, deep isolates jointly carry the Wood-Ljungdahl pathway, group-1 [NiFe]-hydrogenase, and dissimilatory sulfate-reduction toolkit at much higher rates than soil or shallow cohorts (mean toolkit score 1.89 of 3 modules, versus 0.39 and 0.03). Sulfate-reduction enrichment is overwhelming against a rock-attached null distribution (binomial p = 4x10^-12).

The most important move in this project is honest about what the cohort represents. Because all eight Opalinus genomes trace to two boreholes (BRC-3 and BIC-A1), the cohort's marker profile becomes a quantitative **diagnostic**: it shows that BERDL captures the *porewater* fraction rather than the *rock-attached* fraction. The genomes carry the Bagnoud porewater signature (sulfate-reduction-rich), not the Mitzscherling rock-attached signature (iron-reduction-rich). Cross-platform validation strengthens the claim of indigeneity -- the deep anchor genera include exactly the recurrent Mont Terri lineages (Desulfosporosinus, BRH-c8a) that an independent survey identified across seven boreholes.

The project is candid about its limits. Every conclusion applies only to cultivable porewater-cultured isolates, not the full community: CPR/DPANN episymbionts and rock-attached Geobacter/Geothrix lineages are essentially absent. This cultivation bias also explains a *negative* result. A literature claim that deep-subsurface bacteria are biosynthetically self-sufficient does **not** generalize here -- deep-clay GapMind amino-acid pathway completeness is comparable to or slightly below the soil baseline (mean 15.5 versus 17.1 of 18, p = 0.009). GapMind, a tool that scores how complete an organism's biosynthetic pathways are, is the right lens, but two things blunt it: the 18-pathway universe saturates near 18 for most cultivable bacteria, limiting resolution at the top end; and the extreme self-sufficient lineages the literature highlights (e.g. *Ca.* Desulforudis audaxviator) are characteristically uncultivated, recovered as MAGs, and therefore excluded from a cultured cohort.

Two follow-ups fall out of this design. Ingesting deep-subsurface MAGs from Mont Terri, Olkiluoto, MX-80 bentonite, and Oak Ridge and re-running the framework would test whether the self-sufficiency signal appears once rock-attached and uncultivated lineages are present. And the sulfate-reduction/iron-reduction marker-rate diagnostic can be reapplied to other subsurface cohorts (granite, basalt, salt-cavern) to quantify porewater bias across BERDL.

### bacillota_b_subsurface_accessory

This project zooms into a single phylum, [Bacillota B](../topics/pangenome-architecture.md) (the Bacillota_B lineage), to ask whether deep-clay specialization within one clade expands or streamlines the genome -- and to audit the markers the clay project relied on. The architecture answer is decisive and contrary to expectation: deep-clay Bacillota_B genomes are significantly *larger* than soil-baseline relatives, encoding roughly 1 Mbp more at the same CheckM completeness. This rejects the streamlining hypothesis in the opposite direction. Deep-clay specialization here correlates with **gene-content expansion**, not the reductive streamlining seen in Patescibacteria -- refuting any universal "subsurface equals streamlining" model for this niche. Per-orthogroup Fisher testing found 547 eggNOG orthologous groups enriched in deep-clay versus soil Bacillota_B, far above the pre-registered prediction of at least 10, and these populate the five expected functional categories (anaerobic respiration, sporulation revival, mineral attachment, regulators, osmoadaptation), with anaerobic respiration the largest annotated hit. A strong single signal is the molybdopterin-cofactor orthogroup COG1977 -- a cofactor source for anaerobic-respiration enzymes -- recovered in all ten anchor genomes but only 11 of 62 baseline genomes.

This project also functions as a correction layer over the clay work, and the corrections cut both ways. On the robust side, the clay project's sulfate-reduction markers (K11180, K11181, K00394, K00395, K00958) were correctly identified, so that side of the story holds. On the fragile side, the clay project's *iron-reduction* markers (K07811, K17324, K17323) turned out to be mismatched genes -- TMAO reductase, glycerol ABC transport, and glycerol permease -- rather than iron-reduction functions. When iron reduction is re-detected properly, using a triple-signal multi-heme cytochrome approach, the clay project's original "shallow much greater than deep" iron-reduction pattern disappears and no cohort comparison stays significant.

The reason proper detection matters is a [Functional Dark Matter](../topics/functional-dark-matter.md) story about how multi-heme cytochromes hide. Multi-heme cytochrome PFAMs are sparse within Bacillota_B, so counting the CXXCH heme-binding motif directly on protein sequences -- not PFAM hits -- carried most of the corrected iron-reduction signal. Likewise, keyword-based functional categorization undercounted the anaerobic-niche signal, leaving a large unannotated bucket that manual reclassification suggests holds roughly 80-100 truly anaerobic-respiration orthogroups. The reusable lesson is methodological: combining curated PFAMs with sequence-based CXXCH counting is a portable BERDL pattern for detecting multi-heme cytochromes whenever KEGG KO assignment is unreliable.

Statistical honesty bounds all of this. With only 10 anchor versus 62 baseline genomes, marginal effects resting on anchor counts of 3-5 are descriptive, not inferential, and genus-level phylogenetic confounding is only partly mitigated. A natural next step is per-genus decomposition of the enrichment signal, expanded via BacDive linkage to additional clay-isolated genomes, to separate genus-specific lineage markers from genuine deep-clay signatures.

### lanthanide_methylotrophy_atlas

This project maps the genomic machinery for lanthanide-dependent methylotrophy -- the use of rare-earth elements as cofactors in methanol oxidation -- across roughly 293,000 genomes. The headline finding is that the lanthanide-dependent methanol dehydrogenase **xoxF** outnumbers the calcium-dependent **mxaF** by about 19:1, strongly supporting that rare-earth-dependent methanol oxidation, not the classical calcium pathway, dominates. This dominance is not an artifact of one oversized phylum: a Bayesian binomial mixed model that corrects for phylogeny still yields a roughly 143:1 ratio.

The atlas redraws the environmental and taxonomic map of where this metabolism lives ([Environment Biogeography](../topics/environment-biogeography.md), [Microbial Ecotypes](../topics/microbial-ecotypes.md)). Soil/sediment is the strongest broad environmental enrichment for xoxF (OR = 1.92, p_BH = 6x10^-39), consistent with soil methanol oxidation and soil rare-earth mineral reservoirs, while host-associated environments are dramatically depleted (OR = 0.058), matching the absence of methylotrophy in the gut. Crucially, the highest *per-genome* xoxF rates appear in phyla rarely tied to one-carbon metabolism -- Acidobacteriota at 28.3 percent, plus Gemmatimonadota -- which expands the candidate set of lanthanide-utilizing organisms well beyond classical methylotrophs.

Lyon's annotation-trust theme is central here. Bakta-validated lanmodulin (the lanthanide-binding protein) is found in 62 genomes, all 100 percent confined to three alpha-Proteobacterial methylotroph families (Beijerinckiaceae, Acetobacteraceae), strongly supporting total clade restriction. For this reason, the bakta `product` field should be used exclusively for lanmodulin in the BERDL pangenome, because the eggNOG "lanM" calls are noise. Two further apparent paradoxes turn out to be annotation gaps rather than biology: the xoxF-without-PQQ asymmetry is largely artifactual, since 59 percent of xoxF genomes lacking eggNOG PQQ annotations carry a bakta PQQ product that eggNOG misses.

The project is careful to mark what stays descriptive. xoxF co-occurs with lanmodulin in only 79.0 percent of lanmodulin genomes -- just under the pre-registered 80 percent threshold -- so lanmodulin presence does not reliably predict a co-located lanthanide-MDH operon. And rare-earth-impacted samples show a 3.5-fold elevation in xoxF rate, but at n = 37 this misses the significance threshold (p_BH = 0.082) and stays descriptive. Probing one such set, the 37 rare-earth acid-mine-drainage MAGs turned out to be dominated by acidophilic, metal-tolerant lineages rather than methylotrophs -- only 4 of 37 carry any xoxF and none carry validated lanmodulin. Two follow-ups would convert description into inference: recruiting a larger collection of explicitly rare-earth-impacted metagenomes from sequence archives, and -- bridging to [Gene Fitness](../topics/gene-fitness.md) -- targeted RB-TnSeq (random-barcode transposon sequencing, a genome-wide method that measures each gene's fitness contribution under a condition) under lanthanum or cerium chloride stress in the FitnessBrowser organism panel, which would yield the first transposon-validated rare-earth-tolerance gene set in soil organisms that already carry xoxF.

## Topics

Lyon's work touches eight topics in this wiki, weighted toward subsurface genomics and metabolic pathways.

- [Subsurface Genomics](../topics/subsurface-genomics.md) and [Pangenome Architecture](../topics/pangenome-architecture.md) anchor the two clay projects: the central architectural result is that deep-clay Bacillota_B *expand* rather than streamline, with 547 enriched orthogroups and genomes about 1 Mbp larger than soil relatives.
- [Metabolic Pathways](../topics/metabolic-pathways.md) recurs in every project -- the anaerobic toolkit (Wood-Ljungdahl, [NiFe]-hydrogenase, sulfate reduction) in the clay work, and the xoxF/mxaF/PQQ machinery in the lanthanide atlas. Within-phylum control matters here: the toolkit signal is largely phylogenetic, since Wood-Ljungdahl and [NiFe]-hydrogenase track the Bacillota_B lineage at high background rates even in soil, while only dissimilatory sulfate reduction survives within-phylum control (enriched in 5/5 deep versus 4/19 soil Bacillota_B, p_BH = 0.04).
- [Metal Resistance](../topics/metal-resistance.md) covers the rare-earth element biology: xoxF dominance, lanmodulin clade restriction, and the proposed RB-TnSeq follow-up.
- [Microbial Ecotypes](../topics/microbial-ecotypes.md) appears wherever a marker maps cleanly to a niche or clade -- the deep/shallow mirror image, the alpha-Proteobacterial lanmodulin restriction, and the recurrent Mont Terri lineages.
- [Environment Biogeography](../topics/environment-biogeography.md) frames the soil-enriched, host-depleted xoxF distribution and the rare-earth-impacted-sample analyses.
- [Functional Dark Matter](../topics/functional-dark-matter.md) is the connective tissue: most caveats and corrections -- sparse multi-heme PFAMs, keyword undercounting, eggNOG/bakta disagreement, hidden PQQ products -- are about functions that automated annotation misses or mislabels.
- [Gene Fitness](../topics/gene-fitness.md) enters only as a proposed direction: RB-TnSeq under lanthanide stress to experimentally validate rare-earth-tolerance genes.
