# Kbase Phenotype

The KBase Phenotype data collection is a reference dataset of host-disease phenotype labels and microbiome compositional profiles used to anchor differential-abundance analysis in IBD research. In this wiki it exists as the shared data foundation that connects per-patient clinical phenotype to metagenome-derived ecotype calls, making it the bridge between a patient's diagnosis label and the microbial community state that determines which phage cocktail is appropriate for them.

## Overview

Inflammatory bowel disease (IBD) — encompassing Crohn's disease (CD) and ulcerative colitis (UC) — presents a fundamental challenge for microbiome-targeted therapies: the gut microbial community is not uniform across patients even within a single diagnosis. The key insight driving this data collection's use is that cohort-level pooling of case and control metagenomes produces a structural confound: in curatedMetagenomicData (cMD), healthy and CD samples come from entirely disjoint source studies, so a naively pooled mixed model cannot distinguish disease from study-of-origin effects [\[1\]](#references). The workaround — comparing CD vs. non-IBD controls *within* IBD sub-studies — recovers a clean canonical Crohn's signal of pathobionts elevated and protective commensals depleted, with high sign concordance across four cohorts [\[2\]](#references).

Layered on top of this confound-free differential-abundance strategy is an ecotype framework. Running two independent clustering algorithms — Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA, a topic-model approach that treats each sample as a mixture of microbial "topics") and a Gaussian Mixture Model (GMM) on CLR-transformed, PCA-reduced profiles — across 8,489 MetaPhlAn3 metagenomic samples selects K = 4 ecotypes by a cross-method Adjusted Rand Index (ARI) parsimony rule [\[3\]](#references). ARI measures how consistently two independent clustering methods assign the same samples together; a parsimony rule picks the smallest K that comes within 0.02 ARI of the global maximum, yielding K = 4. The four ecotypes (E0 diverse commensal, E1 Bacteroides2 transitional, E2 Prevotella-dominant, E3 severe Bacteroides-expanded) are biologically coherent and carry distinct disease-association patterns.

An important practical limitation: clinical metadata such as diagnosis codes, medication classes, or disease activity scores cannot substitute for metagenomics when assigning within-IBD ecotype. Covariates easily separate healthy from IBD subjects but fail to distinguish the transitional E1 from severe E3 ecotypes, so metagenomics remains a hard requirement for ecotype assignment [\[4\]](#references).

A multi-cohort integration pitfall encountered during this work is taxon-name format divergence: cMD healthy cohorts, cMD-IBD cohorts, and the UC Davis whole-genome shotgun dataset all use different MetaPhlAn3 name formats. Without a taxonomy synonymy layer — an NCBI taxid plus GTDB-version-aware rename table — naively pivoting the abundance table splits the same species across multiple zero-overlap rows and produces artifactual log2-fold-change values near 28 [\[5\]](#references). This synonymy layer is identified as a reusable convention for any multi-cohort microbiome project.

The ecotype framework reveals that phage cocktail design at the cohort level is insufficient: because individual patients land in different ecotypes with different dominant pathobionts, a single pooled target list will mismatch a substantial fraction of patients [\[6\]](#references). Projecting 23 UC Davis Crohn's patients onto the K = 4 reference confirms this — the cohort distributes non-randomly across three ecotypes (27 % E0, 42 % E1, 31 % E3; none in E2), with 73 % of patients in the active-disease ecotypes E1 or E3 [\[7\]](#references). The absence of E2 is consistent with a Western-diet cohort where *Prevotella copri*-dominant communities are rare.

## Projects Using This Collection

The single project drawing on the KBase Phenotype collection is **ibd_phage_targeting**, which uses phenotype labels from this resource alongside curatedMetagenomicData metagenomic profiles to train, validate, and apply the four-ecotype framework to UC Davis CD patients.

Several downstream analyses build on the ecotype assignments produced from this foundation. On the pathobiont side, genomic analysis of Tier-A targets — the highest-priority organisms for phage intervention — shows that iron-siderophore biosynthetic gene clusters (BGCs, genomic operons encoding secondary metabolites) are enriched at odds ratios exceeding 44 in pathobiont genomes relative to background, establishing iron acquisition as a genomic capability that defines Crohn's pathobionts rather than merely appearing as a pathway signal in cohort-level data [\[8\]](#references). Drilling further, only *Escherichia coli* (specifically the adherent-invasive AIEC subset) among the six actionable Tier-A pathobionts carries both iron-siderophore and genotoxin BGCs — including Colibactin, a DNA-damaging compound — while the remaining five Tier-A organisms sit in genomic "dark matter" with no MIBiG-annotated iron or genotoxin clusters [\[9\]](#references). This is connected to the [Mobile Genetic Elements](../topics/mobile-genetic-elements.md) topic because BGCs are horizontally mobile genomic islands.

On the phage side, the coverage analysis reveals a mismatch between pathobiont priority and phage availability: *Hungatella hathewayi* and *Mediterraneibacter gnavus*, the two highest-scored species by the pathobiont prioritization model, have the weakest phage availability in the literature [\[10\]](#references). This gap means that no E1 patient can receive a pure phage cocktail; every treatment plan requires a hybrid strategy combining direct-lytic phages for *E. coli* (*E. bolteae* and *E. lenta*) with non-phage alternatives — enzyme inhibitors, bile-acid binding co-therapy, or lytic-engineering approaches — for the gap species [\[11\]](#references). For *E. coli* specifically, a greedy minimum-set-cover algorithm over an experimentally measured phage-strain susceptibility matrix in the PhageFoundry database yields a 5-phage cocktail covering 94.7 % of 188 strains, comparable to the EcoActive 7-phage clinical-trial cocktail [\[12\]](#references). Closing the gap for gut-anaerobe targets would require querying external databases — INPHARED (~25,000 phages) and IMG/VR (~3 million uncultivated viral genomes) — which are out of scope for the current analysis but identified as the highest-priority near-term extension [\[13\]](#references). This connects directly to the [Microbiome Engineering](../topics/microbiome-engineering.md) topic.

The endogenous phageome (the natural virome already present in a patient's gut) adds another layer: Gokushovirus WZ-2015a, a member of the *Microviridae* family with a small single-stranded DNA genome that infects *Bacteroides* and *Prevotella* commensals, is robustly depleted in Crohn's disease across multiple ecotypes, with the strongest depletion in the E1 ecotype [\[14\]](#references). This independently reproduces a signal from the HMP2 precomputed reference, lending cross-dataset confidence. The [Microbial Ecotypes](../topics/microbial-ecotypes.md) page is adjacent here because the depletion pattern is ecotype-stratified, and [Mobile Genetic Elements](../topics/mobile-genetic-elements.md) is adjacent because Microviridae are themselves mobile genetic elements whose abundance reflects the broader ecology of horizontal gene transfer in the gut.

Longitudinal analysis of a single patient (patient 6967) who was sampled twice and shifted ecotypes between E1 and E3 shows that a patient's ecotype is not necessarily stable over time: the optimal cocktail at visit 1 and visit 2 shares a core trio (*H. hathewayi*, *M. gnavus*, *E. lenta*) but differs in two visit-specific targets (Jaccard similarity = 0.60), motivating state-dependent cocktail re-design rather than a fixed prescription [\[15\]](#references).

Two caveats limit the generalizability of these findings. First, the E3 Tier-A target list rests on evidence from a single sub-study (HallAB_2017) because the HMP2 cohort has too few E3 patients to support an independent meta-analysis; the E3 list should be treated as provisional until a second cohort with sufficient E3 patients and both diagnosis groups becomes available [\[16\]](#references). Second, the broader structural issue that the E0 and E2 ecotypes have no viable within-substudy contrast in cMD means that ecotype-specific Tier-A lists for those two subtypes must rely on cross-ecotype cohort-level evidence.
