# Enigma Coral

ENIGMA CORAL is the shared field-data platform that underpins the ENIGMA (Ecosystems and Networks Integrated with Genes and Molecular Assemblies) research program at the Oak Ridge Reservation (ORR) in Tennessee. The database, hosted on BERDL (Biological and Environmental Research Data Lake), integrates thousands of groundwater and sediment samples collected across a network of contaminated and background monitoring wells with paired geochemistry measurements, 16S amplicon community surveys, cultured-isolate genomes, and laboratory phenotype assays. This wiki page exists because CORAL is the common data backbone shared by every ENIGMA project in this compendium: ecology studies, functional-potential analyses, genotype-to-phenotype modelers, and gene-fitness conservation analyses all draw from its tables. Understanding what CORAL contains, what its taxonomic resolution is, and where its coverage gaps are is therefore essential context for interpreting results across all five projects.

## Overview

The Oak Ridge Field Research Center site is a legacy nuclear-weapons-production site contaminated with nitric-acid leachate carrying uranium, chromium, nickel, and other heavy metals. CORAL stores data from the SSO (Subsurface Science Observatory), a 3×3 grid of monitoring wells spanning roughly 6 meters. The spatial structure of SSO microbial communities is governed by a contamination plume entering from the northeast and flowing southwest through the saturated zone [\[1\]](#references). Community similarity tracks this flow path at meter scale—16S Bray-Curtis dissimilarity reveals a northeast-to-southwest U3-M6-L7 corridor of unexpectedly similar communities that aligns with the expected plume trajectory from the Area 3 uphill source [\[2\]](#references). This means that 16S community composition alone can map subsurface hydrology without direct geochemical measurements [\[3\]](#references).

Depth (hydrogeological zone) is the strongest predictor of community composition in sediment cores, explaining 27.5% of variance by PERMANOVA (p = 0.0001) while well identity is not significant [\[4\]](#references). Ten of twelve dominant phyla show significant depth associations that split into shallow-oxic-enriched groups (Chloroflexi, Patescibacteria) and deep-anoxic-enriched groups (Firmicutes, Bacteroidota, Proteobacteria), mirroring the subsurface redox gradient [\[5\]](#references). In groundwater, the spatial pattern is remarkably stable over time: well identity explains 49.9% of variance while sampling date explains only 0.8%, validating the interpretation that spatial differences reflect persistent environmental structure [\[6\]](#references).

Genus-level functional inference—assigning biogeochemical roles to annotated genera—maps the classic thermodynamic redox ladder (O₂ → NO₃⁻ → Fe(III) → SO₄²⁻ → fermentation) onto the physical grid. The central well M5 hosts the highest denitrification potential (7.7% *Rhodanobacter*), placing the plume nitrate-meets-organic-carbon mixing zone [\[7\]](#references). Metabolic guild co-occurrence reveals tight ecological coupling: nitrifiers and iron oxidizers co-occur strongly (ρ = +0.95), both concentrated at plume-entry well U3, while denitrifiers and syntrophs are mutually exclusive across the redox gradient (ρ = −0.67) [\[8\]](#references). Groundwater carries a distinct planktonic community enriched in denitrifiers and iron oxidizers relative to sediment, rather than being a simple suspension of sediment communities [\[9\]](#references).

When geochemistry data is paired with community composition across the 108 sample sites where both are available, contaminated sites (split at the median uranium concentration) show distinctly different community composition, with rare-biosphere taxa and subsurface specialists becoming more prominent [\[10\]](#references). Five of eleven Fitness Browser genera with sufficient site coverage show statistically significant correlations with uranium after BH-FDR correction—*Herbaspirillum* and *Bacteroides* increase at high-uranium sites, while *Caulobacter*, *Sphingomonas*, and *Pedobacter* decrease [\[11\]](#references). *Caulobacter* and *Sphingomonas* emerge as the most sensitive indicators of uranium contamination [\[12\]](#references).

Interestingly, a global pH-driven niche partition mirrors the local contamination gradient: analysis of 464K worldwide 16S samples shows two anti-correlated genus clusters at Oak Ridge—an acid-tolerant Cluster B (dominated by *Rhodanobacter*, *Ralstonia*, and *Dyella*) whose global average environment is pH 5.4, and a neutral-preferring Cluster A (*Brevundimonas*, *Caulobacter*, *Sphingomonas*) averaging pH 6.8 [\[13\]](#references). The local contamination gradient, driven by nitric-acid leachate, thus reproduces a globally recognizable pH ecotype separation (see [Microbial Ecotypes](../topics/microbial-ecotypes.md) for more on this pattern).

## Projects Using This Collection

**enigma_sso_asv_ecology** uses CORAL's 16S amplicon and sample data to characterize community ecology across the SSO 3×3 well grid. It provides the spatial contamination-plume model, the depth-zonation analysis, and the functional-guild inference described above. The project's key limitation is that 221 SSO geochemistry sample tubes registered in CORAL have not been loaded into BERDL, so all environmental inference is from community composition alone—the plume model's predictions await geochemical confirmation [\[14\]](#references). Only 5 of 9 wells have groundwater ASV data, missing critical wells M5 (the denitrification hotspot) and U3 (the plume entry), so hotspot interpretations rest on sediment data [\[15\]](#references). Furthermore, genus-level functional annotation covers only 21% of total reads—process abundance estimates are lower bounds and the unclassified majority could harbor additional functional capacity [\[16\]](#references).

**enigma_contamination_functional_potential** asks whether contamination gradients translate into shifts in inferred stress-related functional potential. It builds a contamination index from eight metal columns (arsenic, cadmium, chromium, copper, lead, nickel, uranium, and zinc) using per-metal log₁p z-scoring [\[17\]](#references). The pre-declared confirmatory test—Spearman correlation of a site defense score against the contamination index—remained non-significant in both genus-level mapping modes (relaxed rho = 0.059, q = 0.862; strict rho = 0.068, q = 0.849) [\[18\]](#references). Retesting under four contamination-index variants including uranium-only left all results non-significant after FDR correction [\[19\]](#references), and within-fraction Spearman tests in each micron-filter stratum were similarly non-significant [\[20\]](#references). Exploratory coverage-adjusted models did show positive defense associations with contamination, but these attenuated under global multiple-testing correction [\[21\]](#references). The null confirmatory result is consistent with prior Oak Ridge studies reporting pronounced compositional shifts alongside only modest functional-diversity decline [\[22\]](#references). One interpretation is that contamination-driven taxonomic turnover is functionally redundant at genus resolution, or that the relevant adaptation operates at species-to-strain resolution that COG-fraction proxies cannot resolve [\[23\]](#references). The project contributes a reproducible ENIGMA-to-BERDL workflow with strict-versus-relaxed feature construction and mapped-coverage diagnostics [\[24\]](#references). Readers should be aware that the main residual interpretive risk is over-weighting the exploratory adjusted-model significance relative to the null confirmatory endpoint [\[25\]](#references).

**lab_field_ecology** is the first study to directly link Fitness Browser (FB) lab fitness data with ENIGMA CORAL field community composition across a geochemical gradient [\[26\]](#references). The Fitness Browser is a publicly accessible database of RB-TnSeq (randomized-barcode transposon sequencing) gene-fitness measurements—a technique in which a dense library of transposon mutants is competed under a test condition and the relative fitness of each mutant, reflecting each gene's importance for growth under that condition, is measured by barcode sequencing. Fourteen of 26 FB genera were detected at Oak Ridge via 16S amplicon sequencing; *Sphingomonas* is found at 93% of sites, *Pseudomonas* at 91%, *Caulobacter* at 82% [\[27\]](#references). The ENIGMA model organism *Desulfovibrio*, however, is detected at only 34% of sites and at very low relative abundance (max 0.09%), making it unreliable for field abundance correlation [\[28\]](#references), and indeed neither *Desulfovibrio* nor *Pseudomonas* show significant uranium abundance correlations despite being the primary lab workhorses [\[29\]](#references). Crucially, aggregate lab metal tolerance does not significantly predict field abundance ratio (rho = 0.503, p = 0.095, n = 12 genera) [\[30\]](#references): lab metal tolerance does not simply translate to field dominance because field niches are multidimensional (pH, redox, carbon sources, community interactions) and 16S resolves only to genus [\[31\]](#references). Metal resistance genes are enriched in the accessory genome rather than the conserved core, consistent with *Rhodanobacter* strains acquiring heavy-metal resistance via horizontal gene transfer [\[32\]](#references). The [Metal Resistance](../topics/metal-resistance.md) topic page covers this genomic pattern across multiple projects.

**field_vs_lab_fitness** uses CORAL's discovery of *Desulfovibrio vulgaris* Hildenborough (DvH) absence to motivate its design: exploration found CORAL contains no DvH fitness data—the single TnSeq library in the database is for *Pseudomonas* strain FW300-N2E2 [\[33\]](#references). The project instead classifies 757 DvH RB-TnSeq experiments from the Fitness Browser into field and lab conditions (337 field versus 420 lab) [\[34\]](#references) and tests whether field-relevant conditions predict higher pangenome conservation. Field-stress genes (genes important for FRC contaminants including uranium, mercury, and nitrate) are significantly enriched in the core genome (83.6% core, OR = 1.58, q = 0.026) and this result is robust to fitness threshold choice from −1 to −3 [\[35\]](#references) [\[36\]](#references). In a surprising counter-finding, genes with fitness defects only under lab conditions are 96.0% core—slightly higher than field-specific genes at 88.5%—suggesting that fitness importance per se, regardless of ecological context, is the primary predictor of conservation [\[37\]](#references) [\[38\]](#references). The broader finding is that condition type matters less than fitness magnitude for conservation prediction: the core genome reflects general functional importance rather than niche-specific selection [\[39\]](#references). In logistic regression, gene length is a much stronger predictor of core-genome status (CV-AUC 0.645) than field or lab fitness effects, neither of which alone is informative (CV-AUC near 0.5) [\[40\]](#references)—though gene length is confounded with both fitness measurement quality and core status itself [\[41\]](#references). At the module level (ICA modules—groups of genes that respond together across experiments), field condition activity does not correlate with conservation (rho = 0.071, p = 0.62), but nine "lab" modules with mean core fraction 0.516 are clearly accessory, while 21 "ecological" modules are highly conserved (mean core fraction 0.980) and contain 52 unannotated genes as candidates for environmental adaptation [\[42\]](#references) [\[43\]](#references). Antibiotic-resistance and heavy-metal-resistance genes are the least conserved categories (73.4% and 71.2% core respectively), below the 76.3% baseline, suggesting these are recently acquired accessory traits carried on mobile genetic elements [\[44\]](#references). The [Mobile Genetic Elements](../topics/mobile-genetic-elements.md) page is adjacent because this project directly ties the CORAL accessory genome to horizontally transferred resistance determinants.

**genotype_to_phenotype_enigma** builds the largest genotype-to-phenotype model for ENIGMA strains, assembling 46,389 genome-by-condition training pairs from five complementary datasets including ENIGMA growth curves, the Fitness Browser, Web of Microbes, and a Carbon Source Phenotype corpus [\[45\]](#references). The ENIGMA growth-curve corpus from CORAL (27,632 curves across 123 strains and 195 molecules) aligns with Fitness Browser data through 486 strain-by-condition anchor pairs [\[46\]](#references). Binary growth (can a strain grow or not?) is predictable from KEGG Ortholog (KO) gene presence/absence on amino acids (AUC 0.775) and nucleosides (AUC 0.780), but not on metals, antibiotics, or nitrogen from gene content alone [\[47\]](#references). Continuous growth parameters (maximum growth rate, lag time, and yield) are not predictable from KO content under cross-genus holdout—because gene presence encodes metabolic capability but not kinetic rate [\[48\]](#references). Growth-predictive KOs and metabolite-production-associated KOs are essentially uncorrelated feature sets (Spearman rho = 0.043), answering different biological questions; both are explained by gene content when the analysis is matched to sample size [\[49\]](#references). With 46K training pairs, SHAP analysis (a method for explaining machine-learning predictions) surfaces mechanistically coherent condition-specific catabolic genes—the ribose transporter rbsC for ribose growth, the protocatechuate cycloisomerase pcaB for aromatic catabolism [\[50\]](#references). The top SHAP features show only 1.19× enrichment for Fitness Browser essentiality signals, reflecting the fundamental difference between gene presence across genera and gene essentiality within a single strain [\[51\]](#references). The [Gene Fitness](../topics/gene-fitness.md) topic and [Metabolic Pathways](../topics/metabolic-pathways.md) topic are adjacent because CORAL's phenotype data anchors both fitness-conservation inference and pathway-level growth prediction.

ENIGMA strains are ecological outliers within their genera—all ENIGMA *Pseudomonas* isolates belong to the environmental *Pseudomonas_E* (fluorescens/protegens) clade rather than the clinically dominant lineage [\[52\]](#references). A data quality pitfall was discovered and documented: linking ENIGMA field isolates to reference databases using short strain names caused 12 of 32 genus-level mismatches; for example, "MT20" (an ENIGMA *Rhodanobacter glycinis*) matched unrelated NCBI organisms (*Streptococcus pneumoniae*) and injected 1,751 spurious clinical genomes into environmental profiles before the error was caught using GTDB-Tk taxonomy [\[53\]](#references). This pitfall affects any project linking ENIGMA strains to the pangenome by name.

## Caveats and Open Directions

Several shared limitations run across all five projects that use CORAL. The ENIGMA taxonomy table provides labels only through genus, making true species-level or strain-level analysis impossible from field community data [\[54\]](#references). This also means that 862 of 1,392 observed genera were unmapped to the current pangenome bridge, and the COG-fraction proxies used as functional features are coarse summaries rather than curated pathway annotations [\[55\]](#references). Trying to refine to species resolution via a species-proxy mode (restricting to genera mapping to a single GTDB clade) sharply reduces mean mapped abundance fraction from 0.343 to 0.031, cutting statistical power severely [\[56\]](#references). Cross-dataset condition alignment is string-based and found only 42 molecular matches; ChEBI-ID-based canonicalization would likely expand this to 60-80 matches [\[57\]](#references). Confounding geochemistry—pH, dissolved oxygen, carbon sources—is not controlled for in field abundance correlations, so uranium's independent role on community structure remains uncertain [\[58\]](#references). The aggregate metal tolerance score used to compare genera is crude; condition-specific fitness scores would be more informative [\[59\]](#references). For gene-fitness analyses based on DvH, the species is represented by a single strain from a pangenome with few members and a high 76.3% baseline core fraction that compresses effect sizes [\[60\]](#references), and the field-specific versus lab-specific gene sets are small (n ≈ 50) limiting statistical power [\[61\]](#references).

Open directions proposed across the projects include loading SSO geochemistry into BERDL to confirm the contamination-plume model with direct chemical data [\[62\]](#references), deploying metagenomics at the SSO wells to move beyond 16S taxonomy limitations [\[63\]](#references), adding curated metal-resistance gene sets to replace broad COG proxies [\[64\]](#references), building a species-to-strain taxonomy bridge to unlock strain-level functional inference [\[65\]](#references), adding *Rhodanobacter* to the Fitness Browser to bridge the gap between CORAL's most ecologically prominent contamination-indicator genus and genome-scale fitness data [\[66\]](#references), developing species-level metagenomics to enable strain-resolved ecology [\[67\]](#references), designing active-learning experiments targeting Oak Ridge-relevant substrates where the genotype-to-phenotype model is least confident [\[68\]](#references), and linking geochemistry gradients directly to phenotype models [\[69\]](#references). The [Functional Dark Matter](../topics/functional-dark-matter.md) page is adjacent because 52 unannotated genes in the conserved ecological modules, and the 21% functional annotation coverage of the SSO, both exemplify the annotation gap that constrains all CORAL-based analyses. The [Subsurface Genomics](../topics/subsurface-genomics.md) topic page is adjacent because CORAL is the primary genomic field data resource for the Oak Ridge contaminated subsurface.
