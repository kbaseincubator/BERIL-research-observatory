# Kbase Msd Biochemistry

The KBase Microbial Sciences Division (MSD) Biochemistry collection is a shared computational infrastructure connecting genome-scale metabolic models, biochemical reaction databases, and curated pathway annotations to experimental phenotype data. It sits at the heart of this wiki because nearly every project that queries "what metabolic reactions does this organism carry, and which are essential?" ultimately reaches into the same ModelSEED biochemistry tables, GapMind pathway predictions, and KBase pangenome data structures. Understanding what this collection contains—and its known limitations—is prerequisite reading before interpreting results from any of the seven projects that draw on it.

## Overview

Genome-scale metabolic modelling in bacteria typically follows a workflow: a draft model is reconstructed from genome annotations by mapping genes to reactions in a reference biochemistry database (here, ModelSEED), the model is checked for the ability to grow in silico using flux balance analysis (FBA, a linear-programming method that finds the set of reaction fluxes consistent with stoichiometric and thermodynamic constraints), and gaps—reactions required for growth that have no annotated gene—are filled algorithmically (gapfilling). The KBase MSD Biochemistry collection is the substrate on which this entire workflow runs.

A critical quantitative caveat sits at the top of this picture: 87% of the 121,519 growth phenotype predictions made across projects in this corpus rely on at least one gapfilled reaction [\[1\]](#references). Gapfilling is not a weakness in principle, but it means that wherever predictions turn out to be wrong, gapfilling quality is the first place to look. Compounding this, FBA gene-knockout validation is effectively circular when gapfilled reactions are the only path to growth on carbon-source minimal media—removing a predicted essential gene also removes the gapfilled reactions needed to simulate that condition, so the model cannot grow regardless [\[2\]](#references).

Alongside the model layer, the collection includes pathway-level annotations from GapMind—a curated database that evaluates completeness of specific metabolic pathways (amino acid biosynthesis, central catabolism, carbon utilization) by comparing genome sequences against experimentally characterised enzyme families. GapMind outputs are pathway-level calls (complete, incomplete, or absent) rather than individual gene predictions, and this coarser granularity limits the resolution of cross-comparisons with reaction-level gapfilling results [\[3\]](#references).

Metabolite identity across databases is a recurring problem. The Web of Microbes (WoM) exometabolomics dataset uses metabolite names and molecular formulae that do not map directly to ModelSEED compound identifiers. High-confidence name-match links cover only a fraction of the WoM metabolite space, while formula-only matches are inherently ambiguous—one WoM formula can expand to ~8.4 candidate ModelSEED molecules [\[4\]](#references). This means cross-collection analyses that depend on metabolite identity (for example, testing whether a metabolite secreted by an organism in exometabolomics also supports fitness in RB-TnSeq experiments) must treat formula-matched links as provisional [\[5\]](#references).

The WoM snapshot that projects in this corpus used has an additional limitation: it records no metabolite consumption (decrease) actions for any organism, capturing only what organisms produce [\[6\]](#references). This asymmetry means one cannot directly test whether a metabolite consumed by one organism is produced by another—the uptake side of cross-feeding is invisible. The underlying exometabolomic experiments were also performed on rich R2A medium, while Fitness Browser fitness measurements use minimal single carbon- or nitrogen-source media, so condition-dependent metabolite profiles may be comparing different physiological states [\[7\]](#references).

## Projects Using This Collection

Seven projects drew on KBase MSD Biochemistry resources; their findings illustrate both the power and the limits of this shared substrate.

**Annotation Gap Discovery** built the most direct audit of the biochemistry layer. It integrated five evidence types—gapfilling signals, RB-TnSeq gene fitness (a high-throughput transposon-sequencing method that measures the growth cost of disrupting individual genes across many conditions simultaneously), pangenome conservation, GapMind pathway predictions, and BLAST sequence homology—to resolve metabolic annotation gaps across 14 organisms [\[8\]](#references). No single evidence stream resolved more than 35% of gaps on its own; BLAST homology alone reached 34.8%, and the full integrated pipeline reached 47.8% by adding 13 percentage points across all streams [\[9\]](#references). Each stream contributes uniquely—leave-one-out cross-validation confirmed that removing any single type degrades overall performance [\[10\]](#references). The combined approach resolved 96 of 201 gapfilled enzymatic reaction-organism pairs (47.8%), exceeding the pre-specified 30% success threshold [\[11\]](#references).

Within the resolved gaps, branched-chain amino acid (BCAA) biosynthesis reactions dominated: two sequential reactions (rxn02185 and rxn03436) were resolved with high confidence in 9 of 14 organisms, and their co-resolution across independent evidence streams validates that the triangulation approach is finding real biology [\[12\]](#references). The toughest gaps are the "dark reactions"—50 of 201 gapfilled reactions (24.9%) lack an EC number assignment in ModelSEED—and these were resolved only 16% of the time compared to 58.3% for reactions with a known EC number [\[13\]](#references). Despite their difficulty, the dark reactions are high-value targets precisely because resolving even one provides a novel enzyme-function assignment [\[14\]](#references). Organism-level resolution varied 3.5-fold (20% for Bacteroides thetaiotaomicron to 71% for Klebsiella michiganensis), tracking reference-genome quality and Fitness Browser coverage depth [\[15\]](#references). Twelve of the 14 organisms are Proteobacteria, and the sole Bacteroidetes, B. thetaiotaomicron, had the lowest resolution rate—a phylogenetic bias caveat that limits generalisation to distant clades [\[16\]](#references).

The baseline FBA accuracy before integration is informative: across 574 organism-carbon-source combinations, FBA achieves 42.5% accuracy with high recall (86.5%) but low precision (42.5%), confirming the commonly observed over-prediction problem in draft metabolic models [\[17\]](#references).

**Acinetobacter ADP1 Explorer** used the KBase biochemistry layer differently: as the backbone for a six-modality multi-omics SQLite database (15 tables, 461,522 rows, 135 MB) that combines genome annotations, FBA flux predictions, RB-TnSeq essentiality, proteomics, growth phenotypes, and pangenome cluster assignments for the model organism Acinetobacter baylyi ADP1 [\[18\]](#references). Because ADP1 is absent from the public Fitness Browser, its condition-specific mutant growth data on 8 carbon sources is unique—not available anywhere else in BERDL [\[19\]](#references). Four of five connection types between the ADP1 database and the BERDL data lakehouse matched at over 90% (including 100% of genome IDs and compounds, and 91% of reactions), demonstrating deep integration [\[20\]](#references). The pangenome cluster ID bridge was non-trivial to construct: mmseqs2-style cluster IDs in the ADP1 database had 0% direct string match to BERDL centroid gene IDs, requiring a gene junction table to achieve a complete 4,891-to-4,081 mapping [\[21\]](#references).

Core metabolism across the 14 ADP1 genomes is strikingly conserved: 1,248 of 1,330 unique reactions (94%) are shared by all genomes, with only 20 genome-unique reactions [\[22\]](#references). FBA flux predictions and TnSeq essentiality calls agreed for 73.8% of the 866 genes with both measurements; the discordant 26% are candidates for model refinement or regulatory explanations not captured in the stoichiometric framework [\[23\]](#references). Essential genes are concentrated in the core pangenome and are dramatically more annotation-rich than dispensable ones: 33% carry COG assignments versus 5%, and 92% carry KEGG KO assignments versus 53% [\[24\]](#references). The ~8% of essential genes that lack KO annotations are particularly interesting as potential novel functions [\[25\]](#references). Gene essentiality is media-dependent: 499 genes are essential on minimal media versus 346 on rich LB, reflecting the additional biosynthetic burden imposed when external nutrients are withdrawn [\[26\]](#references). A key caveat is that the underlying RB-TnSeq experiments were conducted in rich media, so biosynthetic genes may appear non-essential when they are in fact required for minimal-media growth [\[27\]](#references).

**Essential Metabolome** applied GapMind across a 7-organism panel focused on free-living bacteria, querying which amino acid biosynthesis and carbon utilization pathways are complete. Seventeen of 18 amino acid biosynthesis pathways are present in all 7 organisms, with serine being the single exception: Desulfovibrio vulgaris (DvH) appears to lack a complete serine biosynthesis pathway [\[28\]](#references). This near-universality supports the hypothesis that amino acid prototrophy (the ability to synthesise all amino acids from inorganic precursors) is the ancestral state for free-living bacteria [\[29\]](#references). However, the data show near-universal rather than strictly universal pathway completeness, with organism-specific gaps [\[30\]](#references). TCA cycle intermediates, fermentation products, and amino acid catabolism were conserved across all 7 organisms as shared central catabolic capacity [\[31\]](#references).

The DvH serine finding is flagged with appropriate uncertainty. The apparent auxotrophy is ecologically plausible—DvH lives in anaerobic, amino-acid-rich sediments, biofilms, and intestinal tracts where scavenging serine from the environment would be advantageous and genomic economy would favour losing the biosynthetic pathway [\[32\]](#references). But the detection itself is a medium-confidence call: DvH could use a non-canonical pathway or divergent enzymes that fall below GapMind's homology threshold, producing a false-positive auxotrophy signal [\[33\]](#references). Direct experimental validation has not been done [\[34\]](#references).

**FW300 Metabolic Consistency** stress-tested how well the KBase biochemistry layer, WoM exometabolomics, Fitness Browser fitness data, and BacDive phenotype records agree for a single well-characterised organism, Pseudomonas FW300-N2E3. Across the testable metabolites this organism produces, all four databases agreed at a mean concordance score of 0.94, with no fully discordant metabolites [\[35\]](#references). Malate, arginine, and valine achieved gold-standard four-way consistency—produced in WoM, fitness-supported in Fitness Browser, species-utilizable in BacDive, and computationally complete in GapMind [\[36\]](#references). All 13 GapMind-matched metabolites had complete predicted pathways and all showed growth in Fitness Browser experiments, validating GapMind prediction accuracy for this organism [\[37\]](#references). Across 21 produced metabolites with matching Fitness Browser experiments, 601 unique genes showed significant fitness effects, generating 4,764 total significant gene-condition hits [\[38\]](#references).

The most biologically interesting discordance is tryptophan: FW300-N2E3 produces it and can grow on it as a sole carbon source, yet no BacDive P. fluorescens strain can catabolize it—a pattern consistent with overflow metabolism or active secretion for cross-feeding or signaling rather than catabolic use [\[39\]](#references). This is the only metabolite reaching high confidence for a genuine production-versus-utilization discordance; other BacDive discordances rest on small per-strain samples [\[40\]](#references). Tryptophan secretion by a prototroph that cannot re-assimilate it is a hallmark of cross-feeding potential, suggesting it may supply essential amino acids to auxotrophic community members [\[41\]](#references).

A key conceptual distinction reinforced by this project: metabolite production and utilization measure fundamentally different capabilities [\[42\]](#references). A bacterium can secrete a metabolite via overflow metabolism without being able to catabolize it for energy, and this distinction matters for interpreting any cross-collection comparison. Only 21 of 58 WoM metabolites could be tested against any other database, and just 3 achieved four-way coverage, leaving 64% of the exometabolomic space untested [\[43\]](#references). The fitness genes shared across all metabolite conditions are amino acid biosynthesis enzymes—pleiotropic housekeeping functions rather than substrate-specific catabolic machinery [\[44\]](#references).

**Web of Microbes Explorer** examined the WoM database itself as a cross-collection bridge resource. It is the first project to systematically compare WoM exometabolomic profiles with Fitness Browser gene fitness data, despite both coming from the same ENIGMA/Arkin Lab research programme [\[45\]](#references). The WoM action encoding uses four semantic categories (produced, consumed, not detected, missing), but the 2018 snapshot available in BERDL records only production events [\[46\]](#references). The database is small—37 organisms (20 with experimental data)—and only E. coli BW25113 has Fitness Browser coverage, with just 12 WoM observations focused narrowly on sulfur and cysteine metabolism [\[47\]](#references). At the database scale, 22–41% of compounds remain unidentified [\[48\]](#references). Despite these gaps, the hypothesis that WoM provides actionable cross-collection links is partially supported: real connections exist to Fitness Browser, ModelSEED, and the pangenome, offset by the no-consumption limitation and the small single-laboratory dataset [\[49\]](#references). Obtaining the current GNPS2 or Northen lab WoM dataset (the de Raad et al. 2022 NLDM 110-organism panel) would resolve the consumption gap and increase organism count several-fold [\[50\]](#references).

**CF Formulation Design** used metabolic data from the KBase biochemistry layer to design a commensal bacterial community that suppresses Pseudomonas aeruginosa in the cystic fibrosis (CF) airway by competitive metabolic exclusion. Lung-adapted P. aeruginosa undergoes metabolic streamlining—losing sugar catabolism pathways under relaxed selection in amino-acid-rich sputum—while its amino acid catabolism becomes an invariant evolutionary adaptation [\[51\]](#references). The formulation strategy targets exactly these conserved amino acid catabolic pathways, which are 97.4% conserved across 1,796 lung PA genomes [\[52\]](#references). Metabolic carbon-source overlap with PA14 significantly predicts planktonic inhibition (r = 0.384) but explains only ~27% of variance, confirming that metabolic competition is necessary but not sufficient [\[53\]](#references). No individual commensal outgrows PA14 on any tested carbon substrate [\[54\]](#references), so full amino acid niche coverage of PA14 requires a community: a three-organism core (M. luteus, N. mucosa, S. salivarius) achieves full niche coverage first [\[55\]](#references), and the final five-organism formulation (Neisseria mucosa, Streptococcus salivarius, Micrococcus luteus, Rothia dentocariosa, Gemella sanguinis) achieves 100% amino acid niche coverage with 78% mean inhibition [\[56\]](#references). GapMind pangenome analysis confirmed that these metabolic capabilities are species-level traits conserved above 95% across hundreds of genomes, supporting strain-agnostic formulation design [\[57\]](#references).

Important caveats surround the CF formulation work. All inhibition assays used PA14, an ExoU+ Pel-only strain representing under 5% of CF PA isolates; the dominant ExoS+/PAO1-type clinical strains have not been tested [\[58\]](#references). All assays were planktonic, whereas PA14 grows primarily in biofilms in the CF lung where metabolic dynamics and spatial structure differ substantially [\[59\]](#references). Micrococcus luteus is the keystone species for full niche coverage yet has zero detected patient engraftability and no lung genomes, creating a design tension [\[60\]](#references). The multivariate model of inhibition also overfits the training cohort (cross-validated R² ~0.145 vs. training R² 0.274) [\[61\]](#references).

**IBD Phage Targeting** illustrates how KBase biochemistry data intersects with a disease-stratification problem. A within-substudy confound-free meta-analysis across four IBD cohorts recovers the canonical Crohn's Disease (CD) signature of pathobionts elevated and protective commensals reduced [\[62\]](#references). Among pathobionts, four IBD ecotypes structure the patient population and determine cocktail requirements [\[63\]](#references); only E. coli (the adherent-invasive E. coli, AIEC, subset) carries the iron-siderophore and colibactin genotoxin biosynthetic gene cluster signature [\[64\]](#references). A greedy minimum-set-cover phage cocktail design achieves 94.7% coverage of 188 E. coli strains tested [\[65\]](#references). A key practical finding: phage cocktails designed at the cohort level will mismatch individual patients unless each patient's microbiome ecotype is determined first [\[66\]](#references). A patient's ecotype can also drift between visits (Jaccard similarity 0.60), motivating state-dependent rather than fixed cocktail design [\[67\]](#references). The two highest-priority CD pathobionts—H. hathewayi and M. gnavus—have the weakest phage availability, creating a coverage gap precisely where it is most needed [\[68\]](#references).

## Connections

The KBase MSD Biochemistry data page is adjacent to several topic pages in this wiki because it provides the infrastructure that makes those topics computationally tractable. The [Metabolic Pathways](../topics/metabolic-pathways.md) topic page gathers evidence about which specific pathways (amino acid biosynthesis, central catabolism, carbon utilization) are conserved or disrupted—evidence that is assembled by querying the GapMind and ModelSEED layers described here. The [Gene Fitness](../topics/gene-fitness.md) topic page synthesises RB-TnSeq results that are only interpretable when linked to metabolic reaction annotations from this collection. The [Functional Dark Matter](../topics/functional-dark-matter.md) topic page describes unannotated genes that remain dark precisely because the biochemistry reference databases lack the enzyme characterisations needed to close their function—the dark reactions discussed above are a direct manifestation. The [Pangenome Architecture](../topics/pangenome-architecture.md) and [Adp1 Model System](../topics/adp1-model-system.md) pages both rely on the pangenome cluster tables that are linked to biochemistry reaction assignments through the same KBase infrastructure.

## Caveats and Open Directions

Several structural limitations recur across all projects that use this collection and deserve explicit enumeration for engineers building on these results.

Gapfilling quality is the dominant uncertainty. With 87% of predictions gapfilling-dependent and knockout validation circular when gapfilling is required for in silico growth, improving draft model quality—either by curating more gene-reaction associations or by developing better gapfilling algorithms—would propagate improvements across every downstream analysis [\[1\]](#references).

The E. coli coverage gap is a notable omission: E. coli genomes were excluded from the KBase pangenome GapMind pilot because too many genomes exist for species-level GTDB analysis, restricting the pilot to seven organisms rather than the intended 45 [\[69\]](#references). Given that E. coli Keio is one of the richest Fitness Browser organisms, this gap limits the ability to validate biochemistry predictions for the most intensively studied bacterial model system.

The WoM exometabolomics snapshot is the bottleneck for cross-collection metabolite linking. Its small size (37 organisms), production-only action encoding, and condition mismatch with Fitness Browser all limit its utility [\[6\]](#references). Upgrading to the current GNPS2 or de Raad et al. 2022 NLDM dataset would substantially expand the usable metabolite space [\[50\]](#references). Similarly, extending EggNOG orthology coverage and expanding BacDive phenotype matching would help close the annotation gaps that currently prevent many gapfilled reactions from being resolved [\[70\]](#references).

Community metabolic modelling—simulating how multiple organisms interact metabolically rather than modelling each in isolation—is identified as an open opportunity for the freshwater isolate community studied in the FW300 project [\[71\]](#references). The metabolite cross-feeding signal (tryptophan overflow from FW300-N2E3, potential consumption by auxotrophs) cannot be tested with current data because consumption data is absent from WoM and community-level metabolic models do not yet exist for this community.
