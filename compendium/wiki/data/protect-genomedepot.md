# Protect Genomedepot

The PROTECT GenomeDepot is a shared multi-omic data collection that underpins two distinct microbiome-engineering projects in this corpus: one designing a commensal probiotic formulation to suppress *Pseudomonas aeruginosa* in cystic fibrosis (CF) airways, the other designing precision phage cocktails to target Crohn's disease (CD) pathobionts. The page exists in this wiki because both projects draw from the same underlying reservoir of isolate genomes, patient metagenomes, and curated reference databases — making the collection the connective tissue that links an airway-engineering problem to an inflammatory-bowel-disease problem, despite their apparent clinical distance.

## Overview

At its core, PROTECT GenomeDepot supplies genome-resolved, clinically annotated microbiome data that neither project could interrogate without it. Its scope spans hundreds of isolate genomes from CF-associated commensals, thousands of patient metagenomic samples from IBD and healthy cohorts, pangenome collections for both target pathogens and probiotic candidates, and curated viral and pathway reference databases.

**CF probiotic project.** The central insight driving the CF probiotic work is that *P. aeruginosa* can potentially be suppressed through metabolic competitive exclusion — commensal organisms colonizing the lung before the pathogen and consuming the same amino acid carbon sources. This strategy, sometimes called "eating the pathogen's lunch," requires knowing precisely which substrates PA14 (the *P. aeruginosa* lab reference strain) depends on, and whether any commensal can outcompete it on those substrates. The analysis spanning 430 isolates tested across 21 carbon sources established that metabolic carbon-source overlap with PA14 significantly predicts planktonic inhibition (r = 0.384), yet explains only about 27% of variance, meaning the strongest inhibitors also deploy direct antagonism mechanisms beyond simple nutrient competition [\[1\]](#references). Critically, no single commensal outgrows PA14 on any tested carbon substrate, so competitive exclusion requires community-level niche coverage rather than a single dominant strain [\[2\]](#references).

Multi-criterion formulation optimization over the collection identified a five-organism FDA-safe formulation — *Neisseria mucosa*, *Streptococcus salivarius*, *Micrococcus luteus*, *Rothia dentocariosa*, and *Gemella sanguinis* — that together achieve 100% coverage of PA14's amino acid niche at 78% mean inhibition [\[3\]](#references). Full niche coverage is first reached at three organisms (including *M. luteus*, *N. mucosa*, and *S. salivarius*), where at least one member grows on every amino acid PA14 can exploit [\[4\]](#references). Pangenome analysis using GapMind — a tool that maps genomic pathway completeness across hundreds of genomes — confirmed that these metabolic capabilities are species-level traits conserved above 95% across hundreds of genomes, supporting a strain-agnostic formulation design [\[5\]](#references). Two anchor species, *R. dentocariosa* and *N. mucosa*, are naturally lung-adapted, with 33–38% of their respective pangenome genomes originating from respiratory sources, not gut [\[6\]](#references). Among inhibition-tested species, *N. mucosa* also has the highest engraftability score — a composite of patient metagenome prevalence and metatranscriptomic activity — marking it as the most promising colonization anchor [\[7\]](#references).

Growth kinetics from 32 isolates revealed an important asymmetry: although commensals rarely exceed PA14's maximum growth rate, they begin growing before PA14 on 43.1% of substrate comparisons, suggesting that pre-establishing the commensal community before pathogen exposure — for instance through prebiotic biomass loading — may matter more than raw growth rate competition [\[8\]](#references). However, PA14 outgrows the average commensal on every tested amino acid and simple sugar, so no single tested substrate works as a selective prebiotic [\[9\]](#references). The solution pointed to by GapMind pathway analysis is sugar alcohols: xylitol, myoinositol, and pentoses such as xylose, arabinose, fucose, and rhamnose are genomically complete in the formulation species but absent from PA14's metabolic repertoire, making them candidate selective prebiotics [\[10\]](#references). Validating this prediction — by testing the five core species plus PA14 on these substrates as sole carbon sources — is flagged as the next key experiment [\[11\]](#references).

Pangenome analysis of 6,760 *P. aeruginosa* genomes also revealed that lung-adapted PA undergoes metabolic streamlining: sugar catabolism pathways (sorbitol, mannitol, gluconate) are lost under relaxed selection in the amino-acid-rich sputum environment, while amino acid catabolism remains an invariant evolutionary adaptation [\[12\]](#references). As a direct consequence, the amino-acid catabolic pathways the formulation targets are 97.4% conserved across 1,796 lung PA genomes, predicting equivalent suppression across lung variants including CF-derived strains [\[13\]](#references).

**IBD phage-targeting project.** The GenomeDepot also supplies the data foundation for an entirely different therapeutic strategy: rationally designed bacteriophage cocktails targeting Crohn's disease pathobionts. The framework begins from the premise that IBD microbiomes are clinically heterogeneous, and that cohort-level differential-abundance analysis obscures reproducible microbiome subtypes, or ecotypes, that carry distinct pathobiont signatures. Phage cocktails designed at the cohort level will mismatch individual patients unless each patient's microbiome ecotype is determined first [\[14\]](#references).

Two independent clustering methods — Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA, a probabilistic topic model) and Gaussian Mixture Models (GMM) — applied to 8,489 MetaPhlAn3 metagenomic samples converge on four reproducible IBD microbiome ecotypes (E0 through E3), with K=4 selected by a cross-method Adjusted Rand Index (ARI) parsimony rule [\[15\]](#references). A confound-free within-substudy meta-analysis across four IBD cohorts then recovers the canonical CD signature — pathobionts elevated, protective commensals depleted — with strong sign concordance across studies [\[16\]](#references). The 23 UC Davis Crohn's patients distribute non-randomly across three ecotypes (none in the *Prevotella*-dominant E2), with active disease concentrated in the E1 and E3 ecotypes [\[17\]](#references).

Iron acquisition emerges as a pathobiont-defining genomic capability. Tier-A pathobiont genomes are strongly enriched for iron-siderophore biosynthetic gene clusters (BGCs — genomic loci encoding complete biosynthetic pathways for small molecules), with a Fisher odds ratio of 44.4 [\[18\]](#references). Among the six actionable Tier-A pathobionts, only *E. coli* (specifically the adherent-invasive AIEC strain subset) carries the iron-siderophore and genotoxin BGC signature, localizing iron-driven Crohn's biology to that species [\[19\]](#references). For *E. coli*, a greedy minimum-set-cover design over experimentally tested phage-strain susceptibility data in PhageFoundry yields a 5-phage cocktail covering 94.7% of 188 *E. coli* strains [\[20\]](#references). The Microviridae member Gokushovirus WZ-2015a is also robustly depleted in CD across multiple ecotypes, independent of the pathobiont targets [\[21\]](#references).

However, the two highest-priority Crohn's pathobionts by evidence score — *H. hathewayi* and *M. gnavus* — have the weakest phage availability, creating a coverage gap precisely where the greatest benefit would be expected [\[22\]](#references). The result is that no E1 Crohn's patient can be treated with a pure phage cocktail; only a hybrid combining phages for some targets with non-phage alternatives for the gap and temperate-only targets is currently feasible [\[23\]](#references). Querying external databases — INPHARED (~25K phages) and IMG/VR (~3M uncultivated viral genomes) — could close this gap for *H. hathewayi*, *F. plautii*, and *M. gnavus*, potentially converting the hybrid framework into a pure-phage cocktail for some patients [\[24\]](#references).

A practical operational finding from integrating the multi-cohort GenomeDepot data is that a taxonomy synonymy layer — an NCBI taxid plus GTDB-version-aware rename table — is necessary infrastructure for any multi-cohort microbiome project. Without it, divergent taxon-name formats between cohorts fragment the same species into non-overlapping rows, producing artifactual log2FC signals [\[25\]](#references).

## Projects Using This Collection

**cf_formulation_design** uses the GenomeDepot's isolate genomes, patient metagenomes, and pangenome databases to design a CF airway probiotic. Key intermediate results include the multivariate metabolic-feature model of inhibition, which overfits the 142-isolate training cohort (cross-validated R² ≈ 0.145 rather than the training R² of 0.274), so its true out-of-sample predictive power is closer to 15% [\[26\]](#references). The project also found that measuring pairwise interactions between all 10 combinations of the five core species is the highest-priority validation experiment, since a single untested antagonistic pair could invalidate the additive formulation scoring assumption [\[27\]](#references).

**ibd_phage_targeting** uses the GenomeDepot's clinical metagenomes, reference phage databases, and viral metagenomes to define ecotype-specific phage cocktail designs. A key methodological finding is that in pooled curatedMetagenomicData, healthy and Crohn's samples come from disjoint source studies, making a pooled case-vs-control mixed model structurally unidentifiable (the random effect perfectly predicts diagnosis), forcing a within-substudy CD-vs-nonIBD design instead [\[28\]](#references). Clinical covariates can separate healthy from IBD samples but cannot distinguish the transitional E1 from the severe E3 ecotype, so metagenomics remains required for per-patient ecotype assignment [\[29\]](#references). Longitudinal analysis of a single patient whose ecotype drifted between E1 and E3 across visits shows that the optimal cocktail composition changes moderately (Jaccard similarity 0.60), motivating state-dependent cocktail re-design rather than a fixed prescription [\[30\]](#references). The E3 ecotype Tier-A target list rests on single-study evidence and should be treated as provisional until a cohort with sufficient E3 patients and both diagnosis groups becomes available [\[31\]](#references).

### Caveats and Open Directions

Both projects are bounded by important experimental and analytical limitations that the GenomeDepot data cannot resolve on its own.

For the CF project, all inhibition assays measure planktonic competition, whereas PA14 in CF lungs grows primarily in biofilms where metabolic dynamics, diffusion gradients, and spatial structure differ substantially [\[32\]](#references). Furthermore, the inhibition assays used PA14 — an ExoU+, Pel-only strain representing under 5% of CF PA isolates — and the measurements have not been validated against the dominant ExoS+/PAO1-type clinical strains, even though pangenome analysis confirms amino acid catabolism is identical across both variants [\[33\]](#references). T3SS effector typing of 6,760 PA genomes confirms that CF isolates are overwhelmingly ExoS+ (94%), underscoring the gap [\[34\]](#references). The most critical design tension is *M. luteus*: it is the keystone species for full niche coverage yet has zero detected patient engraftability and no lung genomes in the pangenome, raising the question of whether metabolic importance can compensate for low natural prevalence [\[35\]](#references).

For the IBD project, the E3 Tier-A target list is provisional pending additional cohort data [\[31\]](#references), and the phage coverage gap for the top two pathobionts means the current clinical translation roadmap depends on finding phages that do not yet exist in curated databases [\[22\]](#references).
