# Kbase Ke Pangenome

## Overview

The KBase KE Pangenome is a large-scale comparative genomics resource — a cross-species pangenome database built from genomes deposited in KBase and organized according to GTDB taxonomy — that serves as the shared reference backbone for the entire BERDL research observatory. A *pangenome* is the full repertoire of genes found across a set of related genomes: the *core genome* contains genes present in nearly all members of a species (typically ≥95% prevalence), while the *accessory genome* contains genes present in only a subset. The KBase KE Pangenome collection extends this concept to the kingdom level, clustering gene sequences across tens of thousands of bacterial and archaeal species so that any project can ask, for a gene of interest, whether it is universally conserved or ecologically specialized.

This page exists in the wiki because virtually every project in this corpus queries the pangenome collection to give conservation context to their biological findings. The collection spans approximately 293,059 genomes [\[1\]](#references) across 27,690 species [\[2\]](#references), making it the single largest substrate shared across projects. A single Pseudomonas sub-study, for example, drew GapMind carbon-pathway predictions for 12,732 genomes across 433 Pseudomonas species clades from this collection alone [\[3\]](#references).

## Projects Using This Collection

All 50 projects in this corpus access the KBase KE Pangenome, but they do so for remarkably different purposes, which reveals how versatile a cross-species gene-cluster resource can be.

**Genome-scale integration and bridging.** The Acinetobacter ADP1 Explorer project demonstrates the depth of integration possible. It connected a user-curated SQLite database of 5,852 genes spanning six data modalities — fitness, essentiality, proteomics, metabolic models, functional annotations, and growth phenotypes — to the BERDL lakehouse via the pangenome [\[4\]](#references). The connection required a gene junction table that mapped mmseqs2-style cluster IDs in the ADP1 database to BERDL centroid gene IDs, achieving 100% coverage of 4,891 BERDL clusters despite zero direct string matches [\[5\]](#references). Once bridged, four of five connection types (genome IDs, compounds, reactions, and gene clusters) matched at over 90%, confirming deep integration [\[6\]](#references). The ADP1 project then showed that core metabolism is highly conserved across 14 *Acinetobacter* genomes, with 94% of unique reactions shared by all members [\[7\]](#references), and that essential genes are preferentially located in the core pangenome [\[8\]](#references).

**Gene fitness and pangenome conservation.** Multiple projects cross-reference fitness data (from RB-TnSeq, a pooled-mutant fitness assay where barcoded transposon libraries are grown in different conditions and each gene's fitness effect is quantified by sequencing) with pangenome conservation to ask what drives gene retention. The key finding is that fitness importance predicts conservation regardless of condition type: universally important genes are significantly more core than neutral genes (OR=1.35) [\[9\]](#references), and this pattern holds whether the fitness data comes from laboratory or field-relevant conditions [\[10\]](#references). The ADP1 deletion phenotype project extends this by showing that the 272 missing dispensable genes are significantly less conserved across *Acinetobacter* species, linking gene dispensability to pangenome status through evolutionary retention pressure [\[11\]](#references).

**Antimicrobial resistance at pangenome scale.** Several projects use the collection to study how antimicrobial resistance (AMR) genes are distributed across the core-versus-accessory divide. The AMR Pangenome Atlas quantified this for the first time simultaneously across 14,723 species, finding that AMR genes are markedly depleted from the core genome (30.3% core vs. 46.8% pangenome baseline, OR=0.49) with a 2.2-fold enrichment in the accessory genome [\[12\]](#references). This depletion is consistent across 63.7% of the 4,252 tested species [\[13\]](#references). The AMR strain variation project extended this to within-species resolution, becoming the first study to systematically quantify within-species AMR variation across more than 1,300 species simultaneously, enabled by the pangenome resource [\[14\]](#references). Prophage-AMR co-mobilization was analyzed at comparable scale in the first pangenome-level study of prophage-AMR gene-neighborhood co-localization, spanning 100 species, 1,953 genomes, and 36,041 AMR gene instances [\[15\]](#references).

**Functional dark matter and annotation gaps.** The functional dark matter project queried the full 27,690-species pangenome to show that over half of dark gene ortholog groups (genes with no known function) are pan-bacterial at the kingdom level, demonstrating that annotation gaps are not a minor problem but a fundamental limitation in understanding broadly conserved biology [\[2\]](#references). This finding motivated a separate re-annotation effort: Bakta v1.12.0 reclassifies 83.7% of pangenome-linked dark genes as no longer hypothetical [\[16\]](#references), suggesting that many "dark" genes are simply annotation-vintage artifacts rather than genuinely unknown biology. The annotation gap discovery project further integrated pangenome conservation as one of five evidence streams — alongside gapfilling, fitness, GapMind pathway scores, and BLAST homology — to resolve metabolic annotation gaps simultaneously across multiple organisms [\[17\]](#references).

**COG functional partitioning.** The COG analysis project used the pangenome to measure functional category distributions of core versus accessory (novel) genes across 32 diverse species. Core genes are consistently enriched in ancient metabolic and biosynthetic functions — nucleotide and coenzyme metabolism — relative to novel genes [\[18\]](#references), supporting the view that the core genome represents a conserved metabolic engine while the accessory genome contains recent ecological adaptations [\[19\]](#references). This two-speed functional partitioning holds universally across bacterial phyla [\[20\]](#references).

**Costly and dispensable genes.** The costly dispensable genes project became the first systematic cross-organism characterization of genes that are simultaneously metabolically burdensome and not conserved in the pangenome, spanning 142,190 genes across 43 bacteria [\[21\]](#references). The pangenome conservation dimension helped reveal that these costly-but-dispensable genes represent the genomic debris of horizontal gene transfer (HGT) — insertion sequences, prophage remnants, transposases, and defense systems that impose metabolic cost without providing species-wide benefit [\[22\]](#references).

**Defense systems and ecological traits.** The SNIPE defense system project used the pangenome to measure the phylogenetic distribution and core/accessory status of a newly characterized anti-phage defense domain. The SNIPE domain (DUF4041, PF13250) was detected in 4,572 gene clusters across 1,696 species spanning 33 bacterial and archaeal phyla, far exceeding the published estimate [\[23\]](#references). Only 13.3% of SNIPE genes are core; the remaining 86.7% are accessory or singletons, consistent with HGT-mediated spread of defense islands [\[24\]](#references).

**External database bridging.** The BacDive metal validation project linked phenotypic culture data from BacDive to pangenome metal tolerance scores, connecting 42,227 BacDive strains (43.4% of 97,334) to 6,426 unique GTDB species [\[25\]](#references). Species name matching was the primary join key, leaving 56.6% of strains unmatched — a gap that direct GCA accession matching via Spark could substantially reduce.

## Caveats and Open Directions

Several important limitations apply when interpreting results based on the KBase KE Pangenome. The core/accessory classification is inherently binary: a gene is labeled core (≥95% prevalence) or accessory, discarding the quantitative information about exactly how prevalent a gene is [\[26\]](#references). For species with few sequenced genomes — the median Fitness Browser species has only 9 genomes — the 95% threshold is imprecise and core-versus-accessory comparisons should be interpreted cautiously [\[27\]](#references). External database bridging introduces additional coverage gaps: AlphaEarth embeddings cover only 28.4% of the 293,059-genome database and are biased toward genomes with geographic metadata [\[1\]](#references). *E. coli* genomes are completely absent from GapMind species-level analysis within BERDL because the number of *E. coli* genomes exceeded Spark's per-query size limit [\[28\]](#references), and *Klebsiella pneumoniae* was excluded from an ecotype study for a similar reason. Annotation-vintage bias also matters: dark gene counts derived from early Fitness Browser annotations overestimate the truly unannotated fraction because modern re-annotation tools classify most as known [\[16\]](#references).

An open and tractable future direction is to test pangenome openness (the proportion of a species' genes that are accessory) as a predictor of ecological niche breadth, which could reveal whether a large accessory genome is the underlying driver of observed correlations between metal type diversity and habitat range [\[29\]](#references). Resolving the HGT origin of costly-but-dispensable genes in more detail and extending the AMR fitness cost analysis to a broader set of pangenome species are complementary next steps enabled by this collection.
