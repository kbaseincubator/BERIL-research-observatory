# Metal Fitness Atlas

The Metal Fitness Atlas assembled fitness data across 31 bacterial species and 16 metals, creating a comprehensive map of how genes respond to metal stress at scale. The project's defining discovery is that metal tolerance is fundamentally a core-genome robustness problem: genes with significant fitness defects under metal stress are enriched in conserved core genes (87.4% core) rather than concentrated in specialized accessory resistance pathways, upending prior assumptions about how bacteria survive metal toxicity. This reframing identified 1,182 conserved metal-response gene families across multiple organisms, including 149 novel candidates prioritized by their cross-species fitness signatures.

## Key findings

- Genome-size-normalized pangenome scoring suggests that metal tolerance genes are broadly distributed across bacteria rather than concentrated only in specialist taxa. *(confidence: high)*
- Cross-species metal fitness defects are enriched in core genes, reframing metal tolerance as a core-genome robustness problem rather than mainly an accessory-resistance problem. *(confidence: high)*
- The metal atlas identifies conserved metal-phenotype gene families, including novel candidates that can be prioritized from cross-species fitness evidence. *(confidence: high)*
