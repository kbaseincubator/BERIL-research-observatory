# Ecotype Functional Differentiation

This project sampled 12 bacterial species to test whether within-species ecotypes (gene-content subpopulations) differ in predictable, biologically meaningful ways across functional gene categories. The core finding is that ecotype gene-content variation is not random—adaptive functions like transport and defense differentiate 2.1× more strongly than housekeeping functions, and this pattern holds consistently across species, though much of the strongest ecotype signal involves genes of unknown function and mobile genetic elements that remain biologically cryptic.

## Key findings

- The strongest ecotype differences include unknown-function and mobile-element categories, so much of the niche-specific signal remains hard to interpret biologically. *(confidence: high)*
- Adaptive functions differentiate more strongly between ecotypes than housekeeping functions, although both classes can vary. *(confidence: high)*
- Ecotype gene-content variation is functionally structured rather than random, with COG categories differentiating across all sampled species. *(confidence: high)*
- Gene-content ecotypes are widespread in the sampled bacterial species and represent recurring within-species pangenome structure. *(confidence: high)*
