# Metal Cross Resistance

This project systematically characterized how bacterial genes respond to multiple metal stresses at gene resolution across diverse organisms, testing whether resistance to one metal predicts resistance to another. The central finding is that metal cross-resistance is overwhelmingly universal: 98.1% of gene-level fitness correlations between metal pairs are positive and statistically significant, revealing that bacteria share a fundamental genetic basis for tolerating different metals due to overlapping stress mechanisms. This universal signal operates alongside a chemistry-specific layer where the strength of cross-resistance varies by metal pair, reflecting how chemically similar metals compete for the same cellular binding sites.

## Key findings

- BacDive validation of multi-metal scores is underpowered at the Fitness Browser organism scale. *(confidence: high)*
- Metal cross-resistance has a universal stress layer and a chemistry-specific magnitude layer, with divalent cations showing especially strong shared responses. *(confidence: high)*
- Gene-level metal cross-resistance is directionally conserved: metal-pair fitness correlations are overwhelmingly positive across organisms. *(confidence: high)*
- Metal-important genes form a conservation gradient from general stress genes through metal-shared genes to metal-specific genes. *(confidence: high)*
