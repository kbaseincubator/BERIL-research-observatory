# Metal Specificity

This project identified and characterized metal-specific genes—genes that show fitness defects under metal stress but not in general non-metal experiments—across 24 microbial organisms. Among 7,609 metal-important genes analyzed, 55% are genuinely metal-specific, while the remainder are either broadly stress-responsive or metal-stress-only genes, a distinction robust across detection thresholds. Metal-specific genes remain core-enriched (84.8% core) but less strongly than general sick genes, and they are 1.64x more likely to carry metal-resistance annotations, confirming that the specificity filter captures biologically meaningful tolerance determinants rather than noise.

## Key findings

- Metal-specific genes remain core-enriched, but less strongly than general sick genes, preserving the core-genome robustness model while exposing a modest accessory contribution. *(confidence: high)*
- A majority of analyzed metal-important gene records are metal-specific rather than generally sick across non-metal conditions. *(confidence: high)*
- Metal-specific genes are enriched for metal-resistance annotations, indicating that the specificity filter captures biologically meaningful tolerance determinants. *(confidence: high)*
