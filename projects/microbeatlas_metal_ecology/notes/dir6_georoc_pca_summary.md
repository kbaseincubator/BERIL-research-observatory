# Direction 6: Multi-metal GeoROC PCA — 2026-04-04

## Hypothesis
A composite multi-metal loading score (PC1 from PCA of GeoROC rock geochemistry)
should predict desert richness suppression more strongly than any single metal
or MRDS mine distance alone.

## Metals analyzed
Cu, Zn, Pb, Cd, Co, Ni, Cr (As excluded: only n=20 non-null in desert)

## Key Findings

- **Individual GeoROC metals are far stronger univariate predictors than MRDS** (ρ~0.6 vs ρ=0.148), suggesting the actual geochemical content of the rock matters more than the categorical fact of mine proximity.
- **Surprising Co/Ni positive correlations** (ρ=+0.633, +0.546): higher Co/Ni ppm predicts higher richness. This is counterintuitive for toxicity but may reflect a geological signature — mafic/ultramafic rocks (high Co/Ni) are geologically distinct from Cu/Zn/Pb ore deposits.
- **PC1 (uniform metal loading) is NOT univariately predictive** (ρ=-0.068, p=0.405): despite Cu/Zn/Pb being negative and Co/Ni being positive, their combined PC1 score averages out to near-zero correlation. The relevant signal is metal-type-specific, not total loading.
- **R²=0.968 in PC1+MRDS model is suspicious**: the n=136 GeoROC-annotated subset with MRDS data may be heavily biased (likely near-mine sites at specific geological formations). Treat with caution.
- **GeoROC coverage is limited** (45–57% of desert samples for Cu/Zn/Co/Pb; only 7% for all 7 metals). The multi-metal PCA is a small, potentially unrepresentative subset.

## Critical Issues

1. **Sign conflict in PCA**: Cu/Zn/Pb/Cr are negative predictors, Co/Ni are positive predictors. PCA forces these onto a single axis, producing a near-zero PC1-richness correlation that masks the real structure.
2. **Geologically biased subset**: the 152 samples with all 7 metals likely come from specific geological surveys near known ore deposits.
3. **Confounded with rock type**: GeoROC rock chemistry reflects parent rock type, not contamination per se. High Pb sites may be naturally occurring ore-bearing rocks, not pollution.

**Disprover required**: Test whether the metal-richness correlations survive within rock type categories (mafic vs. felsic vs. sedimentary). If Co/Ni are high in mafic rocks that happen to host more productive desert soils, the effect is geological not toxicological.
