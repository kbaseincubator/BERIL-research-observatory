# Direction 4: EPA TRI + NPL (Superfund) vs. MRDS Mine Proximity — 2026-04-04

## Hypothesis
Active industrial chemical releases (EPA TRI) and legacy Superfund contamination (EPA NPL)
should independently suppress microbial richness in desert soils, potentially additively
with or beyond the established MRDS mine-proximity effect.

## Key Findings

- **TRI dominates MRDS**: TRI nearest facility (ρ=+0.657) and carcinogen releases (ρ=-0.658)
  are 4× stronger predictors of desert richness than MRDS mine distance (ρ=+0.148).
- **TRI model explains more variance** (R²=0.461) than MRDS model (R²=0.273) on the overlapping
  n=667 samples that have TRI annotations.
- **MRDS becomes non-significant** (p=0.12) when TRI is added to the model, suggesting TRI
  subsumes or mediates the MRDS signal on the n=666 TRI-annotated subset.
- **However: TRI coverage is biased**. Only 667/2235 (30%) of desert samples have TRI annotations.
  The TRI-annotated subset may be geographically biased toward the USA (where EPA TRI operates).
- **NPL (Superfund) insufficient**: Only 80 desert samples have NPL data, too few for robust inference.
- **Carcinogen releases = facility count**: ρ(carcinogen, count)=0.999 — these are collinear; the
  carcinogen signal is driven by presence/density of TRI facilities, not specifically carcinogen toxicity.

## Critical Caveat

The TRI dataset applies primarily to US soil samples. The MicrobeAtlas desert dataset spans
global deserts (Namib, Sahara, Atacama, Gobi, etc.), but EPA TRI annotations only exist for
US sites. The TRI-annotated n=667 are likely a non-representative US-heavy subset, which could
explain both the stronger effect and the MRDS mediation — US desert sites near industrial
facilities may have fundamentally different ecologies than global mining sites.

**Disprover required**: Examine whether TRI-annotated samples are predominantly USA-located and
whether controlling for country/continent eliminates the TRI advantage over MRDS.
