# References: Gene-Environment Association Across Pangenome Species

## Pangenome Methods

- **Tettelin H, Masignani V, Cieslewicz MJ, et al.** (2005). Genome analysis of multiple pathogenic isolates of *Streptococcus agalactiae*: implications for the microbial "pan-genome." *PNAS*, 102(39), 13950-13955. -- Foundational paper defining the bacterial pangenome concept and the core/dispensable partition.

- **Gautreau G, Bazin A, Gachet M, Plber R, Koszul R, Medigue C, Vallenet D.** (2020). PPanGGOLiN: depicting microbial diversity via a partitioned pangenome graph. *PLoS Comput Biol*, 16(3), e1007732. -- Partitioned pangenome graphs distinguishing persistent, shell, and cloud gene families; our framework complements this by testing individual accessory clusters for environment-specific prevalence.

- **Brynildsrud O, Bohlin J, Scheffer L, Eldholm V.** (2016). Rapid scoring of genes in microbial pan-genome-wide association studies with Scoary. *Genome Biol*, 17, 238. -- Pan-GWAS tool scoring gene presence/absence for trait associations while accounting for population stratification; a natural next step for our framework.

- **Lees JA, Vehkala M, Valimaki N, et al.** (2018). pyseer: a comprehensive tool for microbial pangenome-wide association studies. *Bioinformatics*, 34(24), 4310-4312. -- Bacterial GWAS package with population structure correction via linear mixed models; more sophisticated alternative to our Fisher's exact approach.

## Pangenome Ecology

- **Brockhurst MA, Harrison E, Hall JPJ, Richards T, McNally A, MacLean C.** (2019). The ecology and evolution of pangenomes. *Curr Biol*, 29(20), R1094-R1103. -- Review of how environmental context shapes accessory genome content; framework for interpreting mobilome enrichment in environment-associated genes.

- **Maistrenko OM, Mende DR, Lassmann M, et al.** (2020). Disentangling the impact of environmental and phylogenetic constraints on prokaryotic within-species diversity. *ISME J*, 14(5), 1247-1259. -- Environmental preferences explained up to 49% of pangenome variance, stronger than phylogenetic inertia; validates our approach of linking gene cluster presence/absence to environmental metadata.

## Horizontal Gene Transfer and Niche Adaptation

- **Ochman H, Lawrence JG, Groisman EA.** (2000). Lateral gene transfer and the nature of bacterial innovation. *Nature*, 405(6784), 299-304. -- Seminal review establishing HGT as a primary driver of bacterial genome evolution and niche adaptation.

- **Koskella B, Brockhurst MA.** (2014). Bacteria-phage coevolution as a driver of ecological and evolutionary processes in microbial communities. *FEMS Microbiol Rev*, 38(5), 916-931. -- Mobile genetic elements (phages) as vehicles for accessory gene acquisition enabling rapid adaptation.

## S. aureus Ecology and Genomics

- **Richardson EJ, et al.** (2018). Gene exchange drives the ecological success of a multi-host bacterial pathogen. *Nat Ecol Evol*, 2(9), 1468-1478. -- Demonstrated that gene exchange, particularly via mobile genetic elements, enables *S. aureus* to colonize diverse host species and environments.

- **Weinert LA, Welch JJ, Suchard MA, Lemey P, Rambaut A, Fitzgerald JR.** (2012). Molecular dating of human-to-bovid host jumps by *Staphylococcus aureus* reveals an association with the spread of domestication. *Biol Lett*, 8(5), 829-832. -- Showed multiple host-switching events in *S. aureus* evolution, associated with human domestication of animals.

- **Fitzgerald JR.** (2012). Livestock-associated *Staphylococcus aureus*: origin, evolution and public health threat. *Trends Microbiol*, 20(4), 192-198. -- Reviews the emergence of livestock-adapted *S. aureus* clones and their zoonotic potential.

## Methylobacterium

- **Chistoserdova L, Kalyuzhnaya MG.** (2018). Current trends in methylotrophy. *Trends Microbiol*, 26(8), 703-714. -- Notes that xoxF genes are more widespread than mxaF across methylotrophic bacteria; our genome-level environmental analysis extends this to show no environment-specific MDH pattern.

- **Hesse C, et al.** (2022). Comprehensive phylogenomics of *Methylobacterium* reveals four evolutionary distinct groups and underappreciated phyllosphere diversity. *Genome Biol Evol*, 14(8), evac123. -- Analyzed 189 genomes; identified 45 candidate species, mostly phyllosphere-associated.
