# Speaker notes — substory `S5`

**Substory punchline:** Caulobacter's sphingolipid/ChvI route is taxonomically restricted — comparators lack it and use other routes.
**Throughline:** A single envelope catastrophe — lipid A loss — triggers five partially independent adaptive layers (Fur-released transport, ChvI two-phase signaling, constitutive sphingolipid substitution, peptidoglycan remodeling, and cross-species structural unavailability) that together explain how *and why* *Caulobacter* alone tolerates the deletion.
**Tier:** STRONG
**Mode:** talk-30

---

## position 0 — section_divider — `Caulobacter's sphingolipid/ChvI route is taxonomically restricted — comparators lack it and use other routes.`

The four preceding substories described a complete intra-Caulobacter adaptive program: Fur-released transport relief carrying the Layer 1 fitness signal; ChvI orchestrating the response over two temporal phases; a constitutive sphingolipid pool delivered by maintained Lpt machinery substituting for the absent lipid A; and a coordinated peptidoglycan downshift with targeted Pal-Tol induction. S4 closed by raising the question this substory answers — why does this four-layer program exist in Caulobacter and nowhere else? S5 answers that with a phylogenetic fact rather than a signaling result.

Three other Gram-negative species — Acinetobacter baumannii, Neisseria meningitidis, and Moraxella catarrhalis — are known from published genetics to survive loss of lipid A biosynthesis. None of them encodes the Caulobacter rescue program. This is not a coincidence of organism selection or a gap in characterization: both halves of the Caulobacter route — the sphingolipid biosynthesis pathway and the ChvG-ChvI envelope-stress two-component system — are absent from all three comparator genomes. They cannot run the program because they lack the component parts.

This section rests on three analyses confirmed at two evidence levels. A21 and A22, both assessed as direct evidence in the plan inventory, use NCBI protein annotation to establish that spt (NCBI 7,0,0,0) and cerR (NCBI 6,0,0,0) are Caulobacter-unique, and that ChvG (NCBI 5,0,0,0) and ChvI (NCBI 6,0,0,0) are alphaproteobacterial-restricted. A23, assessed as partial, closes the explanatory loop by showing that each comparator is consistent with using an alternative route already available in its own genome — PBP1A and ld-transpeptidases in A. baumannii (Kang 2021), capsular polysaccharide in N. meningitidis (Steeghs 2001), and late acyltransferase truncation in M. catarrhalis (Gao 2008). A24, the method-robustness check, grounds the absence claims: PaperBLAST returns 0 hits for LpxA, LpxC, LpxD, and LpxK in Caulobacter and only 1 for LpxB, while NCBI returns 11 to 18 hits per gene — approximately an 80% false-negative rate for Caulobacter's own known lipid A biosynthesis genes. That rate establishes that PaperBLAST alone cannot anchor a biological absence claim; the comparator absences rest on NCBI-confirmed results from NB06b. One caveat stands from the outset: the route-to-mechanism link for each comparator is inferred from published genetics in separate experimental systems, not measured in this project. The first content slide explains the method.

## position 1 — methods_summary — `NCBI re-test validates comparator absences; PaperBLAST false-negative rate ~80% in Caulobacter`

The method for this layer is comparative genomics applied to a four-species panel — a different approach from the transcriptomics and fitness analysis used for Layers 1 through 4. The core question is presence or absence of the gene families that define the Caulobacter rescue route across four phylogenetically diverse Gram-negatives: Caulobacter crescentus (Alphaproteobacteria), A. baumannii (Gammaproteobacteria), N. meningitidis (Betaproteobacteria), and M. catarrhalis (Gammaproteobacteria). None of the three comparators is an alphaproteobacterium — the phylogenetic class where both the sphingolipid biosynthesis pathway and ChvG-ChvI signaling are concentrated.

The initial screen (NB06) used PaperBLAST via the BERDL kescience_paperblast collection, querying by description-text matching and reporting hit counts as C/A/N/M (Caulobacter/A. baumannii/N. meningitidis/M. catarrhalis) throughout this substory. PaperBLAST is fast and literature-linked, but it retrieves hits based on published vocabulary: if a gene's literature uses non-canonical naming, the method misses it. NB06 generated the original presence/absence matrix that motivated the post-review robustness check.

The critical contribution of NB06b is the NCBI annotation re-test. The same focal gene families were queried against NCBI curated protein annotations via Biopython Entrez — GenBank records rather than text-mining. The robustness diagnostic applied this to Caulobacter's own lipid A biosynthesis genes, genes that are demonstrably present because LpxC is the gene deleted in the Δlpxc rescue strain. PaperBLAST returns 0 hits for LpxA in Caulobacter; NCBI returns 11. LpxC: PaperBLAST 0, NCBI 15. LpxD: PaperBLAST 0, NCBI 15. LpxB: PaperBLAST 1, NCBI 18. LpxK: PaperBLAST 0, NCBI 18. Four of the five genes are complete false negatives in PaperBLAST and the fifth is an under-count by a factor of 18, yielding the approximately 80% false-negative rate reported in REPORT.md §NB06b and NB06b cell 3.

The implication for every absence claim in this substory is direct: PaperBLAST absence alone cannot be trusted. Comparator absences in this substory rest on NCBI annotation, where the method correctly identifies the full set of known-present genes for Caulobacter. A separate concern applies specifically to M. catarrhalis: PaperBLAST indexes only 162 total genes for that organism, making its presence/absence figures advisory regardless of the general false-negative rate. NCBI annotation provides the load-bearing evidence for the comparative arm of this throughline. The next slide shows the NCBI-confirmed results for spt, cerR, ChvG, and ChvI across all four species.

## position 2 — data_figure — `Sphingolipid pathway and ChvG-ChvI absent in all three comparators (NCBI-confirmed)`

The comparative heatmap from NB06 visualizes the PaperBLAST presence/absence screen across the four-species panel. Hold in mind that the authoritative figures come from NB06b cell 3 — the NCBI annotation re-test — which is the source for every biological claim on this slide.

For the sphingolipid biosynthesis cluster, NCBI returns spt at 7, 0, 0, 0 and cerR at 6, 0, 0, 0 for Caulobacter, A. baumannii, N. meningitidis, and M. catarrhalis respectively — verbatim from REPORT.md §Finding 6 and the hypothesis scorecard. Note the important correction the NCBI re-test reveals: PaperBLAST reported cerR as 1, 1, 0, 0, giving A. baumannii a spurious hit that suggested partial sphingolipid pathway presence in that organism. NCBI corrects that to 0 in all three comparators, consistent with the published absence of the ceramide biosynthesis pathway outside Caulobacter and related Alphaproteobacteria. Serine palmitoyltransferase encoded by spt is the committed first step of sphingolipid biosynthesis; ceramide reductase cerR acts downstream. Both are absent across all three comparators by NCBI annotation. Independent confirmation comes from Olea-Ozuna 2021, which characterized the five-gene ceramide biosynthesis cluster required for Caulobacter survival and outer membrane integrity, and Olea-Ozuna 2024, which extended the cluster to eleven sphingolipid genes required for membrane stability and polymyxin B sensitivity. Neither paper found these genes outside the Caulobacter lineage.

For ChvG-ChvI, NCBI returns ChvG at 5, 0, 0, 0 and ChvI at 6, 0, 0, 0. A. baumannii and M. catarrhalis are Gammaproteobacteria; N. meningitidis is a Betaproteobacterium. Greenwich 2023 reviewed the ChvG-ChvI regulatory network across the Alphaproteobacteria and confirmed it as a conserved alphaproteobacterial circuit with no identified homologs in other bacterial lineages. NCBI annotation, phylogenetic class assignment, and the published circuit review all converge on the same result.

A partial-evidence caveat marks the interpretive step that follows from these absence data: the A23 analysis — connecting each comparator's gene-content absence to its specific published tolerance mechanism — is assessed as partial in the plan inventory because the gene-presence-to-mechanism links come from published genetics in separate experimental systems, not from measurements in this project. The NCBI-confirmed absence data (A21 for the sphingolipid pathway, A22 for ChvG-ChvI — both direct evidence) establish the structural unavailability claim. What each comparator uses instead is addressed on the next slide.

## position 3 — two_column_compare — `Structural unavailability explains species specificity — each comparator uses its own available route`

The two-column layout maps the argument onto its two evidential layers. The left column — structural unavailability — rests on the NCBI-confirmed absences from A21 and A22: spt 7, 0, 0, 0; cerR 6, 0, 0, 0; ChvG 5, 0, 0, 0; ChvI 6, 0, 0, 0. The right column — comparator alternative routes — rests on published genetics from three separate experimental systems, which is why A23 carries partial-evidence status. The argument is not that the comparators tried and failed — it is that they physically lack the parts.

For A. baumannii, Kang 2021 showed that colistin-resistant LOS-null isolates require loss of function in PBP1A combined with ld-transpeptidase activity from LdtJ and LdtK — a peptidoglycan-remodeling route that does not involve Fur derepression or sphingolipid substitution. A. baumannii encodes PBP1A and Ldt family enzymes; it does not encode spt or ChvI. The mechanism is established by Kang 2021's direct genetic experiments in A. baumannii — not measured in this project.

For N. meningitidis, Steeghs 2001 established that viable lpxA-null mutants require capsular polysaccharide. The NB06 PaperBLAST screen found approximately 9 capsule-biosynthesis hits for N. meningitidis (versus 2-3 in the other species), consistent with a large locus that N. meningitidis encodes and Caulobacter does not. Caulobacter's surface is the crystalline RsaA S-layer protein — not a polysaccharide capsule — so capsule substitution is structurally absent from Caulobacter's own genome.

For M. catarrhalis, Gao 2008 identified the late acyltransferases lpxX and lpxL, whose deletion yields viable mutants with truncated lipid A acyl-chain content and no Fur involvement. The NB06 screen found the lpxX/lpxL family present in A. baumannii (8 hits) and N. meningitidis (3 hits) but absent in Caulobacter (0 hits). The sharpest caveat applies here: PaperBLAST indexes only 162 total genes for M. catarrhalis, making its presence/absence figures advisory; the Gao 2008 mechanism is independently established in the published experimental literature.

The A23 partial-evidence designation covers the entire right column: gene-presence-to-mechanism links come from published genetics in separate experimental systems, not from this project. The left column's NCBI-confirmed absences (A21, A22 — direct evidence) are the load-bearing foundation. Together, A21 through A24 show that structural unavailability — not chance — explains why Caulobacter alone tolerates the Δlpxc deletion while three other lipid-A-loss-tolerant Gram-negatives use entirely different solutions. Five adaptive layers across S1 through S5 now compose a complete mechanistic account. S6 integrates them into the throughline's unified claim.
